# Scope & Cut Ruthlessly

> Shipping beats perfection. The fastest way to deliver value is to cut mercilessly from your V1.

## What It Does

This skill helps teams escape scope creep by identifying the core problem, distinguishing essential features from nice-to-haves, and making ruthless cuts to accelerate delivery. It reframes scope reduction as strategy—not compromise—by ensuring you cut scope, never quality. If your team has been debating features for days or feels paralyzed by too many options, this skill forces clarity and enables you to ship your minimal viable version in half the time.

## Who It's For

- **Product Managers**: Drowning in feature requests from customers and executives. Need a framework to say "not in V1" with confidence and move deferred items to a post-launch roadmap.

- **Engineering Leads**: Your timeline is compressed but scope is expanding. Use this to have a data-driven conversation about what actually ships versus what gets deferred.

- **Design Leaders**: Building something new but your design backlog has 40 ideas. Cut to the cupcake—the smallest version that proves the concept—and add layers post-launch.

- **Startup Founders**: Resources are finite. Must ship fast to prove-out an idea and raise capital. This skill prevents gold-plating V1 and forces you to test the actual problem before over-engineering.

- **Operations/Program Managers**: Managing timelines across teams. Use this skill to surface what's truly critical path versus what's nice-to-have, then resequence work by risk.

- **Executive Sponsors**: Overseeing a major product launch and concerned about timeline slip. This skill helps your teams cut decisively and communicate what trade-offs they're making.

## Use Cases

**Case 1: Sales Dashboard Redesign**
Your team wants to redesign the dashboard with 8 major features. You have 6 weeks. Run this skill → Cut to 3 essential features (deal status, filtering, email sync) → Ship in 4 weeks with room for QA → Add forecast and collaboration post-launch as your Phase 2.

**Case 2: Mobile App Launch**
Everyone agrees you need a mobile app, but defining the MVP is a nightmare (full feature parity? read-only? lite version?). This skill forces you to name the core problem (reps need to check status on-the-go), identify the minimum (read-only deal view + notifications), and defer editing, advanced filters, and analytics → faster launch, real user feedback.

**Case 3: Enterprise Feature Requested**
Your largest customer asks for multi-user collaboration on deals. You could spend 8 weeks building it perfectly, or cut it from V1 (ship in 4 weeks, gather feedback, then build it smarter in Phase 2). This skill gives you the framework to defer without feeling like you're saying no.

**Case 4: Feature Creep Mid-Project**
Two weeks into your timeline, the team proposes adding reporting, dark mode, and bulk actions. This skill reframes the conversation: "What's our core problem?" If these don't solve it → post-launch → scope holds → timeline holds.

**Case 5: Unclear Core Value**
Your product briefs mention 15 different use cases. Run this exercise to force specificity: "We're solving for sales reps who spend 4 hours/week on status checking, not for forecasters or deal collaboration yet" → Cuts scope in half → Clarifies messaging.

**Case 6: Cross-Team Alignment on Cuts**
Design, Engineering, and Product disagree on what's essential. This skill uses a structured process (not just opinions) to tier features by impact on core problem → faster consensus → fewer re-negotiations.

## Connectors

Works standalone with any product backlog or brainstorm list. But richer with:

- **Linear**: Pulls your current sprint and backlog to see what's scoped, estimated effort, and customer requests attached to issues. Helps identify what's actually in V1 versus just aspirational.

- **Notion**: Accesses your product roadmap and prioritization framework. Ensures your cuts align with strategic decisions made months ago, preventing re-litigation.

- **Slack**: Captures team discussions about scope and shipping decisions. If the team debated a feature in Slack, this provides context for whether it's truly core or a tangent.

- **Google Drive**: Reaches spreadsheets, docs, or decks where you've tracked feature ideas or customer requests. Useful if your backlog lives in a Google Sheet instead of a tool.

## Setup

Start with a list of everything currently scoped (features, epics, requirements). No special data needed—the skill walks through a structured cutting process. If you're using Linear, Notion, or Slack, connecting these gives richer context (customer requests, roadmap alignment, team sentiment), but the core exercise works with just a raw feature list.

## What You Get

- **Core problem statement**: One sentence that becomes your north star and kills ambiguity about what you're building
- **Ruthless tier analysis**: Essential features (solves core), Helpful (makes it better), Nice (polish)—with honest rationale for each
- **Reduced scope with timeline impact**: See exactly how many days you save by cutting (often 40-60% of original estimates)
- **Quality floor**: Non-negotiable standards (no data loss, zero crashes) that prevent cutting from destroying user trust
- **Post-V1 roadmap**: Everything you deferred gets a phase and a date—team sees it's strategy, not deletion
- **Work sequence by risk**: Identifies what to build first (riskiest/most uncertain) to catch problems early, not in week 5

## Key Insight

Most teams don't fail because they built something bad—they fail because they tried to build something too big. The teams that ship fast aren't smarter or more resourced; they cut mercilessly from V1. This skill is the difference between a 12-week death march and a 4-week sprint that ships the core value and proves the concept. Your V1 should be embarrassingly simple. That's the point.
