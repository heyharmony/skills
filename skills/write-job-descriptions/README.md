# Write Job Descriptions

> Job descriptions are made up. Focus on progress, not task lists.

## What It Does

Craft job descriptions that attract right-fit talent by leading with 12-month success vision and outcome-driven requirements. Move beyond task checklists and credential shopping lists to define where candidates spike, frame requirements from candidate perspective, and use intentionally polarizing language to filter for working-style fit. Includes templates, examples, and iteration framework to improve descriptions based on applicant quality. Treats job descriptions as recruiting leverage, not checkbox compliance.

## Who It's For

- **Hiring managers & founders** — creating new job postings that attract candidates who'll actually thrive in your role
- **People & HR leaders** — standardizing job description quality across your company and tracking which descriptions attract strong candidates
- **Startup leaders** — hiring early team members without cookie-cutter descriptions that don't convey your culture
- **Technical leaders** — writing engineering job descriptions that attract systems thinkers, not just resume-matchers
- **Growth-stage teams** — scaling hiring while keeping descriptions specific and compelling, not generic
- **Chronic vacancy roles** — job descriptions have run months without good applicants; time to rewrite from scratch

## Use Cases

**Starting from scratch for new role** → Define 12-month success vision first, then work backward to competencies → description focuses on outcome, not arbitrary tasks

**Existing role with weak applicant pool** → Rewrite to emphasize what makes the role different, polarize for working-style fit → attract candidates who want your specific role, not just "a job"

**Multiple similar openings with inconsistent hiring** → Standardize descriptions and track which language attracts strong hires → iterate templates based on post-hire performance

**Competitive talent market** → Use polarizing language to stand out and filter for fit → "We move fast, you need to be comfortable with ambiguity" attracts builders, repels control-seekers

**Domain-switching candidates** → Make requirements outcome-based, not credential-based → engineer from fintech can apply if they've built reliable systems, even if not fintech experience

**Clarifying role scope before hiring** → Writing the description forces clarity on what success looks like → catch scope creep and undefined expectations before interviewing

## Setup

1. **Define 12-month success vision** — What specifically changed? What outcome did they unlock?
2. **Identify core spike** — What one thing must they be exceptional at?
3. **Decide on polarizing language** — How do you filter for your working style?
4. **Set logistics clearly** — Compensation range, location, timeline, interview process
5. **Share draft with hiring team** — Get input on whether vision matches reality
6. **Write for candidates, not for job boards** — Your description should speak to what matters to people, not ATS keywords
7. **Iterate based on applicants** — Track which language attracts strong candidates vs. tire-kickers

## Key Insights

**Task lists repel good candidates.** World-class people don't apply for "maintain systems and debug code." They apply for "design infrastructure that handles 100M users." Frame outcomes, not tasks.

**Polarizing language is a feature.** Saying "we move fast, change priorities weekly" filters for people who thrive in ambiguity and repels people who need stability. That's exactly what you want.

**Credentials are shortcuts that lie.** "5 years of experience" tells you nothing. Someone with 2 years of deep learning beats someone with 5 years of cruising. Define outcomes; let candidates prove they can deliver.

**Iterate based on who applies.** If your description attracts no-shows or culture mismatches, rewrite. The applicant pool is feedback; use it.

**Compensation transparency filters smartly.** Posting salary range means lower-paid candidates self-select out early. That's a feature. You want people who'll stay, not negotiate aggressively post-hire.
