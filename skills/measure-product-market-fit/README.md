# Measure Product-Market Fit

> Product-market fit isn't binary. It's a spectrum. Measure where you are so you can make decisions with confidence instead of hope.

## What It Does

Product-market fit is when your product resonates deeply with real customers. But it's not a light switch you flip. You have PMF with some segments and not others. You can have PMF in one month and lose it when competition arrives. This skill teaches you how to measure PMF on a spectrum using retention curves, customer satisfaction surveys, segmentation analysis, and behavioral signals. Rather than waiting six months to know if you have PMF, you identify leading indicators that predict stickiness early. You segment your users to understand where you have real traction and where you're fooling yourself. The output is clarity: Do we have PMF? With which segments? Are we improving or declining? Should we scale, pivot, or keep iterating?

## Who It's For

- **Founder deciding whether to raise capital** — Investors ask about PMF. Before you pitch, know the answer. Show retention curves and "very disappointed" data. Pain: pitching without evidence of customer demand.

- **VP Product considering a pivot** — Your current strategy isn't working. Before you pivot, measure: Do you have PMF with any segment? Are you just reaching the wrong customers? Data decides. Pain: pivoting when you should double down, or doubling down when you should pivot.

- **CEO deciding whether to hire a sales team** — Huge investment. If you don't have PMF, hiring sales burns money. If you have PMF, sales accelerates growth. Measure first. Pain: scaling without product-market fit, burning cash on sales.

- **Growth lead optimizing acquisition** — You're acquiring customers fast but they churn fast too. Measure PMF separately for each acquisition channel. Maybe organic users have PMF and paid don't. Pain: growth metrics that hide poor unit economics.

- **Product Manager in a scaling company** — Your retention declined last quarter. Did you lose PMF? Or is it a cohort mix issue? Measure to diagnose. Pain: false signals that make you doubt good strategy.

- **Head of Operations evaluating business health** — You track MRR and customer count, but those are vanity metrics if churn is high. Measure PMF to assess real business health. Pain: growth that masks underlying customer dissatisfaction.

## Use Cases

- **You're raising a Series A and need to prove PMF** — You have 100 paying customers and $10k MRR. Strong start, but is this sustainable? Show retention curves, segment analysis, and "very disappointed" data. Arrow: investors see that you have PMF with one segment (enterprise), should focus there, which improves retention from 35% to 55%.

- **Your product is confusing new users and retention is weak** — You have great features but only 15% of users activate. Use leading indicator analysis to identify: users who complete the onboarding flow and invite one collaborator have 70% Day 30 retention. Users who don't have 5%. Arrow: invest in onboarding, activation improves to 35%, retention improves to 40%.

- **You're growing fast but cash is running out** — You're signing up 500 customers per month but burning cash because CAC is too high relative to LTV. Measure PMF by cohort to find: your organic customers have 3x LTV. Your paid customers have 1.2x CAC. Arrow: shift to organic/referral growth, CAC drops, unit economics improve.

- **You launched in a new market and it's not working** — You have PMF in North America. You launched in Europe and signups are anemic. Measure: PMF in US is strong, but in Europe, retention is half as good. Arrow: not a product problem, an execution problem. Invest in local go-to-market.

- **You want to know if your redesign actually helped** — You redesigned the onboarding. Before/after: old cohorts had 18% Day 30 retention, new cohorts have 24%. That's a 33% improvement. Arrow: redesign worked, double down on it.

- **Churn is rising and you're not sure why** — Measure cohort retention: newer cohorts are churning faster than older ones. That suggests product decline or you're acquiring wrong customer type. Arrow: investigate what changed in product or acquisition to reverse the trend.

## Connectors

Works standalone, but richer with **Google Analytics** (behavioral data and cohort retention), **Salesforce** (customer health scoring and churn reasons), **Gmail** (customer feedback and satisfaction signals), and **Linear or Jira** (feature request demand as leading indicator of segment maturity).

## Setup

Start by defining what "activated" means for your product (first meaningful action). Track activation rate and retention curves for cohorts over the past 6 months. Run a 10-question PMF survey on your power users asking "How would you feel if this went away?" and "What would you use it for?" Segment your users by company size, use case, or product tier and measure PMF separately for each. The time investment is 2-3 days for first measurement, then 4-6 hours monthly to update. Create a monthly dashboard to track PMF trends and share findings with leadership. The most critical measurement is retention curves — they reveal the true story of whether customers love your product or just tolerate it. Don't skip this step. Everything else flows from understanding retention.

## What You Get

- **Retention curves showing the true pattern** — See which cohorts stick and which churn, revealing whether product quality is improving or declining

- **Clear PMF score by segment** — Know exactly which customer types have PMF and which don't, enabling focused strategy

- **Leading indicators for faster measurement** — Instead of waiting 30 days for retention data, identify early behaviors that predict stickiness

- **Organic and referral growth trends** — See whether word-of-mouth is growing (strong PMF signal) or shrinking (PMF erosion)

- **Churn diagnosis** — Know why customers leave, which reveals what you're missing

- **Quarterly PMF health dashboard** — Monitor whether you're improving or declining, catch PMF erosion before it's too late

- **Data to guide decisions** — Should you scale? Pivot? Double down? Data answers the question instead of opinion.

## Key Insight

The difference between a successful startup and a failed one often comes down to the quality of PMF measurement and action. If you measure PMF clearly and discover you don't have it yet, you can invest to fix it. If you measure and discover you have strong PMF with one segment but not others, you can focus and scale. The mistake is not measuring (and thus scaling prematurely) or measuring poorly (and missing clear signals). Retention curves and segmentation never lie. People stick with products they love and leave products they don't. Measure that stickiness, understand who's sticking and why, and build your strategy from there. The companies that grow fastest are those that measure PMF rigorously and focus ruthlessly on their highest-PMF segments.

Remember: PMF is not the end. It's the beginning. Once you have PMF, you can scale. But without clarity on PMF, you're scaling uncertainty. Get this right first, then everything else becomes easier.
