# Navigate Career Transitions

> Progress matters more than prestige. Know what you're optimizing for, then move toward it.

## What It Does

This skill helps you evaluate and plan career moves using data, intuition, and deliberate experiments rather than fear or external pressure. Instead of treating transitions as all-or-nothing decisions, you'll clarify what "progress" actually means to you, diagnose whether you need to move (or need to think differently), identify decision criteria that matter, run experiments to test hypotheses before committing, and make moves that feel lucky, not desperate. You'll also learn to leverage relationships and build optionality so good moves find you.

## Who It's For

- **People feeling plateaued** who've mastered their current role and need new challenge to keep growing
- **Career changers** considering a switch in role, function, or industry for the first time
- **High performers between jobs** trying to decide between multiple offers or directions
- **People who feel "unlucky"** in their role and wondering if the problem is the role or something else
- **Managers considering IC or IC considering management** who want to test before committing
- **Sabbatical seekers** who want to take time off intentionally and plan how to use it
- **People in stable roles who sense they're not on the right trajectory** but aren't sure what to change
- **People after layoffs** who want to reset and think clearly instead of jumping to the first offer
- **Long-tenure employees** considering whether to stay at a company that's changing or move

## Use Cases

- **You got a job offer and you're trying to decide between 3 different paths** → Use decision criteria framework to compare them
- **You're thinking about leaving but you're not sure if it's the job or you** → Root cause diagnosis helps you figure it out
- **You want to switch from IC to manager (or vice versa)** → Run an experiment to test before you commit
- **You feel like you're coasting and not learning** → Diagnose what type of role/company would fix it
- **You got laid off and you're reconsidering what you actually want** → Genie framework helps you reset
- **You're being recruited to an exciting role but it's riskier than your current job** → Evaluate using your criteria, not fear
- **You want a sabbatical but don't know how to plan for it** → Create a learning agenda so the time compounds
- **Your company changed (acquisition, restructure, new leadership)** → Decide whether to adapt or move on
- **You're stuck between comfort and growth** → Clarity on what risk is actually worth taking

## Setup

No setup required. The skill works with reflection and conversation. If you connect data sources, it can help you track career patterns and decisions over time.

When Harmony conversation data exists — 1:1 recordings, mentorship calls, skip-levels — the skill surfaces recurring themes about what energizes you, where you feel stuck, and what others see as your strengths. These patterns often reveal your real career direction better than any spreadsheet analysis.

## What You Get

- Clarity on what "progress" actually means to you (not just title or money; what drives you)
- Root cause diagnosis (is it the role, the team, the company, or you? Which are fixable?)
- A decision criteria rubric you'll apply to any opportunity (weighted by what matters to you)
- An "unlucky signal" detector (when to know it's time to move, when to stay)
- An experimentation framework (test hypotheses before committing; how to gather real data)
- A relationship and serendipity strategy (how moves find you, how to build optionality)
- A transition timeline with 6-month planning (skills to build, visibility to establish, relationships to strengthen)
- Edge case handling (for risk, constraints, market conditions, competing priorities)

## Key Insight

Most career transitions fail because people treat them as binary decisions — stay or go. The best transitions are experiments. Before committing, run small tests: take on a project in the new domain, shadow someone doing the role, or do a short consulting engagement. Treat your career like a product — form hypotheses, test them cheaply, and let data guide the big moves.
