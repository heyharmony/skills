## Purpose

Partnerships only work when both sides get clear, measurable value. Most partnerships fail because of benign neglect—you signed an agreement, then nobody does anything. This skill teaches you to:

- **Build partnerships with clear, measurable value** — not vague "let's explore opportunities" agreements
- **Start with integrations** — technology integrations are the easiest entry point; formalize relationships after proving mutual benefit
- **Structure revenue models that work** — percentage of referred revenue, flat fee per lead, or co-sell commission; keep it simple
- **Assign dedicated resources** — partnerships don't manage themselves; assign one person from each side
- **Use executive sponsorship** — both sides need CEO or VP-level alignment to prevent deals from stalling
- **Measure partnership health** — track pipeline sourced, revenue influenced, integration adoption, NPS; what you measure, you improve
- **Start with pilots** — don't sign a 3-year agreement; run a 6-month pilot first, prove value, then expand
- **Identify partner fit before pitching** — know what they need from you before you approach them

Partnerships are 80% relationship and 20% process. Get the relationship right; process will follow.

## When to Use This Skill

- **You're past $1M ARR and growth is slowing** — direct sales alone won't scale; partnerships accelerate growth
- **You've built sticky product with high retention** — integration partners want to embed with products customers love
- **You have an ideal customer that uses a complementary tool** — that tool's vendor is a potential partner
- **You've identified a channel or partner that could reach your ICP** — e.g., consultants, system integrators, or salesforce automation platforms
- **You're building a platform and want ecosystem adoption** — partners building on top of you increase your value
- **You want to expand to a new geography or use case** — local partners often move faster than direct expansion
- **Your growth is stalling despite good product** — partnerships unlock new customer segments and revenue streams

## Instructions

1. **Define partnership types and value for your business**
   - **Technology integration partnerships** (easiest to start)
     - Example: You integrate with Salesforce, Slack, or HubSpot; their customers can now use your product alongside theirs
     - Value for you: New customer acquisition channel; you reach their user base
     - Value for them: Product gets richer ecosystem; customers have more reasons to stay
     - Revenue model: Free (traffic-based) or revenue-share if integration drives subscription adoption

   - **Channel partnerships** (requires sales help from partner)
     - Example: System integrators, resellers, or referral partners sell your product to their customers
     - Value for you: Sales expansion without hiring; partners bring existing relationships
     - Value for them: New revenue stream (they take commission or margin on resale)
     - Revenue model: 20-30% margin on deals they close; or 10-15% referral fee for leads

   - **Co-marketing partnerships** (builds awareness jointly)
     - Example: Joint webinar, co-branded content, shared event sponsorship
     - Value for you: Access to their audience; shared brand credibility
     - Value for them: Same (shared audience, credibility)
     - Revenue model: Usually none (both benefit from brand lift); sometimes tie-in to sales (co-sell commission if pipeline converts)

   - **Strategic alliances** (go-to-market together)
     - Example: Two complementary products jointly selling to customers who need both
     - Value for you: Bundled solution is more compelling than standalone product
     - Value for them: Same (your product makes theirs more valuable)
     - Revenue model: Revenue-share based on deal size; sometimes fixed co-sell commission rate

   - **Platform/OEM partnerships** (your product becomes part of their offering)
     - Example: You're embedded in Salesforce; customers can use you within Salesforce UI
     - Value for you: Massive distribution; deep integration with leader platform
     - Value for them: Ecosystem becomes deeper; more reasons for customers to stay
     - Revenue model: Revenue-share (% of your revenue from their platform) or flat minimum; sometimes exclusivity clause

2. **Start with integrations, not agreements**
   - Build the integration first (pre-built API connector, data sync, UI embed)
   - Don't ask for permission; just build it
   - Once users are adopting the integration, approach the partner about formalizing relationship
   - Formal relationship = joint marketing, revenue-sharing, roadmap alignment, executive sponsorship
   - Why this works: You prove value with users; partner sees adoption and benefits; much easier conversation
   - Example: Build Slack integration → 100 Slack teams using it → Approach Slack about partnership → Slack features you in their app store

3. **Identify the right partners and their incentives**
   - **Who is your partner's customer?** (Should overlap with yours)
   - **What does your partner want?** (More stickiness, richer ecosystem, revenue share?)
   - **What can you offer that's unique?** (If 10 other companies offer similar integration, why partner with you?)
   - **Is there mutual benefit?** (If only you benefit, it's not a partnership; it's an ask)
   - **Does the partnership scale?** (Will it be one integration or many? Will it drive recurring revenue?)
   - Red flags: Partner wants you to do all the work, partner has no clear motivation, overlap with your customer base is 5% or less

4. **Structure the revenue model simply**
   - **Percentage of referred revenue** (cleanest model)
     - Example: "15% of all revenue we generate from your referrals goes to you"
     - Pro: Aligned incentives; you both care about deal size and frequency
     - Con: Requires tracking which deals came from partner (can be fuzzy)

   - **Flat fee per lead** (easier accounting)
     - Example: "You give us 100 qualified leads/month; we pay you $500 per lead"
     - Pro: Predictable; you know your CAC
     - Con: Doesn't reward quality; leads might be garbage

   - **Co-sell commission** (works for complex sales)
     - Example: "We close a deal together; partner gets 5% of deal value"
     - Pro: Aligned on closing deals
     - Con: Only works if partner has sales ability; requires deep integration

   - **Revenue-share tiered by volume** (rewards scaling)
     - Example: "0-$100K revenue/year: 10% to partner | $100K-$500K: 15% | $500K+: 20%"
     - Pro: Motivates partner to scale with you
     - Con: More complex; requires clear measurement

   - **Rule of thumb:** Partner makes 10-20% of deal value (your margin still 70-80%+); enough to motivate them, not enough to cannibalize your margin

5. **Assign dedicated resources from both sides**
   - **Your side:** Partner Manager (full-time if 5+ active partnerships; part-time if 1-2)
     - Responsibilities: Integration roadmap, lead quality, deal collaboration, quarterly business reviews, troubleshooting issues
   - **Partner's side:** Dedicated contact (usually account manager or business development person)
     - Make sure it's not "you report to some guy who's also managing 20 other integrations"
     - Good partner: Has quarterly goals for your partnership; measured on your success
     - Bad partner: Nice to work with but has no mandate to push your product
   - **Monthly sync:** 30-minute call to review pipeline, remove blockers, discuss upcoming deals
   - **Quarterly business review:** Both leadership (CEO/VP level) on the call; discuss strategic direction, roadmap alignment, performance review

6. **Use executive sponsorship to prevent stalls**
   - **Your side:** Usually CEO or VP of Sales/Revenue
     - Role: Remove blockers, commit to roadmap items, make strategic decisions fast
   - **Partner's side:** Equivalent level (CEO, VP Product, VP Partnerships)
     - Role: Same; prevent mid-level bureaucracy from killing partnership
   - **Why this matters:** Without exec sponsorship, deals stall when: (1) integration has a bug and nobody fixes it (2) partner sales team is unmotivated and CEO doesn't know (3) roadmap alignment slips and people argue about priorities
   - **Cadence:** Quarterly exec sponsor alignment call (30 min); covers strategic direction, budget allocation, any blockers
   - **Decision-making:** Escalation path is clear (if operational team is stuck, escalate to execs to decide)

7. **Measure partnership health relentlessly**
   - **Pipeline influenced by partner:**
     - # of deals sourced from partner (direct referral)
     - $ value of sourced deals
     - Close rate of partner-sourced deals (if low, integration might be weak)

   - **Revenue influenced by partner:**
     - Year-to-date revenue from partner-sourced customers
     - NRR of partner-sourced cohort (are they churning? if yes, partner quality is bad)

   - **Product adoption metrics:**
     - # of their customers using your integration
     - Activation rate (% of their customers who try your product)
     - Stickiness (% of activated customers still using after 30 days / 90 days)

   - **Relationship health:**
     - NPS from partner (do they recommend you to others?)
     - Escalations (how many times did you fight about terms or roadmap?)
     - Exec engagement (do they still show up for reviews?)

   - **Annual partner ROI:** If partnership generated $X revenue and cost you $Y resources, ROI = (X - Y) / Y
     - Rule of thumb: Partnership should ROI positive by year 2

8. **Start with a pilot agreement before long-term commitment**
   - **Pilot terms:**
     - Duration: 6 months (enough time to see signal, not enough to be stuck long-term)
     - Scope: Limited (e.g., "US-only" or "tech companies only")
     - Revenue share: Same as full agreement (commit to the economics now)
     - Exclusivity: None (both sides keep options open)
     - Exit clause: Either party can exit with 30 days notice

   - **Success criteria for pilot:**
     - $[Amount] in sourced revenue OR [#] customers acquired
     - [%] adoption rate among their customer base
     - [Rating] NPS score from partner (assess relationship health)

   - **After pilot (if successful):**
     - Move to 2-year agreement (long enough to build trust, not forever)
     - Expand scope (remove geographic or segment limits)
     - Consider exclusivity (if both parties are all-in, exclusivity can be mutual benefit)

   - **After pilot (if unsuccessful):**
     - End gracefully; no hard feelings
     - Document lessons learned (integration wasn't sticky, partner didn't deliver leads, revenue share model didn't work)
     - Stay friendly; revisit in 12-18 months when conditions might be better

9. **Build compelling co-marketing to drive adoption**
   - **Joint webinar:** Partner hosts; you present your integration; they host their audience
     - Effort from you: 30 min presentation + 1 hour prep
     - Effort from them: Email to customer list + webinar logistics
     - Outcome: 50-200 registrants; hopefully 5-10% convert to trials

   - **Co-branded case study:** Joint customer (using both your products) agrees to tell story
     - Feature: How they use both products together; business outcomes from combined solution
     - Distribution: Both sides promote to their audiences
     - Outcome: Social proof; NPS boost; used in sales conversations with prospects

   - **Partner program page:** Feature their partner on your website; they feature you on theirs
     - Outcome: Long-tail SEO traffic; brand association

   - **Joint go-to-market plan:** Shared territory or customer segment; both sides drive strategy
     - Outcome: Coordinated marketing; combined sales effort

10. **Know when to end partnerships (gracefully)**
    - Reasons to end: Partner isn't delivering sourced leads, your products are less complementary after roadmap changes, economics don't work (partner eats margin, no real revenue return)
    - How to end: 60-day written notice, kind conversation with their leadership, document what worked/didn't, leave door open for future (markets change)
    - Recovery: Don't burn bridges; you might want to rekindle partnership in 3 years when conditions improve

## Edge Cases

1. **Partner integrates with competitor instead**
   - How it breaks: You're building integration with them; they launch integration with your competitor first (faster, better funded)
   - Prevention: Move fast; lock in partner with letter of intent early; communicate regularly
   - Recovery: Don't be bitter; stay in their ecosystem; eventually your integration will be superior; market will reward quality

2. **Partnership looks good but partner is unmotivated internally**
   - How it breaks: Partner CEO loves you; partner sales team ignores you; no deals are sourced; you're waiting for nothing
   - Prevention: Vet the partner's internal champion before signing; make sure they have budget + KPIs tied to partnership
   - Recovery: Call out the issue; ask for dedicated resources; if they can't provide, consider ending partnership

3. **You integrate with partner; they change their product strategy**
   - How it breaks: You invest 3 months in Salesforce integration; Salesforce decides to compete with you (happens!)
   - Prevention: Legal agreement that prevents them from competing; though with big platforms this is hard
   - Recovery: They're allowed to compete; your job is to be better; many partners will integrate with competitors; be the best in your space

4. **Revenue-share model creates accounting mess**
   - How it breaks: You close a deal with Partner A; customer came from warm intro (which partner do they get credit?) disputes emerge
   - Prevention: Define sourcing rules upfront (does "sourced from" mean they made the first intro? Did they close it? Or influenced it?)
   - Recovery: Implement CRM tracking for partner attribution; quarterly reconciliation calls; clear documentation

5. **Partnership scaling slower than direct sales**
   - How it breaks: You invested in partnership; direct sales is still 80% of revenue; partnership only 20%; dilemma: double down or kill?
   - Prevention: Set realistic expectations; partnerships take time; don't compare 3-month partnership ROI to 3-year direct sales ROI
   - Recovery: Partnerships are long-term investments; give them 12-18 months before deciding to kill

6. **Partner's revenue recognition kills your commission accounting**
   - How it breaks: Partner says they closed deal; you say customer came from your demo; who gets credit?
   - Prevention: Document sourcing clearly (who made first contact? Who closed it? Whose logo did customer recognize?)
   - Recovery: Implement partner portal where partner logs leads they're sourcing; CRM automatically tracks
