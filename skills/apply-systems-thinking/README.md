# Apply Systems Thinking

> See the whole system, not just the pieces. Trace second and third-order effects before you decide. Find the leverage point where small changes create outsized impact.

## What It Does

Systems thinking teaches you to see interconnected wholes instead of isolated pieces. When you optimize one variable without understanding the system it belongs to, you create unintended consequences. This skill maps all the players involved in a situation, their incentives, how they interact, what feedback loops reinforce or dampen behavior, and where small changes create disproportionate impact. It prevents you from solving a symptom instead of the root cause, and helps you make decisions that stick rather than create new problems downstream. You'll learn to distinguish between reinforcing loops (that build momentum) and balancing loops (that resist change), and recognize that many recurring problems persist because the system is working exactly as designed—just not for your goals.

## Who It's For

- **Leaders making strategic decisions**: Pricing changes, organizational restructuring, and go-to-market shifts ripple across your entire system. Map them first.
- **Product managers building two-sided marketplaces**: Supply and demand have complex feedback loops. Understanding them is the difference between scaling and collapsing.
- **Engineers designing systems and processes**: Well-designed systems align incentives; poorly designed ones create unintended behavior.
- **Operations and finance teams**: You see the numbers, but do you see why the numbers move the way they do?
- **Anyone solving recurring problems**: If the same problem keeps happening despite solutions, you're treating the symptom, not the cause. Map the system.
- **Change management and organizational design leaders**: You need to understand how new systems will interact with existing incentives before rolling them out
- **Data analysts and researchers** trying to understand causation instead of just correlation

## Use Cases

- **Marketplace is growing supply but not demand** → Map the feedback loops between shippers and carriers, identify that carriers multi-platform because they lack load visibility, create exclusive program to fix incentives
- **Sales team is hitting numbers but customer satisfaction is dropping** → Map shipper incentives (speed), sales incentives (deals closed), customer success incentives (cost), uncover misalignment
- **Market is consolidating and you're losing margin** → Trace second and third-order effects of competitor price cuts, recognize that price competition is a balancing loop eroding margins industry-wide
- **Organizational restructuring fails repeatedly** → Map reporting lines, incentives, information flow, identify that the new structure creates silos you didn't predict
- **Feature improves but adoption doesn't follow** → Map shipper behavior loops—maybe better matching doesn't matter if shippers still use 5 competitors simultaneously
- **You implemented a policy change but behavior didn't change** → Map the system to see why: the incentives still reward the old behavior, so the policy just annoyed people

## Connectors

Works standalone, but richer with:
- **Google Docs**: Draw diagrams of players, incentives, and feedback loops. Document assumptions about second and third-order effects
- **Notion**: Store system analyses alongside decision logs. Track whether your predictions about effects actually happened
- **Slack**: Share diagrams with cross-functional teams. Gather perspectives from people closer to different parts of the system
- **Linear**: Link system insights to product decisions, feature prioritization, and organizational changes
- **Looker or analytics tools**: Visualize actual feedback loops in data (e.g., how demand affects supply which affects price which affects demand again)

## Setup

1. Define the system: what are you analyzing? (a feature, a market, a process, an org structure). Be clear on the boundaries.
2. List all players: who has a stake? What does each player want? Include people you might not have considered.
3. Map stocks and flows: what accumulates? What moves between states? (inventory, people, money, information).
4. Identify feedback loops: what reinforces? What dampens? Draw them. Reinforcing loops accelerate change; balancing loops resist it.
5. Trace second and third-order effects: if we do X, what happens next? Then what? Then what? Most leaders only think one step ahead.
6. Find the highest-leverage intervention point: where does a small change create the biggest cascade? This is where to focus effort.

## What You Get

- **Clarity on root causes, not symptoms**: Stop chasing problems that recur because you're treating the symptom. You'll understand why the problem exists.
- **Better decisions with fewer unintended consequences**: You see second and third-order effects before they surprise you. Your plans are more robust.
- **Identification of high-leverage intervention points**: Stop trying to force change. Find where the system is ready to move. Your efforts compound.
- **Stronger cross-functional alignment**: When everyone sees the same system map, disagreements become conversations about facts, not opinions. Teams debate data, not politics.
- **Predictive power**: You can forecast what happens if competitors respond, if regulations change, if supply shifts. You're less reactive.
- **Immunity to naive solutions**: You'll recognize when a change might solve the immediate problem but create a bigger one downstream. You avoid policy disasters.
- **A visual system map**: A diagram you can update and reference as you learn more about how the system actually behaves. This becomes a team asset

## Key Insight

Most recurring problems aren't failures of effort—they're consequences of system design. The system is doing exactly what you built it to do. If you want different outcomes, you need to redesign incentives, not just ask people to work harder. Look for the leverage point where a small change to how people are incentivized creates a cascade that shifts everything.

## Pro Tips

- Draw the system map. Boxes and arrows. Label incentives and flows. Seeing it visually reveals patterns that thinking about it misses.
- Ask "what's the incentive?" when behavior doesn't match your expectations. There's always an incentive driving it. Find it.
- When a policy fails, don't blame people. Audit the system. What unintended incentive is the system creating that's making people behave differently than you expected?
- The best leverage points are often counterintuitive. Small changes to information flow or incentive structure often create bigger cascades than big changes to resources or effort.
