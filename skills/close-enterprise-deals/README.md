# Close Enterprise Deals

> Navigate multi-stakeholder selling with buying committee maps, value quantification, and procurement mastery — turn long cycles into predictable revenue.

## What It Does

This skill breaks down enterprise sales into repeatable processes that work at every stage and deal size, from land to expansion to renewal. You'll map the buying committee (economic buyer, technical buyer, user buyer, coach, influencers) before pitching, quantify ROI in the customer's specific language, and treat procurement as the actual sale—not an obstacle to overcome. It guides you through managing complex timelines, multi-threading relationships strategically, and land-and-expand strategy so deals don't stall at legal reviews or security questionnaires. Each lesson includes templates, examples, and real objection responses you can apply immediately to your current pipeline. Most importantly, you'll learn to predict where deals will stall and unblock them before momentum dies.

## Who It's For

- **VP Sales & Sales Directors** — Managing large deals ($100K+), frustrated by long cycles and unexpected objections
- **Enterprise Account Executives** — Need structured frameworks for navigating buying committees instead of guessing who to call
- **Sales Operations Managers** — Want to forecast enterprise opportunities accurately and identify stall risks early
- **Solutions Engineers** — Supporting technical evaluation and proof-of-concept success criteria
- **Partnership Managers** — Coordinating vendor evaluations across business, technical, and finance stakeholders
- **Customer Success Leaders** — Expanding existing enterprise accounts to new business units and managing renewal risks
- **Finance & RevOps** — Understanding deal dynamics and forecasting accuracy for large pipeline items

## Use Cases

- **Mapping a new enterprise prospect** → Identify all stakeholders (economic buyer, technical buyer, user buyer, coach), understand their priorities, prevent dead-end relationships with non-decision-makers early
- **Stalled deal at procurement stage** → Security questionnaires and legal reviews drag on; use this skill to shepherd them through and assign clear DRIs with accountability
- **POC that isn't closing the deal** → Set success criteria upfront with the technical buyer, use the pilot to expand relationships beyond your initial champion, transition to contract negotiation
- **Revenue disparity in pipeline** → Some deals close in 3 months, others in 12; understand where you're losing momentum and fix the blockers (stakeholder alignment, value consensus, risk mitigation)
- **Renewal at risk due to internal changes** → Your champion left; use multi-threading to lean on tier-2 relationships and executive sponsor alignment to prevent churns
- **Expanding a customer to new departments** → Use land-and-expand plan to identify expansion triggers and build the business case for growth with the new department's economic buyer
- **Accelerating a stalled negotiation** → Legal says no, finance says the budget isn't there; use your executive sponsor to escalate and find creative solutions

## Setup

Load this skill whenever you're managing a deal over $100K with more than one approval layer. Start by mapping all stakeholders (roles, priorities, concerns). Define your executive sponsor clearly (not a nice-to-have, essential). Use the output template to map your deal, update it weekly, and share with your executive sponsor to stay aligned. Track procurement timelines separately from negotiation; these run in parallel but require different stakeholder management.

## What You Get

- **Buying Committee Map** — Never waste time with the wrong stakeholder again; identify who actually decides, their priorities, and how to influence them
- **Value Quantification Framework** — Show ROI tied to their KPIs, not generic features; makes discounting unnecessary and speeds negotiation
- **Procurement Playbook** — Security questionnaires, legal reviews, compliance—treat them as the main event, not a surprise; clear ownership and timelines
- **Multi-Threading Guide** — Build relationships across the organization; one person leaving doesn't kill the deal; tier your relationships strategically
- **Land-and-Expand Plan** — Identify secondary departments and use cases; expand contracts systematically post-signature with cross-sell timing
- **Risk Assessment & Recovery** — Know which deals are at risk before they stall; have backup plans for champion turnover and budget cuts midstream
- **Executive Sponsorship Alignment** — Structure your champion relationship so they're incentivized to push internally and help unblock legal/procurement delays

## First Steps

1. Map your current enterprise deals: For each deal over $100K, list all known stakeholders and their titles. Identify gaps (who's missing?).
2. Identify your economic buyer and technical buyer for the next deal before the first sales call, not after.
3. Document one procurement timeline end-to-end; identify the actual DRIs and which review gets reviewed twice.
4. Find your champion (who benefits most from this deal?) and your executive sponsor (who outranks their economic buyer and will push internally).
5. Share this deal map with your sponsor weekly; if they're not willing to review it with you, find a new sponsor.

## Key Insight

Enterprise sales isn't unpredictable—it's just multi-stakeholder. The deals that close fastest are those where you've mapped the buying committee upfront, quantified value in their language, and treated procurement as the sale, not an afterthought. Your executive sponsor isn't a nice-to-have; they're your counterweight to their economic buyer and your insurance against mid-level stalls when champions leave.

The second insight: procurement delays aren't obstacles—they're opportunities. Every security questionnaire and legal review is a chance to embed your value deeper and expand your stakeholder relationships. Reps who view procurement as a roadblock lose deals. Reps who own the procurement process close deals because they're building consensus systematically.

Finally, land-and-expand isn't a nice-to-have post-sale strategy. It starts during contract negotiation. Identify secondary departments with similar pain points before you sign. Include them in your go-live planning. Your first contract should be structured so expansion is natural, not political. If you can close one enterprise deal systematically, you can scale that process. Document it, teach it, and repeat it.
