# Build a Sales Team

> Scale beyond founder-led sales by hiring a player-coach, building enablement, and creating culture where competitive behavior drives growth without destroying collaboration.

This skill guides you through hiring and ramping your first sales team without losing the founder's domain expertise or repeatable motion. You'll structure hiring in pairs (for credibility and comparison), build compensation plans that drive the right behaviors without creating toxicity, document sales enablement thoroughly before hiring so new reps have a playbook to follow, and implement weekly pipeline reviews so you catch stalls early before they become culture problems. It covers everything from ideal first hire profile (player-coach, not VP) to ramp expectations (monthly milestones, not annual surprises) to culture building (competitive but fair). Includes templates and real examples you can adapt immediately. Most importantly, you'll avoid the trap of hiring too fast and losing the process you spent months building.

## Use Cases

- **Hiring the first sales rep** → Define ideal player-coach profile, build comp plan, onboard with playbook instead of figuring it out together; structured interview process
- **Structuring a sales team** → Decide whether you need full-cycle AEs or SDR+AE split based on deal size and sales cycle; territory design prevents conflicts
- **Building sales enablement** → Document playbook, case studies, battle cards, and call recordings so reps don't have to reinvent discovery; invest before hiring
- **Setting ramp expectations** → Month-by-month targets keep both you and the rep aligned on success (not surprised at month 6); predictable progression
- **Implementing pipeline reviews** → Catch deal stalls and activity issues early; don't discover problems at performance review; weekly rhythm prevents surprises
- **Creating sales culture** → Competitive but collaborative; reward individual achievement and team success simultaneously; celebrate wins and handle losses with transparency
- **Managing compensation** → Design plans that drive right behaviors (land, expand, retain) without creating toxicity; keep it simple and mathematically fair
- **Identifying hiring mistakes early** → 30-60-day checkpoints catch bad fits before 6 months; coachability matters more than perfect fit on day one

## Setup

Before hiring, document your sales playbook (ICP, discovery framework, objections, demo flow, closing criteria). Build your compensation plan with legal review to finalize clawback and non-compete language. Define your first hire profile (player-coach with 60/40 selling-to-building split, not a VP title). Use structured interviews to assess sales ability + coachability + culture fit; have multiple interviewers score candidates. Set month-by-month ramp expectations (activity, meetings, pipeline, deals). Implement weekly pipeline reviews from day one; no surprises at performance review.

## First Steps

1. Document your sales playbook before hiring: ICP, discovery questions, common objections + your responses, demo flow, closing criteria.
2. Define your first hire profile: player-coach (60% selling, 40% process building), not a VP, not a solo hire.
3. Build your comp plan: research market rates, decide base/commission split (50/50 standard for AE), calculate quota, define tiers, get legal review.
4. Hire in pairs: source two candidates simultaneously so you can compare execution and results; one person is not enough data.
5. Before day one: build ramp checklists (month 1, 2, 3, 4 expectations), implement weekly pipeline reviews, get exec sponsor alignment.

## Key Insight

Most teams fail at building sales because they hire a VP too early (before they have a repeatable process) or they hire one person and can't evaluate performance (single data point). The right approach is to hire a player-coach who can sell 60% and build process 40%, not a VP title looking for a big team and big budget.

The second insight: hire in pairs. Not because you have budget, but because you can't evaluate one person. With two reps hitting the same market, you can compare their execution and learn what's the reps versus what's the process. One rep's failure is ambiguous. Two reps' failures are data.

Third: weekly pipeline reviews catch problems in week 2, not month 12. You don't need perfect data, just consistency. Are they making enough calls? Is the pipeline healthy? Are there stalls? Culture issues emerge in pipeline reviews faster than in 1-on-1s. Sales teams scale when you have clear expectations, transparent comp, and strong playbooks built before you hire. Without those, you're just hiring people and hoping they figure it out.
