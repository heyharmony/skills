# Design Surveys

> A well-designed survey takes 1 minute and reveals a month's worth of insights. A poorly designed survey takes 10 minutes and reveals nothing.

## What It Does

Surveys are powerful when designed well and dangerous when designed poorly. This skill structures surveys to maximize response rates, minimize bias, and pair quantitative metrics with qualitative context. It maps the right questions to ask, the right people to ask, and the right moment to ask them. The output is a clear survey that generates actionable insights instead of noise.

## Who It's For

- **Product Managers**: Want to validate assumptions before investing engineering time. Pain point: Gut feelings differ across team; need data to align.

- **Growth/Customer Success Teams**: Need to understand churn reasons and satisfaction drivers. Pain point: Hearing anecdotes but not knowing if they're representative.

- **Founders**: Assessing product-market fit and understanding what matters most to customers. Pain point: Can't tell if feedback reflects real trends or vocal minorities.

- **Operations Teams**: Measuring customer satisfaction and health. Pain point: Running NPS surveys but not acting on results or understanding why scores change.

- **Data Analysts**: Designing measurement frameworks and customer insight dashboards. Pain point: Surveys are launched without clear intent; results pile up unused.

## Use Cases

- **Measure product-market fit** → Use the Sean Ellis PMF question to understand if users would be very disappointed if product disappeared

- **Understand your churn reasons** → Post-churn survey asking churned users which problems drove them to leave; segment by user type

- **Validate feature concept before building** → Survey target users on whether they'd use new feature; how much they'd pay for it; which variant they prefer

- **Find surprising patterns in feedback** → Build hypothesis from support tickets; survey all users to quantify and segment

- **Align team on priorities** → Show team: 60% of power users want feature A, 45% of free users want feature B; visibility drives alignment

- **Measure NPS with context** → Don't just score; ask why they gave that score, what would improve it, and what they'd miss most

## Connectors

Works standalone, but richer with:

- **Google Forms / Typeform**: Distribute surveys and collect responses at scale with tracking

- **HubSpot**: Segment customers by lifecycle stage and survey at the right moment (post-purchase, after churn, at renewal)

- **Slack**: Analyze support conversations and feedback mentions to identify themes for survey validation

- **Intercom**: Deploy targeted in-app surveys to specific user segments; measure engagement with survey

## Setup

No setup required. Define the core insight you need, write 3-5 questions, run bias check, and launch. If you have access to customer segments or lifecycle data (HubSpot, Mixpanel), you can target the right audience at the right moment. If not, you can survey through email or in-app link.

## What You Get

- Research question clarity: the 1-3 core questions whose answers will change your roadmap

- Survey structure: quantitative + qualitative question pairs that provide both data and context

- Bias audit: check that your questions are neutral and won't skew responses

- Respondent targeting: definition of who to survey, what moment to ask them, expected response rate

- Segmentation strategy: how to divide results to find hidden patterns by persona, tenure, or other factors

- Post-survey analysis plan: how you'll actually interpret results and what decision follows

- Success metrics: response rate targets, completion rate, and how you'll know the survey was well-designed

- 1-week execution timeline: from finalization to analysis to shared results

## Key Insight

Most surveys fail because they ask the wrong people the wrong questions at the wrong time, or they get answers but don't act on them. A well-designed survey respects respondent time (takes 2-3 minutes), asks clear questions in neutral language, and pairs every rating with a "why" to add context. The best surveys are triggered by moments—post-purchase, post-support, end-of-trial—when users have strong opinions. And they're followed by action: if 60% say feature X matters, you build it. If <40% show product-market fit, you fix the core value prop. Survey only if you're prepared to act on results.
