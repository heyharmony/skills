## Purpose

Media relations is about building relationships before you need them. Journalists get 200+ pitches per week; most are ignored. The ones that get coverage are stories, not product announcements. A story has trend, data, conflict, or insight. A product announcement is "we built this thing." Nobody cares. Your job is to find the narrative your product is part of and pitch that to journalists who cover that beat, not spam the entire media list.

The best media relations strategy starts 12 months before you need coverage. You build relationships with journalists covering your space, you understand what they write about and care about, you offer them insights and access, and you become a trusted source. When you need coverage, you call them and they answer. When you don't offer anything good to pitch, you don't call them. That's the difference between a PR person journalists know and a spammer they delete.

Earned media (journalism that writes about you) is worth 3-5x paid media because it's credible. But it's unpredictable and you can't control the narrative. You can influence it by pitching good stories, not bad ones.

## When to Use This Skill

- You're launching a product and want coverage beyond "company launches product" (need to pitch the trend, not the product)
- You've hired a new C-level executive or made a major organizational change and want to announce it
- Your company is part of a broader industry trend and you want to position as a leader
- A journalist is writing a story about your space and you want to be quoted/featured
- You need to develop a relationship with journalists who cover your market
- You're preparing your CEO for media interviews and need to identify key talking points and prepare for tough questions
- A crisis is emerging (negative story, customer complaint, product issue) and you need to respond quickly
- You want to build a competitive advantage through thought leadership (speaking engagements, bylines, analyst relations)
- You're uncertain whether to issue a press release or pitch a story instead

## Instructions

1. **Build a journalist database by beat and understand what each reporter actually cares about**
   - Identify 3-5 publications that cover your space: industry publications, trade press, mainstream tech press, vertical-specific media
   - For each publication, identify 2-3 journalists by beat: product launches (who covers new launches), trends (who writes trend pieces), company news (who covers personnel/funding), your industry vertical (healthcare, fintech, etc.)
   - Research each journalist: read 5-10 recent articles they've written. What themes appear? What sources do they use? What angle do they take?
   - Document in a spreadsheet: journalist name, publication, email, beat/focus area, 3-5 recent articles they've written, their apparent editorial angle
   - Note: don't just grab "tech journalists." Target ones who specifically cover your market. A HR tech founder should have journalists who cover HR tech, not just "tech journalists."
   - This database is your biggest asset. Update it quarterly (journalists change jobs, new ones emerge, some stop covering your space).

2. **Understand what stories journalists actually want and what they don't want**
   - They want: Trends with data, insights, conflict, industry analysis, competitive angles, impact on broader economy
   - They don't want: Product announcements unless they're genuinely novel, "we hired someone new," "we got funding," congratulations-type news
   - They get thousands of pitches saying "company launches feature that will save time." They never read them.
   - They read pitches saying "employees are 40% more productive when..." (trend + data) or "Here's how companies are solving X problem and why existing solutions fail" (insight + conflict)
   - Your job is to translate your product news into a story angle that a journalist actually cares about.
   - Example: Don't pitch "we built a workflow automation platform." Pitch "knowledge workers are drowning in 240+ SaaS apps and wasting 30% of their time switching between tools—here's why this is a $500B productivity crisis and how it's being solved."

3. **Identify the narrative your product is part of and pitch the story, not the product**
   - Ask: What trend is my product riding? Is it adoption of AI? Shift to remote work? The crisis of employee burnout? The need for real-time data?
   - Ask: What's the broader industry story? Are there 5 other companies solving similar problems? That's a trend worth covering.
   - Ask: What's the customer pain point? Is it an unsolved problem affecting millions? That's a story.
   - Write your story angle as a headline a journalist might write, not as a product announcement
     - Bad: "Harmony Launches Conversation Intelligence Skill to Solve Workplace Liquidity"
     - Good: "Why Two-Sided Marketplaces Are Still Failing—and What It Takes to Fix Them"
   - Your product is supporting evidence for the bigger story, not the story itself.
   - Pitch the trend to the journalist; your product is proof that the trend exists and people are solving it.

4. **Reach out to journalists with genuine relationships, not blast emails**
   - Personalized email (not BCC blast): "Hi [Journalist], I read your recent article on [topic]. I noticed [specific insight]. We're seeing something similar at our company—here's the data. Would this interest you for a follow-up piece?"
   - Start with value, not ask: Share something interesting (data, insight, customer story) before asking if they'd cover it
   - Reference their recent work: "Your [recent article] was great because..." shows you read them, not that you blasted 100 people with the same email
   - Offer exclusivity: Give one journalist an exclusive on a story for 48 hours, then expand to others. Journalists value exclusives and will prioritize them.
   - Follow-up: If they don't respond in 48 hours, follow up once. If no response after that, move on. Don't spam.
   - Relationship building: Even if they don't cover your story right now, you've planted a seed. They know your company exists and that you have insights to share. Next time they're writing in your space, they might call.

5. **Prepare your executives for media interviews: key messages, tough questions, and practice**
   - Media training: If your CEO will do interviews, spend 2 hours on media training covering: (1) key messages (what 2-3 things do you always want to say?), (2) staying on message (how to redirect tough questions back to your points), (3) avoiding quotable mistakes (what should you never say?), (4) practice with a mock journalist asking tough questions
   - Key messages: Distill your story to 2-3 things you always want to convey. When a journalist asks you anything, you answer their question but tie it back to your key message.
     - Example message: "We believe the future of work is asynchronous and insights-driven. That's why we built Harmony—to give teams real-time visibility into what's happening in their conversations."
     - Bad answer to "How do you differ from competitors?": "We have better UX and more integrations."
     - Good answer: "We focus on insights, not transcripts. Our key message is that work is moving async, and that requires a fundamentally different approach to understanding what's happening."
   - Tough questions preparation: Ask your team "what would a critical journalist ask us?" and prepare answers. "Aren't you just spying on employees?" "Your pricing is high—who can afford you?" "This seems like a feature, not a product." Rehearse these before interviews.
   - Quotable language: Help your CEO develop 5-10 memorable quotes they can use in interviews. Journalists love pull quotes, and a great quote makes a story.

6. **Develop a crisis communication playbook: speed, transparency, action**
   - Define what constitutes a crisis: negative story about to break, customer complaint going viral, security incident, product failure impacting customers, executive misconduct accusation
   - First 48 hours: The most critical time. Speed matters more than perfection.
     - Hour 1: Convene leadership, understand facts, decide on response tone (transparent, apologetic, action-oriented)
     - Hour 2-4: Draft initial statement: "We're aware of [issue]. Here's what happened. Here's what we're doing. Here's the timeline for resolution."
     - Hour 4+: Issue statement to press, post on social, email to customers. Don't hide.
   - Response tone: Take responsibility, show empathy, explain what you're doing to fix it, provide timeline
     - Bad: "This is a misunderstanding" or "We don't comment on speculation"
     - Good: "We made a mistake. Here's what we're doing to fix it. Here's how we're preventing it in the future."
   - After the storm: Follow-up communication (weekly updates on fix progress), long-term reputation repair (thought leadership on the issue, industry leadership to solve the underlying problem)
   - Never say: "No comment," "That's private," "I can't talk about that." These fuel speculation. Better: "We're still investigating. Here's what we know so far."

7. **Build thought leadership to become a trusted voice independent of product launches**
   - Bylines: Publish 1-2 thought leadership pieces per quarter in industry publications (not your own blog, actual publications) about trends you're seeing, insights from your data, contrarian takes
   - Speaking: Get your CEO on podcasts, panels, and conferences. Speaking slots build credibility and reach journalists who listen
   - Analyst relations: Develop relationships with Gartner, Forrester, IDC analysts covering your space. They influence press coverage and enterprise buyers.
   - Data/research: Conduct original research (customer survey, data analysis) on a trend you're part of. Journalists love publishing novel data. "We surveyed 500 customers and found 40% of them..." is pitch-worthy.
   - Comment availability: Respond quickly when journalists reach out for comment. If you're available and you provide good insight, you build a relationship and become a go-to source for future stories.
   - Long-term play: This takes 12-24 months to build credibility, but once established, journalists call you when they're writing in your space.

8. **Measure media relations by reach and quality, not volume**
   - Vanity metric (don't measure): Number of mentions, total impressions (meaningless—a mention in a low-traffic blog isn't worth the same as a mention in Forbes)
   - Good metrics:
     - Coverage in top-tier publications (Forbes, WSJ, TechCrunch, industry leaders like VentureBeat for startups, HBR for business innovation, etc.)
     - Quotes attributed to your executives (shows you have voice, not just mentions)
     - Traffic driven by articles (use UTM codes to track)
     - Inbound from media mentions (customers saying "I read about you in [publication]")
     - Analyst reports that feature you
   - Quarterly review: Which stories drove the most value? Which journalists became trusted relationships? Which pitches flopped and why?
   - Iterate: If a journalist didn't bite on your pitch, ask why (if they respond). Adjust your story angle for next time.

## Edge Cases

- **Journalists are too busy to respond**: Don't take it personally. They get 200+ pitches weekly. If your pitch doesn't get a response in 48 hours, assume they're not interested. Move to the next journalist. Persistence is fine, but don't spam.

- **You pitch a story and a competitor wins the coverage**: It happens. Lesson learned: pitch faster next time, or find a different angle. The market can handle multiple companies in one story.

- **A journalist writes a critical piece about you**: Don't respond emotionally. If the criticism is fair, acknowledge it (show you read it, you understand the concern, you're addressing it). If it's factually wrong, correct politely with facts. Build the relationship for the next story, not the one that just ran.

- **Your CEO won't do media interviews**: You can do PR without the CEO's voice, but it's less effective. Offer media training to remove the fear. If they still refuse, escalate to a CMO or technical lead instead.

- **A crisis breaks and you have no journalist relationships**: This is when relationships matter most. If you've built goodwill, journalists give you a chance to respond. If you haven't, they print first and ask questions later. Start relationship building before you need it.

- **Competing PR stories from your own team**: Sometimes your CEO, product lead, or sales team pitch different stories to different journalists. Coordinate centrally so you don't accidentally undermine your own narrative.

- **Analysts want access but you don't have time**: Analyst relations (Gartner, Forrester) are worth the time investment. It's ongoing (quarterly updates), not transactional (one pitch), but the payoff is long-term credibility and influence over enterprise buyers.
