# Manage Timelines

> Estimate in ranges, not points. The goal isn't accuracy—it's visibility.

## What It Does

This skill replaces wishful thinking with structured estimation: estimating in ranges (2-4 weeks, not 3 weeks), calculating your team's actual velocity, identifying what can't slip (the critical path), and re-estimating weekly as you learn. Most teams miss deadlines not because they're incompetent, but because they estimated with single points and ignored reality. This skill builds a planning process that catches problems early—when you can still do something about them—instead of at the last minute when panic sets in.

## Who It's For

- **Project Managers**: Own timelines but can't see why estimates are always wrong. This skill gives you data-driven forecasting and early warning signals instead of surprises.

- **Engineering Managers**: Your team sandbagged estimates, or estimates were too optimistic. You need visibility into actual velocity and weekly status so you can see problems coming.

- **Product Managers**: Making customer commitments on timelines. This skill helps you give honest forecasts with confidence levels, not false certainty that gets you in trouble later.

- **Startup Founders**: Resources are tight and runway is limited. You need accurate timelines to know if you can survive to the next milestone. Wishful thinking kills startups.

- **Operations Leaders**: Managing multiple projects. This skill helps you see which projects are on track, which are at risk, and who's blocking whom through cross-team dependencies.

- **Anyone Committing to a Deadline**: Customer, executive, or internal deadline—you need a way to forecast honestly and catch slips before they become crises.

## Use Cases

**Case 1: New Product Launch**
You have 6 weeks to ship a major feature. Use this skill → Break into 30 tasks → Estimate each in ranges → Calculate velocity → Work backwards from deadline → Realize you need 7 weeks → Cut scope or extend timeline NOW, not week 6.

**Case 2: Timeline Already Slipped**
You were supposed to launch 2 weeks ago. Use this skill to understand why: Were estimates off? Was it hidden dependencies? Team capacity? Then fix the estimation process for next time so you see slips coming.

**Case 3: Conflicting Estimates**
Product says "3 weeks," Engineering says "6 weeks." This skill forces a conversation using data and unknowns instead of gut feelings. You realize there's a technical spike nobody accounted for, so 6 weeks is honest.

**Case 4: Multiple Blockers and Dependencies**
You can't see which critical work is blocking the entire project. Map out the critical path → Realize Task A blocks 5 other tasks → Focus all resources on Task A → Rest of team doesn't sit idle → Project doesn't slip.

**Case 5: Weekly Status Is "On Track" Until It Isn't**
Last week everything looked fine. This week, the project is 2 weeks behind. This skill catches those problems early via weekly re-estimation and checkpoint tracking, giving you time to respond.

**Case 6: Leadership Asking "Are We Done Yet?"**
Instead of guessing, you have confidence levels: "50% confident we ship this week, 80% confident we ship by next week, 95% confident by week after." This is way clearer than "should be done soon."

## Connectors

Works standalone with a task list and deadline. But richer with:

- **Linear**: Tracks estimated vs actual completion time for all tasks. Shows velocity, helps you identify which types of work consistently slip (testing? integration? design?).

- **GitHub**: Shows PR merge frequency and build health. If PRs sit for days before merging, that's a bottleneck hiding in your estimates.

- **Asana**: Captures task estimates and actual time spent. Shows whether your team's estimates are optimistic or realistic.

- **Slack**: Surfaces blockers mentioned in casual conversation before they formally derail you. Early signals.

- **Google Calendar**: Identifies vacation, meetings, and other events that reduce available team capacity. Without this, estimates don't match reality.

## Setup

Start with a list of tasks (break features down to 2-5 day chunks), assign owners, and get best/worst/likely estimates from each owner. No special tools—the skill walks you through calculating velocity, working backwards from deadline, and weekly re-estimation. If you connect Linear or Google Calendar, you get automatic velocity history and capacity adjustments, but the core process works with just a spreadsheet.

## What You Get

- **Range-based timeline**: Not "we'll launch April 1" but "50% confident April 1, 80% confident April 8, 95% confident April 15." Honest about uncertainty.
- **Velocity calculation**: Shows how much work your team realistically completes per week (usually 60-70% of raw capacity due to meetings and context-switching).
- **Critical path identification**: Shows what can't slip without delaying the entire project—focus your energy there first.
- **Checkpoint milestones**: 2-week intervals with measurable outcomes; early signals if you're slipping.
- **Weekly re-estimation**: Every Wednesday, update estimates based on what you learned. Catch timeline drift before it becomes a miss.
- **Dependency mapping**: Shows what blocks what; reveals parallelization opportunities and unavoidable serialization.
- **Early warning system**: If velocity drops 20% or a critical path task is delayed, you see it coming and can adjust while there's time.

## Key Insight

The teams that hit deadlines don't have perfect estimates—they have honest estimates that they update weekly. They plan with ranges, not single points. They calculate velocity from real data, not hope. And they communicate slips early, before they become crises. Your first deadline using this skill will feel slow to plan (breaking down tasks, estimating ranges, calculating velocity). But the second timeline, you'll be 2 weeks ahead of schedule because you actually planned conservatively. The third timeline, your leadership trusts your estimates because you've been honest twice. That trust is worth more than hitting a deadline by luck.
