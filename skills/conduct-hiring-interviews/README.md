# Conduct Hiring Interviews

> Turn 45 minutes of interview into 45 minutes of real insight. Structure beats performance.

## What It Does

Structure behavioral interviews to surface genuine capability beneath polished delivery. Combines persistent technical questioning with story-based assessment to understand how candidates think, learn from failure, and approach unfamiliar problems. Provides rubric, question frameworks, and scoring templates to calibrate hiring team decisions before bias and group dynamics enter the room. Uses the PEARL framework (Problem, Epiphany, Action, Result, Learning) to dig six levels deep into candidate responses instead of accepting surface-level stories.

## Who It's For

- **Hiring Managers** — conducting first-round interviews who want to ask beyond surface-level questions and score consistently across candidates
- **Technical Leaders** — assessing candidates for roles where deep expertise matters and shallow answers hide knowledge gaps
- **HR Partners** — coordinating multi-round interviews and ensuring interview panels stay calibrated and structured
- **Co-founders & executives** — hiring early team members where one bad hire compounds (limited team size amplifies mistakes)
- **Hiring Teams** — multiple interviewers who need to divide competency assessment and avoid duplicative questioning
- **Newly promoted managers** — first time conducting interviews and want a framework that feels natural, not scripted

## Use Cases

**Prepping for technical round with engineer candidates** — Create PEARL-based questions for domain depth, then observe their problem-solving approach on realistic scenarios → discover whether they think systematically or jump to solutions

**Calibrating a hiring panel** → Align on 4-5 actual competencies (not generic "leadership"), score independently before discussing, identify outlier assessments that reveal blind spots → build team consensus on who's truly strong

**Assessing for role requiring judgment & learning** — Ask failure-based questions and probe how they changed behavior → distinguish between candidates who've been tested and those who've skated through

**Interviewing domain switchers** — Use simulation questions to see if core problem-solving transfers, ask how they'd learn your industry, reference past learning in adjacent fields → evaluate adaptability beyond domain expertise

**Speed-dating final round interviews** → Ensure you're not just selling the role; create a working simulation in 45 minutes where you observe their thinking → avoid hiring based on charm or cultural similarity

**Building psychological safety in hiring** — Ask about failure first (normalize it), acknowledge what you don't know as an interviewer, welcome questions about working here → candidates show their authentic selves instead of performing

## Setup

1. **Define your competencies** — What 4-5 things actually predict success in this role? (Not "communication" but "communicates ambiguity to skeptics.")
2. **Create behavioral anchor questions** — 2-3 PEARL prompts per competency
3. **Share rubric with co-interviewers** — 24 hours before interviews
4. **Divide competency assessment** — Each interviewer owns 1-2 competencies to avoid overlap
5. **Set debrief timing** — Schedule 15-minute post-interview team sync before candidate leaves
6. **Prepare working simulation** — Have a realistic problem candidates can think through in real time
7. **Train interviewers** — Ensure team knows how to probe, when to interrupt polish, what listening looks like

## Key Insights

**Prep reveals priority.** How candidates prepare for the interview tells you more than the interview itself. Someone who researched your company, thought about problems you face, and prepared thoughtful questions signals respect and rigor.

**Failure stories matter most.** Everyone talks about wins. Candidates who can articulate failure, what they learned, and how they changed behavior have been tested. The failure story is where authenticity lives.

**Simulation beats talking.** You learn more watching someone approach a real problem than hearing them talk about past problems. Their questions, assumptions, and iteration style appear in real time.

**One strong "no" carries weight.** When one interviewer is skeptical and others are enthusiastic, listen to the skeptic. They may have caught something others missed because they had different chemistry or probed differently.
