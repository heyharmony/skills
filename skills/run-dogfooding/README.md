# Run Dogfooding Programs

> The fastest way to find bugs and usability issues — because the best quality testers are the people who actually use your product.

## What It Does

If you won't use your product, why should customers? This skill teaches you how to run a structured dogfooding program where your team regularly uses the product in real scenarios, catches bugs before customers do, and provides actionable feedback on usability and performance. Rather than relying on QA departments or hoping developers test their own work, you create a deliberate system where product, design, engineering, sales, and support all experience the product as users would. The key is making dogfooding systematic, not ad-hoc — with clear scenarios, standardized feedback capture, and a fast track from finding issues to shipping fixes.

## Who It's For

- **Product Manager** — You're launching a major feature and want to catch obvious UX confusions before your 1,000+ customers encounter them. Pain: shipping features that feel smooth in design but confusing in practice.

- **Engineering Lead** — You want your team to find bugs before customers do. Dogfooding gives you a quality gate that catches issues in hours, not weeks. Pain: reactive bug fixes from customer complaints instead of proactive quality.

- **Design Lead** — Your wireframes look great but you're not sure if they work when users actually interact with them. Dogfooding is user testing with your own team. Pain: gap between design intent and real user behavior.

- **Customer Success Manager** — You hear customer confusion and bugs from support. Dogfooding gives you a voice in product quality before customers complain. Pain: same issues coming up repeatedly from different customers.

- **Operations or QA Lead** — You're building a quality assurance function and need structured processes for testing. Dogfooding is a scalable way to catch issues across the team. Pain: unclear testing standards, inconsistent quality signals.

- **Founder/CEO** — You want your team to have empathy for what customers experience. Dogfooding forces that empathy. Pain: team building features that work on paper but not in practice.

## Use Cases

- **You're shipping a redesigned feature and want confidence it actually works** — You've rebuilt the comment system, navigation, or dashboard. Instead of launching and waiting for support tickets, run a 2-hour dogfooding session with 8 people. You'll find and fix the top 5 issues before launch. Arrow: launch with 90% fewer bugs than comparable features.

- **New team members don't understand the product** — New PMs and designers should go through the product as new users. Dogfooding reveals what's obvious to veterans but confusing to newcomers. Arrow: faster onboarding, fresh eyes catch real UX issues.

- **You're building an admin panel or internal tool** — Features customers don't directly see still matter. Have your ops team and customer success team actually use the admin panel before shipping. Arrow: support tools that work intuitively instead of requiring documentation.

- **Your quality is declining as you ship faster** — You're doing more frequent releases and seeing more customer bugs. Dogfooding catches issues in your release cycle instead of after customers see them. Arrow: same release velocity, better quality.

- **A feature is confusing but you can't tell why** — You've shipped something and customers are confused, but developers think it's straightforward. Watch non-developers use it in dogfooding. Arrow: identify the UX gap and fix it quickly.

- **You want to scale QA without hiring QA people** — Instead of a dedicated QA department, dogfooding distributes quality responsibility across the whole team. Everyone finds bugs. Arrow: quality gates that scale with your team, not as a separate function.

## Connectors

Works standalone, but richer with **Linear or Jira** (dogfood findings surface as prioritized tickets), **Slack** (real-time feedback channel for async findings), **Google Drive** (centralized documentation of dogfooding sessions and findings), and **Zoom** (recorded sessions for pattern detection and asynchronous review).

## Setup

Start by defining whether dogfooding is pre-launch (one-time session before major features) or cultural (recurring cadence). Create 3-5 concrete scenarios that match how customers actually use your product. Schedule a concentrated session (90 minutes to 2 hours) with 8-10 people across product, design, engineering, and customer-facing roles. Use a simple feedback capture template, triage findings, and track which issues make it into fixes. Expect the first session to take 6-8 hours of total time. Set up a feedback channel (Slack or shared doc) so you can do lighter dogfooding asynchronously between major sessions. Plan for regular sessions — weekly if launching fast, monthly if more stable. The best dogfooding programs have a clear owner who runs the sessions and tracks issues to resolution. Make quality feedback visible by sharing metrics: bugs found, fixed, cycle time from finding to ship.

## What You Get

- **Bug discovery before customers** — Find and fix 80%+ of usability issues before they reach real users

- **Cross-functional quality feedback** — Sales, support, and marketing perspectives catch things product and design miss

- **Reduced support volume** — Customers encounter fewer confusing features, leading to fewer support questions

- **Faster issue resolution** — Dogfood findings that are real bugs get prioritized and fixed within days

- **Team empathy for users** — When your whole team actually uses the product, they build better intuition for how customers experience it

- **Quality trends over time** — Track whether your dogfooding findings decrease over time (sign of improving product maturity) or increase (sign of quality drift)

## Key Insight

The best quality gate is using your product as a customer would. Dogfooding catches a different set of issues than QA testing or code review — you find usability gaps, performance problems, and edge cases that feel obvious once someone using the product hits them. The key is making dogfooding regular and structured, with clear scenarios and fast feedback-to-fix cycles. When you find a bug on Tuesday dogfooding and it ships fixed on Wednesday, your team understands that quality matters. Dogfooding scales PM judgment about quality across your entire organization. Teams that dogfood consistently ship noticeably better products than those that don't.

One final note: the goal of dogfooding is not to be critical of your own work. It's to catch issues early. If dogfooding becomes a blame session or a way to delay launches, it becomes dysfunction. Keep dogfooding positive: "Here's what works really well and here's what we should improve." That mindset makes teams want to participate.
