# Evaluate Trade-Offs

> Every product decision is a trade-off. The teams that win are the ones that make trade-offs explicitly, not the ones that pretend they don't exist.

## What It Does

This skill helps you make visible what's usually invisible: the cost of every choice. You'll use decision matrices, Amazon's one-way vs. two-way door framework, and structured analysis to evaluate options without pretending you can optimize for everything. The goal isn't to find the "perfect" decision—it's to make a deliberate choice, understand what you're sacrificing, and be able to explain the reasoning to your team. You'll learn which decisions are reversible (reduce analysis paralysis) and which are permanent (demand deeper thinking). Most importantly, you'll stop the fantasy that you can "have it all"—instead, you'll make explicit what you're choosing to do and what you're choosing not to do.

## Who It's For

- **Product and engineering leaders** making roadmap decisions every quarter
- **Founders** deciding what to build and what to skip, with limited resources
- **Strategy teams** evaluating market entry, acquisition, or org restructuring
- **Teams in disagreement** who need a framework to move past opinions to principles
- **Any leader managing scarcity** (time, money, people) who must choose between competing goods
- **Finance and business leaders** allocating budgets and capital across competing initiatives
- **People new to leadership** who need a mental model for decision-making under constraints

## Use Cases

- **Quarterly planning**: You have 10 potential projects and capacity for 5. Instead of politics or loudest voices, use a trade-off framework to evaluate them against your optimization goal → decisions are defensible and explained to the whole team
- **Crisis or rapid change**: A key customer is at risk, or market shifts, and you need to pivot quickly. Make the trade-off explicit: "We're sacrificing [X] to address [Y] right now. Here's when we'll revisit." → Team understands the urgency and the cost
- **Technical debt decision**: Engineering wants 4 weeks to pay down tech debt; product wants 4 weeks for customer features. Define what you're optimizing for → one-way or two-way door analysis tells you how much analysis to invest
- **Build vs. buy**: Custom infrastructure or vendor solution? Map the trade-off: cost, control, speed, risk → you're not choosing wrong, you're choosing what to optimize for
- **Market expansion or pivot**: Enter a new market or double down on current one? Use a decision matrix and name what you're sacrificing → you avoid the trap of wanting both
- **Org structure**: Centralized or distributed teams? Specialized or cross-functional? Analyze the trade-off and communicate the impact → people understand why you restructured
- **Hiring or headcount constraints**: You can hire 5 engineers. What teams do they go to? Use trade-off analysis to allocate → you're transparent about who's under-resourced and why

## Connectors

**Works standalone**, but richer with:
- **Notion**: Document trade-off decisions, decision matrices, and reasoning for future teams
- **Slack**: Async discussion of trade-offs with leadership; surface disagreements as tradeoff analysis
- **Google Drive**: Create decision matrices and collaborative trade-off analysis with weighted criteria
- **Linear**: Link trade-off decisions to roadmap items, so teams understand what was sacrificed for what they're building

## Setup

1. Identify a decision you're facing (roadmap item, market entry, resource allocation, etc.)
2. List 2-4 realistic options (include "do nothing" as one)
3. Define what you're optimizing for this quarter/year
4. Create a simple decision matrix with criteria and weights
5. Score each option and identify the winner
6. Name what you're sacrificing with the winning choice
7. Determine if it's a one-way or two-way door
8. Document the decision and reasoning in Notion
9. Communicate to the team, addressing the disappointed parties

## What You Get

- A clear decision backed by explicit reasoning, not gut feel or politics
- A matrix showing how you weighted different criteria and why (transparent tradeoff logic)
- Documentation of what you're sacrificing (so teams understand the cost, not just the benefit)
- Clarity on one-way vs. two-way doors (so you know how much analysis to invest before deciding)
- A written record that future teams can reference when someone asks "Why did we make that choice?"
- Permission to move forward without guilt: you've made a deliberate choice, so you can commit fully
- A framework for explaining the decision to disappointed parties (honoring their perspective while being clear on priorities)

## Key Insight

The teams that move fastest are the ones that make trade-offs explicit and commit fully to them. Lingering regret over unchosen options is a form of decision debt. Name what you're sacrificing, communicate it clearly, and move forward. You can always revisit one-way doors—but not if you're still wishing you'd chosen differently.

## Pro Tips

- Use one-way door analysis as a procrastination filter. If it's reversible, decide quickly. Don't waste analysis on cheap decisions.
- Weighted scoring is a tool for thinking, not gospel. The matrix guides conversation; it doesn't replace judgment.
- Document the reasoning, not just the decision. Future you (and future teams) will thank you when they ask "Why did we choose this?"
