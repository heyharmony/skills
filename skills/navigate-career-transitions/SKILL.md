## Purpose

Career transitions feel risky because they're often framed as life-or-death decisions. Most aren't. A transition is an experiment. You're testing a hypothesis: "I'd grow faster as a manager" or "I want to work at a startup" or "I need a slower-paced environment." Good transitions are reversible; you can go back. The goal is to make bets that are educated, not reckless, and to gather information before you commit.

This skill helps you clarify what you're actually optimizing for (not money), identify your metric of progress, run experiments to test hypotheses, and make decisions based on intuition + data, not fear or external pressure.

## When to Use This Skill

- **You're between jobs and considering options** — Multiple offers? Deciding between IC and manager? This helps you decide.
- **You're thinking about leaving your company** — Clarify if it's the role, the team, the company, or you that needs to change.
- **You want to switch functions** (PM to Eng, IC to Manager, Product to Ops) — Test the new role before full commit.
- **You're considering a sabbatical or break** — Useful time if you use it deliberately; framework helps you plan.
- **You're trying to decide between startups and big companies** — Understand what habitat fits you best.
- **You've hit a plateau** — Growth slows when you master the current role. Time to find something new.
- **You're feeling "unlucky" in your role** — Might be misalignment, not capability. Transition might fix it.
- **You want to make a big pivot** (career change, geography, role level) — Plan it like a product launch, not a cliff jump.

## Instructions

**1. Identify your real metric of progress (not the surface metric)**
   - Surface metrics people often chase: salary, title, company brand, org size
   - Real metrics that determine fulfillment: learning speed, autonomy, impact visibility, people quality, pace alignment, work-life fit, mission alignment
   - Exercise: Imagine two roles—one pays $50K more but teaches you nothing; one pays $20K less but teaches you something that scales your career. Which do you take? Your answer reveals your real metric.
   - Write it down: "My metric of progress is [specific thing]." (e.g., "Learning velocity," "Autonomy," "Working with people I admire")

**2. Run the Genie Framework on your current situation**
   - Remove fear of failure: "If I couldn't fail, what would I want to do in my next role?"
   - Work backwards from 10 years: "What would 10-year-old me look back and be proud of?" Not money. Impact. Mastery. Relationships.
   - Identify the gap: "What's between here and there? What do I need to learn? What bets do I need to make?"
   - This isn't wishing. It's diagnosis. Often you realize you don't need to change jobs; you need to change how you approach your current one.

**3. Diagnose why you want to move (root cause)**
   - Is it the role? (You're bored. Want to learn something new. Role is misaligned with your strengths.)
   - Is it the team? (Manager is bad. Peers aren't pushing you. Culture is wrong.)
   - Is it the company? (Wrong stage for you. Wrong mission. Wrong pace. Finances are unstable.)
   - Is it you? (You've outgrown the level. You want to try something different. You need a break.)
   - Is it external? (Spouse moved. Need to be closer to family. Geographic preference changed.)
   - Only move if you're running toward something, not away from something. "I want to build at startups" is better than "I hate big company slowness." (Though both can be true.)

**4. Build your career decision criteria (what matters to you)**
   - List 5–8 factors that determine your success in a role:
     - Learning: What will I master? Will I grow?
     - Pace: Is it too fast (burnout) or too slow (boredom)?
     - People: Who am I working with? Is the manager good?
     - Autonomy: Can I make decisions? Do I have freedom?
     - Impact visibility: Can I see the result of my work?
     - Stability: Will the company/role be around? Can I plan?
     - Mission: Do I believe in what we're building?
     - Work-life fit: Will I have time for things outside work?
   - For each, define: "What's dealbreaker-bad? What's ideal? What's acceptable?"
   - This is your rubric. You'll apply it to any opportunity.

**5. Identify your "unlucky" signals (when to move)**
   - You feel lucky when: You're learning fast. You respect your manager. The team is good. The work matters.
   - You feel unlucky when: You're coasting. The manager doesn't understand you. The team is mediocre or toxic. The work doesn't matter.
   - Luck is real. If you don't feel it, you're either in the wrong role or the wrong company.
   - Action: If unlucky, first try to fix it (talk to manager, switch teams, reframe work). If that doesn't work, move.

**6. Create a transition experiment (test before you commit)**
   - Don't go all-in on a hypothesis. Test it first.
   - Experiment ideas:
     - Want to try management? Mentor someone or lead a project. See if you like it.
     - Want to switch functions? Do a rotation or project in the new function. See if it's for you.
     - Want to work at startups? Do a short contract or internship. Feel the pace.
     - Want to work in a different industry? Talk to 5 people working there. Ask about daily life, not just vision.
     - Want to try a sabbatical? Take a 1-month personal project. See if you like unstructured time.
   - What you learn from experiments: "This is what I thought," vs. "This is reality." Almost always different.

**7. Build serendipity through relationships**
   - The best next move rarely comes from job postings. It comes from people.
   - Relationships matter more than optimization. Know people doing great work. When a move makes sense, they'll know you.
   - Strategy: Every 6 months, grab coffee with 3–5 people doing interesting work (not looking for a job; just learning).
   - When an opportunity aligns (role opens, company is hiring, they remember you), they'll think of you.
   - This is how career serendipity works. It's not magic; it's relationships + optionality.

**8. Evaluate any specific opportunity against your criteria**
   - Role offered: [Role]
   - Your decision criteria checklist:
     - Learning potential: [Rate 1–10. Will you grow?]
     - Pace: [Sustainable or burnout risk?]
     - People/manager: [Do you respect them?]
     - Autonomy: [Can you make decisions?]
     - Impact visibility: [Can you see results?]
     - Stability: [Can you plan 2+ years?]
     - Mission: [Do you believe in it?]
     - Work-life fit: [Realistic pace?]
   - If 6+ of 8 factors are strong, take it. If more than 2 are weak, pass or negotiate.

**9. Plan internal moves before external**
   - Switching teams at your company is easier than switching companies.
   - You have credibility. They know your work. Risk is lower.
   - Before you quit, ask: "Can I pivot to a different role, team, or function here?"
   - Internal moves are underrated. You keep network, benefits, tenure. You prove you can adapt.
   - Only go external if: (a) no good internal options, or (b) you need a hard reset (culture is broken).

**10. Create a transition timeline and commitment plan**
   - Decide: When are you making this move? (6 months? 1 year? Now?)
   - If in 6 months: What do you need to learn/do in that 6 months? (Skill gaps, visibility, relationship building)
   - If now: What's your financial safety net? (3–6 months expenses saved?)
   - If sabbatical: What's the minimum break you need? (2 weeks doesn't reset; 1–3 months often does)
   - Commit to the timeline. Tell your mentor. Share it with your manager (if appropriate). Make it real.

Take 18 months off. Spend 3 months in a startup. 3 months building something myself. 3 months traveling and learning. 3 months working with a mentor on [deep expertise]. Then make a big move.

I'd be proud if I became known for [specific domain expertise]. If I'd mentored 5 people who went on to do great things. If I'd shipped something that mattered. If I'd built judgment—the ability to make good calls even with incomplete information. If I'd worked with people who pushed me.

• Expertise gap: I'm generalist PM. I want deep expertise in [specific domain].

• Peer quality: Peers are solid but not pushing me. No one challenging my thinking.
• Team culture: Good culture. Not toxic. But not a place where people are doing the best work of their lives.
• Signal: Partially. Better team would help, but not the real issue.

Is it the company?
• Stage fit: Series B at slower growth (series A was faster). I want velocity again.
• Mission alignment: Mission is fine. Not inspiring, not off-putting.
• Financial health: Stable. Funded well.
• Pace: Too slow. Decisions take 6 weeks. I want things to move in 2 weeks.
• Signal: YES. The pace is killing me. At early-stage, you move faster.

Is it you?
• Growth plateau: Yes. I've mastered Series B product management. I'm coasting.
• Restlessness: Yes. Very. Feeling like I'm not developing.
• Life changes: Family is stable. Not a driver.
• Signal: YES. I'm plateauing and I need to go somewhere that feels harder.

Primary diagnosis:
It's a combination: You've mastered this role (it's you), the company is too slow (company/stage), and you want a place where you're learning (role + people). You need to go to an earlier-stage company or a new domain where you're humbled again.

• Dealbreaker-bad: Repeating the same work I already know. Easy mode.
• Ideal: Role where I'm 10 years behind. Constantly learning. New domain + new pace.
• Acceptable: New to me (different company stage, function, or industry) even if not ideal

• Dealbreaker-bad: Manager is insecure, controlling, or non-supportive

• Acceptable: Manager is competent and fair, even if not deeply investing in my growth
• How you'll assess: Talk to their direct reports. Ask: "Do they invest in your growth? Can you tell me about feedback they've given you?"

• Dealbreaker-bad: Everything requires sign-off from multiple people
• Ideal: I can move fast on decisions. Can run experiments. Can fail small.
• Acceptable: Some oversight, especially early, but I can prove myself into autonomy
• How you'll assess: "Give me an example of a decision I'd make solo vs. with stakeholder input."

• Dealbreaker-bad: Work is indirect or delayed. Feedback loops are 6 months.

• Acceptable: Quarterly feedback loops. Can connect work to business outcomes.

## Edge Cases

- **You're afraid of taking a risk** — Reframe: Staying in the wrong role is also a risk (to your growth and your fulfillment). What's the actual cost of not moving?
- **You have golden handcuffs (unvested equity, sign-on bonus clawback)** — Calculate the real cost. Sometimes it's worth paying the penalty. Sometimes it's not. Make the math conscious.
- **You're not sure if you want to move or you're just burned out** — Take a 2-week break first. Burnout and real misalignment feel similar. If you still want to move after rest, it's real.
- **You have a family depending on you** — Risk tolerance is lower. More important to have financial cushion. Consider internal moves or sabbaticals before external moves.
- **You're early-career and worried about "not making the right choice"** — Most moves are reversible. Go where you'll learn most. You can always go back or sideways. Learning compounds; regret for not learning is worse than regret for making a "wrong" move.
- **You're late-career and feeling like time is running out** — This is real. Accelerate your timeline. Don't wait. But don't panic. Better to make intentional moves than reactive ones.
- **The market is bad or there's a recession** — You have lower leverage. Build relationships and options now. When the market recovers, you'll be ready. Or double down on internal growth if external risk is too high.
