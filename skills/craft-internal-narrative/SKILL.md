## Purpose

Leaders discuss strategy in board meetings, exec syncs, and 1:1s — but those conversations rarely get translated into a single story for the company. The result: mixed signals, rumor mills, and engagement surveys saying "I don't understand where we're heading." This skill mines leadership conversations to extract the real strategic narrative, reconcile contradictions between what different leaders are saying, and package it into all-hands talking points, a manager FAQ, and a messaging cascade so the story reaches every level consistently.

## When to Use

- **Pre-all-hands or town hall**: Leadership alignment needs to cascade to the whole company in a unified voice
- **After strategic shift or pivot**: Business model change, market repositioning, or major strategic decision that affects how teams prioritize
- **Announcing org changes**: Restructuring, layoffs, new leadership, or team mergers require careful, consistent messaging to prevent speculation
- **Quarterly company update**: Each quarter, translate board-level updates and exec priorities into employee-friendly context
- **Post-board-meeting internal translation**: Board has made a decision; employees need to understand the why and what-now in their own language
- **After M&A or major partnership**: New acquisition, integration, or partnership announcement needs coherent company-wide narrative
- **When employee surveys flag confusion**: Engagement surveys reveal employees don't understand strategy or direction — time to clarify and communicate

## Instructions

**Step 1: Mine leadership meeting transcripts**
Gather transcripts or summaries from recent leadership conversations (board meetings, exec syncs, department head meetings) from the past 2-4 weeks. Extract key themes, repeated phrases, directional statements, priorities, and trade-offs that keep coming up.

**Step 2: Identify contradictions**
Map where different leaders frame the same topic differently. Example: CEO emphasizes efficiency and profitability; Chief Revenue Officer talks about "bigger deals and fewer accounts" as a growth strategy; VP Engineering is still hiring for a product roadmap nobody has discussed limiting. Note these gaps.

**Step 3: Reconcile**
Propose a unified framing that's honest and addresses all perspectives. This is not about sanitizing the truth — it's about finding the coherent story that makes all the pieces fit. In the example above: "We're shifting to profitable growth by focusing on accounts where we can deliver deep value and build sticky, long-term relationships. That means fewer but bigger deals, and product investment focused on depth, not breadth."

**Step 4: Draft the core narrative**
Write 3-5 sentences that answer: Where are we now? What changed? Where are we going? Why does it matter? Make it memorable, honest, and repeatable by any leader in any room.

**Step 5: Build all-hands talking points**
Structure: opening context (why we're meeting), 3 key messages with evidence/wins, what's changing, what's not changing, what's next. Include speaker notes and permission for leaders to add their own examples.

**Step 6: Write manager FAQ**
Anticipate 10-15 questions employees will ask in team meetings. Provide honest answers and suggested framing. Common questions: Are there layoffs? Does this mean we stop building X? How does my job change? When will you know more?

**Step 7: Design messaging cascade**
Map who communicates what, to whom, in what order: leadership announcements first (all-hands or email), then managers brief their teams with FAQ support, then peer discussion and feedback loops. Include timing (same-day comms, no surprises).

**Step 8: Add feedback loop**
Include a mechanism for employees to ask questions, raise concerns, and close the loop. Slack channel, anonymous survey, office hours, or manager check-in — whatever fits your culture.

## Output Format

1. **Core Narrative** (3-5 sentences)
2. **All-Hands Talking Points** (opening, 3 key messages with evidence, what's changing, what's not, what's next)
3. **Manager FAQ** (10-15 Q&As with honest answers and framing suggestions)
4. **Messaging Cascade Timeline** (who, what, when, in order)
5. **Feedback Mechanism** (how employees ask questions and raise concerns)

## Example: Lumina Analytics

**Context**: Lumina is a 200-person B2B analytics company. Leadership decided to shift from growth-at-all-costs to profitable growth. CEO emphasized efficiency and unit economics in a board meeting. Chief Revenue Officer talks about "fewer, bigger deals" in a sales sync. But VP Engineering is still aggressively hiring for a product roadmap that was never discussed in the context of this pivot. Result: mixed signals. Sales team thinks we're cutting product investment. Engineering thinks growth is still the priority. Nobody knows what's actually true.

**Core Narrative**:
"Lumina is shifting to profitable growth. For three years, we chased growth at all costs — more customers, more product, more complexity. We learned a lot. Now we're doubling down on our best customers and deepest capabilities. We're going to make more money per customer, invest in the features they love most, and build a company we can sustain for the long term. That means we're being more selective about new customers, and more focused on product. It means different work for some teams. But it means Lumina stays healthy, and our jobs stay secure."

**All-Hands Talking Points**:
- *Opening*: "We've hit an inflection point. We're shifting from growth velocity to profitable growth. Here's what that means."
- *Message 1 - Why Now*: We've learned that our best customers are in mid-market — accounts where we can deliver deep value and build 10-year relationships. We've been spreading ourselves thin chasing every vertical. We're focusing now.
- *Message 2 - What's Changing*: Sales will focus on accounts where we can do our best work. Engineering will prioritize depth — the features our key customers depend on, not breadth. Finance will measure profitability and unit economics, not just growth.
- *Message 3 - What's Not Changing*: We're still hiring. Still innovating. Still building Lumina into a category leader. We're just being smarter about where we invest.
- *What's Next*: Each department will have a 90-day plan by end of month. Managers will brief their teams. We'll measure progress together.

**Manager FAQ**:
1. Q: Are there layoffs? A: Not planned. We're being more selective about new hiring and more focused on retention of our best people. If your team changes, you'll hear it from leadership first.
2. Q: Does this mean we stop building new features? A: No. We're changing how we prioritize. We're investing more in features our key customers depend on, less in experimental features that don't drive retention or expansion.
3. Q: How does my job change? A: Depends on your team. [Sales: you'll own fewer accounts but build deeper relationships. Engineering: you'll focus on our three core products, not launch new ones. Finance: you'll track unit economics as closely as growth.] Your manager will walk through specifics.
4. Q: When will you know more about changes in my team? A: By end of Q2. We're being thoughtful, not rushed.
5. Q: Why didn't leadership tell us sooner? A: Board decision was made last month. We wanted to align on narrative before we communicated. You're hearing it now because we're ready to execute.
6. Q: What if I disagree? A: Say so. Raise it with your manager, come to office hours, post in Slack. We want feedback.
7. Q: Is the company in trouble? A: No. We're healthy and well-funded. We're being proactive about unit economics, not reactive to a crisis.
8. Q: What does profitability mean? A: It means we're measuring whether each customer is profitable — and doubling down on customers where we make the most impact. It doesn't mean we're cutting costs everywhere.

**Messaging Cascade Timeline**:
- *Day 1, 9am*: CEO all-hands (live or recorded). Sales, Engineering, and Finance leaders sit on stage.
- *Day 1, 5pm*: Email recap sent to all employees with FAQ link.
- *Day 2-3*: Managers brief their teams (1-hour, using talking points and FAQ).
- *Day 3-5*: Slack channel open for Q&A. CEO or department heads respond within 24 hours.
- *Week 2*: Office hours (CEO + each department head). Optional, drop-in.
- *Week 4*: Pulse survey: "Do you understand the strategy? Do you have concerns?" Adjust messaging if needed.

**Feedback Mechanism**:
- Slack #ask-leadership channel (moderated, 24-hour response time)
- Anonymous pulse survey (weekly for 4 weeks, then monthly)
- Manager check-ins (managers trained to ask, "What questions do you have?")
- Office hours (CEO + each dept head, optional, open-ended)

## Edge Cases

1. **Sensitive narrative (layoffs, exec departure)**: Requires legal and executive review before you draft. Flag for CEO approval. Do not share FAQ publicly until approved.

2. **Narrative for remote/async teams**: Add async-friendly formats — recorded video, email with full transcript, Slack thread with Q&A. Don't assume everyone can attend live all-hands.

3. **Contradictory leadership signals that can't be reconciled**: Example: CEO wants to cut costs, COO wants to invest in team; board wants profitability, sales wants to hire aggressively. If the narrative can't bridge these, escalate to CEO. This skill can't fix broken alignment.

4. **Multiple narratives needed for different audiences**: Board narrative (metrics, unit economics) vs. company narrative (culture, mission) vs. customer narrative (product roadmap). Draft core narrative first, then adapt for each audience. Keep core story consistent.

5. **Narrative during crisis** (layoffs, failed funding round, customer loss): Speed matters more than polish. Draft core narrative in 24 hours. Communicate early and often. Add feedback loop immediately so people don't speculate. Update as facts change.
