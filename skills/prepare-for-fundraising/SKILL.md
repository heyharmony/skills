## Purpose

Fundraising is a full-time job for 3-6 months. Most founders are unprepared for the intensity: you'll speak with 50+ investors, hear 49 "nos," and negotiate with one who says "yes." The goal of this skill is not to land one term sheet; it's to get multiple term sheets so you negotiate from strength, maximize valuation, and preserve favorable terms. This requires: meticulous prep (clean cap table, auditable financials), strategic targeting (50+ investors tiered by fit), warm introductions (10x better conversion than cold), clear storytelling (your narrative matters more than your deck), and understanding what you're trading away. Fundraising is not about the money; it's about building a partnership with investors who can add value beyond capital.

## When to Use This

- You're 6-12 months from needing capital
- You have product-market fit signals (consistent growth, strong retention, positive unit economics)
- Your metrics are clean enough to tell a compelling story
- You have a committed founding team and clear vision for next 24 months
- You've decided what type of funding you want (seed, Series A, venture, angels)
- Your cap table is documented and you haven't made major equity mistakes
- You have existing relationships or warm intro paths to investors
- You're willing to commit 3-6 months part-time (or full-time for the CEO) to the process
- You've prepared financially auditable books (not just optimistic projections)

## Instructions

1. **Get Your Foundational House in Order (Months 1-2)**
   - Document your cap table: all equity holders, preferred vs. common shares, vesting schedules, option pools
   - Use a tool like Pulley, Ledger, or Carta; avoid spreadsheets (investors will ask)
   - Clean your cap table: resolve any ambiguities about founder equity, adviser equity, past employee grants
   - Create auditable financial statements: monthly revenue, CAC, LTV, churn, burn rate, runway for past 12-24 months
   - Avoid: overly optimistic projections, rounded numbers, gaps in historical data
   - Build a 3-year financial model: revenue assumptions (conservative), expense forecasts, profitability timeline
   - Document your business: why did you pick this problem, why are you the right team, what have you achieved, what's next
   - Identify 3-5 customer references: customers you can call who'll sing your praises (have these ready before investor calls)
   - Create a simple one-pager: problem, solution, market size, growth to date, funding ask, use of proceeds

2. **Build Your Investment Story and Narrative Deck**
   - Narrative: the story you tell investors (not the pitch deck). "Why this team, why this market, why now?"
   - Start with the problem: illustrate it vividly (customer quote, painful moment, market data)
   - Your unfair advantage: why are you uniquely positioned to win? (founder expertise, network, timing)
   - Market size: TAM, SAM, SOM. Conservative estimates; avoid overstating
   - Traction: key metrics showing you're building something real (revenue, customers, growth, engagement)
   - Vision: where will you be in 3-5 years? (market position, revenue, impact)
   - Fundraising ask: how much are you raising, how will you use it, what will you accomplish
   - Pitch deck (20-30 slides): visual version of your narrative, designed to leave behind, not to present from
   - Practice your pitch: 3-minute version, 10-minute version, 20-minute version
   - Get feedback from advisors and investors before you start pitching seriously

3. **Create Your Investor Target List (Months 1-3)**
   - Define your ideal investor: check size (seed, Series A?), stage preference, industry focus, geography
   - Research 100+ investors who fit your criteria
   - Create three tiers:
     - Tier 1 (your dream investors): 15-20 investors you'd love to work with; known for helping portfolio companies
     - Tier 2 (solid fits): 20-30 investors with strong track record but maybe less aligned
     - Tier 3 (backup options): 30-50 investors who fit criteria but maybe less ideal
   - For each investor, research: portfolio companies, recent investments, public theses, known preferences
   - Identify warm introduction paths: who do you know who knows them? (founder, advisor, customer, other investor)
   - Score each investor on: industry fit, stage fit, founder background fit, speed (do they move fast?), value-add (what can they contribute beyond money?)
   - Aim for at least 3-5 warm intro paths per Tier 1 investor

4. **Execute Warm Introductions and Outreach (Months 2-4)**
   - Start with Tier 1: identify 5-10 investors you want to talk to first
   - Reach out to warm intro contacts: "Can you introduce me to [investor] at [firm]? We're raising a $500K seed round and [brief reason you think they'd be interested]"
   - Include a one-pager or slide deck (let them understand your story before making the intro)
   - Accept that some intros won't happen; follow up with cold email if a warm intro stalls
   - Cold email (if necessary): personalized, specific (mention their portfolio company or thesis), brief, clear ask for 20-min call
   - Expect: 10-15% response rate on cold outreach, 50-70% on warm intros
   - Batch your outreach: aim for 5 investor meetings per week (sustainable pace)
   - Track every conversation: date, investor, notes, follow-up needed, likelihood (very interested, maybe, not a fit)

5. **Master the Investor Conversation (Months 2-5)**
   - First meetings: 20-30 minutes, focus on conversation, not pitching
   - Start by listening: ask what they're looking for in founders and companies
   - Tell your story clearly: problem, why you care, what you've built, traction, vision
   - Be ready for tough questions: "Why will this work?", "Who's your competition?", "Why you?", "What's your contingency plan?"
   - Listen to feedback, but don't over-optimize your pitch based on one investor's comments
   - End by asking: "What would we need to show you for you to get excited about investing?"
   - Follow up: send a note within 24 hours (reiterate key points, answer unanswered questions, share customer reference if helpful)
   - Track: after each meeting, note their interest level and timeline
   - Expect: many investors will need multiple meetings before deciding
   - Recognize when they're not going to invest: politely end the process, stay on good terms, ask for intros to other investors

6. **Prepare for Due Diligence Before It's Asked**
   - Investors will do due diligence; be proactive
   - Clean documents: cap table, articles of incorporation, equity documents, previous fundraising docs
   - Financial records: 24 months of P&L, bank statements, revenue contracts (customer agreements)
   - Customer references: list 5-10 customers with their contact info, willingness to speak to investors
   - Technical diligence: code quality, architecture, security practices, data privacy compliance
   - Legal: no pending litigation, clear IP ownership, no undisclosed investor conflicts
   - Prepare a "data room" (Google folder or Notion) with all documents organized by category
   - Be transparent: if there are issues (security breach, customer loss), disclose them early
   - Answer questions thoroughly but don't volunteer every possible negative; investors will ask

7. **Manage Multiple Term Sheets and Negotiate**
   - Aim for multiple term sheets: you need leverage to negotiate
   - Typical timeline: 1-3 months of meetings, then 2-4 weeks for interested investors to issue term sheets
   - When you have 2+ term sheets, you can compare and negotiate
   - Key terms to understand: valuation (what are you raising per?), liquidation preference (will you get paid back before common?), board seat, anti-dilution, drag-along rights
   - Negotiate from strength: investors respect founders who shop their round and negotiate. Don't accept first offer without thinking
   - Don't optimize only for valuation: work with investors you believe in, whose value-add is real, whose terms are founder-friendly
   - Get a lawyer: $5-10K for seed round documentation is worth it; amateur cap tables create problems later
   - Understand: what are you giving up in this round (dilution, board seat)? Is it worth it?

8. **Execute and Move Fast (Months 4-6)**
   - Once you have a lead investor and term sheet, move quickly to close
   - Keep momentum: don't let due diligence drag for 2 months
   - Document everything: amendments, side letters, any special terms
   - Have a lawyer review everything; don't try to DIY legal documents
   - Communicate transparently with your team and cap table: what's happening, timeline, how it affects them
   - Once term sheet is signed, execute closing documents quickly (1-2 weeks typical)
   - Celebrate the close, but remember: the real work starts with deploying the capital

## Example

**Company:** TimeFlow (construction scheduling SaaS)

**Scenario:** TimeFlow had 45 paying customers, $18K MRR, 92% annual retention. CEO Jack decided to raise a $500K seed round. He had 6 months of runway; fundraising was critical.

### Preparation (Months 1-2)

Jack's cap table was clean: 100% founder equity, no prior investors. He had 24 months of clean financials showing month-over-month 8-10% revenue growth. He identified 3 customer reference accounts willing to speak to investors (directors of operations at construction firms).

Jack's narrative: "Construction companies lose 100+ hours/year to scheduling inefficiency. We're helping them reclaim 10 weeks of productivity. Market is $10B+. We're growing 8% MoM. We're raising to expand sales and product."

He created a 22-slide deck, practiced his pitch with 3 advisors, and built a one-pager.

### Investor Targeting (Months 2-3)

Jack identified 75 seed-stage investors with construction or SMB software focus. He created 3 tiers:

Tier 1 (15 investors): known for hands-on construction expertise, follow founders long-term, known for quick decisions. Jack had warm intro paths to 10 of them.

Tier 2 (25 investors): solid seed investors, interested in SMB software, but less construction-specific.

Tier 3 (35 investors): broader seed investors, fit the check size but maybe less aligned.

### Execution (Months 2-5)

Jack started with Tier 1. He got intros to 8 investors and landed 6 meetings in the first month. Conversations revealed a consistent question: "Will your product still be relevant if contractors start using AI for scheduling?"

Jack adjusted his narrative to address this: "AI is a tool we'll integrate. The real value is personalized context — which crew has expertise for this job? Which site has capacity? These decisions need domain knowledge, not just optimization."

After 3 months of meetings with Tier 1 and Tier 2 investors, Jack had 5 investors saying "very interested" and 2 saying "probably, one more meeting." He had 1 investor ready to issue a term sheet.

### Term Sheets (Month 4)

Investor 1 (prominent seed fund) offered: $500K at $3M post-money valuation, 30% equity dilution, board seat.

Jack didn't rush. He accelerated conversations with the 4 other "very interested" investors. Within 2 weeks, he had 2 more term sheets:

Investor 2: $500K at $3.2M post-money (better valuation), no board seat (better for founder control).

Investor 3: $250K (smaller check) at $3.5M post-money (best valuation but half the funding).

### Negotiation & Close

Jack chose to combine Investor 1 and 2, raising $500K at $3.1M post-money average, with only 1 board seat (negotiated with Investor 1). This gave him capital from two high-quality firms and preserved founder control.

Investor 1 brought technical expertise and construction network; Investor 2 brought sales and scaling expertise.

Jack closed in 2 weeks (fast relative to industry) with clean documentation from his lawyer ($8K cost).

### Key Insights

- **Warm intros were essential:** 70% of Jack's meetings came from warm intros. Cold outreach got <5% response.
- **Multiple term sheets gave leverage:** Without Investors 2 and 3, Jack would have taken Investor 1's term. With competition, he negotiated better terms.
- **Narrative mattered more than deck:** Every investor asked about AI relevance. Jack's thoughtful answer (integrate but differentiate on context) became his strength in conversations.
- **Speed was an asset:** Closing in 2 weeks impressed investors; it signaled strong operator discipline.

## Edge Cases

1. **Great Product, Messy Cap Table:** You forgot to vest some co-founder equity, or promised equity to an adviser in a handshake deal. Before talking to investors, get a lawyer to clean this up ($3-5K). Investors will ask; a clean cap table shows discipline.

2. **Investors Want a Lower Valuation Than You Expect:** You modeled $2M post-money; investors offering $1.2M. This stings, but if you need the money, don't let pride override math. Calculate: what's your runway after this funding? Can you hit milestones to raise the next round at higher valuation? If yes, take it. If no, you may need to extend fundraising or find an alternative.

3. **Term Sheet Takes Forever to Close:** You got a term sheet 8 weeks ago. Due diligence is still happening. Your runway is shrinking. Set a close deadline: "We need to close by [date] or we'll move to other investors." Most investors respect this; a few will walk away. That's okay; you need closure more than you need their money.

4. **Lead Investor Disappears:** They said they were interested, now they're not responding. Move on. Don't wait for them. Reach out to backup investors. Most investors prefer silence to an explicit "no"; accept the signal and move forward.

5. **Customer References Go Badly:** A customer you prepped doesn't show up for the call, or is lukewarm about your product. This is a signal. Either coach your customer better or pick different references. Investors will talk to customers; make sure they're genuinely happy.

6. **You Realize You Don't Need the Money:** You're hitting milestones faster than expected, burn rate is lower, maybe you can bootstrap longer. You can slow down fundraising. But if you've started the process, it's often better to close what you can (at good terms) for future optionality. Having capital gives you choices.
