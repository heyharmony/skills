# Work Backwards from Outcome

> Define products through your customer's eyes before you build. Write the announcement first, and let the hard questions resolve themselves.

## What It Does

The working backwards method forces you to start with a customer-focused press release and internal FAQ before any design or code happens. This flips the typical development process: instead of building something and then figuring out how to explain it, you write the explanation first. This uncovers misalignment, surfaces hard questions early, and grounds the entire effort in customer reality. When you can't write a compelling press release, you don't understand the product well enough. The FAQ forces you to answer the questions that will actually come up—from investors, customers, and your own team—before you've wasted engineering cycles.

## Who It's For

- **Product managers and leaders**: You need clarity on what you're solving before allocating resources. The press release becomes your north star.
- **Design and engineering teams**: Designers can't design without knowing what problem they're solving. Engineers need to know why something matters before they build it.
- **Founders and entrepreneurs**: You must validate that customers care before you invest months of development.
- **Cross-functional teams with disagreement**: When people disagree on features or strategy, let the press release force the conversation.
- **Organizations launching new features or platforms**: Alignment happens through writing, not meetings.
- **Go-to-market leaders** positioning new products or features for launch
- **Board members and investors** evaluating whether a strategy is customer-focused or feature-focused
- **People new to a company or role** needing to quickly understand what the product actually solves

## Use Cases

- **New product launch** → You write a press release and FAQ, surface disagreement on pricing and positioning, iterate until your team agrees on what you're selling
- **Feature roadmap planning** → Instead of debating requirements, you draft the announcement and discover which features actually matter to customers
- **Evaluating acquisition targets** → Write a press release imagining the product five years from now, discover whether the acquisition actually helps you get there
- **Restructuring a business model** → A press release clarifies what changes (pricing, distribution, audience) and forces you to answer why it's better
- **Leadership alignment before investment** → The board needs to understand your strategy. A clear press release shows you've thought through the customer, problem, and solution
- **Preventing wasted engineering effort** → Before an engineering team spends 3 months building, you've already discovered the idea isn't clear enough through the press release exercise

## Connectors

Works standalone, but richer with:
- **Google Docs**: Collaborate on the press release and FAQ with stakeholders in real-time
- **Slack**: Share drafts, gather feedback, debate tradeoffs asynchronously
- **Linear**: Link the press release to your roadmap and engineering tickets so all decisions trace back to customer benefit
- **Notion**: Store press releases as templates for future launches or features
- **Zoom**: Run working backwards sessions with stakeholders to debate hard questions and surface disagreement before code is written

## Setup

1. Schedule a 90-minute working session with product, design, engineering, and leadership. Include the key stakeholders who will execute. Async doesn't work for this—you need to debate in real time.
2. Assemble a one-page template: headline, opening paragraph, customer problem, how it works, why now, about the company. Keep it short enough to read in 2 minutes.
3. Draft the internal FAQ (minimum 5-8 questions: pricing, competitors, feasibility, go-to-market, success metrics, risks, customer validation, timeline). The FAQ forces the hard conversations.
4. Share drafts asynchronously (48 hours for feedback) before the meeting. Let people read and reflect instead of reacting live.
5. Iterate the press release based on disagreements—those disagreements are insights. Don't smooth them over; explore them.

## What You Get

- **Clarity on what you're solving and for whom**: No vagueness, no wasted time on features that don't matter. You'll be able to explain the product in one sentence.
- **Surfaced disagreement early**: When your team can't agree on the press release, you've found the blindspot before engineering starts. This is the best time to resolve it.
- **A north star for all downstream decisions**: Design brief, engineering spec, marketing copy all trace back to the press release. Decisions become easier.
- **Faster execution**: When everyone knows what matters, decisions move faster and easier without constant re-approval. Engineering doesn't build until there's consensus.
- **Alignment with leadership and customers**: The press release becomes the story you tell investors, customers, and your team. Consistency builds trust.
- **Go-to-market clarity**: You know what story sells before launch, not after beta feedback reveals you positioned wrong. Your GTM launch is faster.
- **A shared reference point**: The FAQ captures assumptions and decisions so future teams understand the thinking, not just the output. This becomes institutional knowledge

## Key Insight

The work backwards method is a forcing function. It forces disagreement into the open early, when it's cheap to disagree. If you can't write a press release without a fight, imagine the fights you'll have once code is shipping. Use the press release to make consensus a prerequisite for engineering, not something you discover afterward.

## Pro Tips

- The press release should be customer-focused, not marketing-focused. Use real language, not marketing speak. If it sounds like a tagline, rewrite it.
- The FAQ is where the real work happens. Answer the hard questions: what are we NOT doing? What's the biggest risk? Why is this better than the alternative?
- Use this method for any major decision: feature, pivot, hire, acquisition. Write the "announcement" for each decision and see if your team can agree.
- Don't skip the FAQ. The FAQ is where disagreements surface. If your team agrees on the FAQ easily, your decision is either obvious or people aren't thinking critically.
