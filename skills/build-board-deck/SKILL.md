## Purpose

Board decks shouldn't be a scramble. This skill mines your executive team meetings, pipeline reviews, product updates, customer calls, and finance syncs to assemble a board-ready narrative. Instead of cobbling together last-minute slides, you get a structured 7-slide presentation where each slide has a clear owner, a narrative arc, and answers the fundamental question: "so what?" The result is a deck that tells a story about where your company is, where it's going, and what you need from the board.

## When to Use

- **Quarterly board meeting prep**: Synthesize three months of executive conversations into a cohesive board narrative within 48 hours
- **Monthly investor update**: Build lightweight deck versions for angel investors or board observers between formal meetings
- **Fundraising deck prep**: Generate Series A/B/C deck foundation with investor-facing metrics and narrative arc
- **Emergency board communication**: Rapidly assemble crisis or urgent-news decks with transparent problem-solution-timeline structure
- **Annual strategic review**: Create multi-quarter narrative arc showing company evolution and strategic pivots
- **First board deck for new CEO/founder**: Establish board communication baseline when CEO or founder needs to set tone for board engagement

## Instructions

1. **Mine recent executive meetings** for board-relevant themes: wins (product launches, customer wins), risks (churn signals, pipeline delays), asks (hiring approval, budget reallocation), and strategic shifts (market pivots, competitive moves).

2. **Pull metrics from finance syncs, pipeline reviews, and product updates**: ARR growth, pipeline coverage, burn rate, customer acquisition cost, product velocity, deployment frequency. Ensure metrics connect to business narrative, not just raw numbers.

3. **Extract customer evidence**: Sales call quotes showing why deals won or stalled, NPS signals from customer success meetings, churn context explaining departures, expansion stories showing upsell momentum.

4. **Structure into 7-slide narrative arc**: Executive Summary (situation, progress, ask) → Key Metrics (financial/operational health) → Product & Engineering (shipped, learning, roadmap) → Sales & Revenue (pipeline, expansion, churn) → Customer Health (NPS, retention, case studies) → Team & People (hiring, retention, culture signals) → Asks & Discussion Topics (explicit decisions needed, board input requested).

5. **Apply the "so what" test**: Every slide must answer "what does this mean for the company?" Remove slides that are just status updates. Example: "We shipped feature X" only matters if you can answer how it impacts retention, upsell, or competitive moat.

6. **Handle bad news with the transparency framework**: State the problem clearly, explain the root cause, show the plan to fix it, commit to a measurable timeline. Bad news surfaced early with a plan beats surprises at the board table.

7. **Assign slide ownership**: CEO owns slides 1 (summary) and 7 (asks); CRO owns sales/revenue; CPO owns product; CFO owns metrics. Owners present their slides and field questions, creating accountability and depth.

8. **Draft anticipated board questions** and prepare answers: "Why did we miss pipeline targets?" "What's our response to competitor X?" "When do we hit profitability?" Board decks are the setup for dialogue, not a monologue.

## Output Format

Each slide is presented as a structured outline with:
- **Slide title** and **owner** (role responsible for presenting)
- **Narrative** (2–3 sentences answering "what's happening and why should you care")
- **Key data points** (3–5 metrics, percentages, or trends that support the narrative)
- **"So what" statement** (explicit business implication: growth driver, risk to manage, decision needed)
- **Supporting evidence** (quoted excerpt from exec meeting, customer quote, or financial data source)

## Example: Vantage (Series B Supply Chain Analytics)

**Slide 1: Executive Summary** | Owner: CEO
- Narrative: We're in a pivotal moment. Q1 shows 38% ARR growth and exceeded product roadmap, but our enterprise sales pipeline has softened—sales cycle lengthened from 6 to 9 months as procurement teams tightened budgets. We've learned that enterprise deals require deeper buyer coalition building.
- Key data: $2.8M ARR (up from $2.0M Q4), 38% YoY growth, 2.2x net revenue retention, 8 enterprise customers >$100k ACV
- So what: We're profitable on a run-rate basis, but sales cycle inflation is the biggest near-term risk. Solving it unlocks the revenue we've earned.
- Evidence: Q1 board deck; sales pipeline review from March 15

**Slide 2: Key Metrics** | Owner: CFO
- Narrative: Financial health remains strong. Burn is controlled, and MRR growth outpaced churn. CAC is climbing due to increased sales headcount, but payback period is still healthy at 14 months.
- Key data: Monthly burn: $340K, 22-month runway, CAC: $48K (up 12% YoY), LTV:CAC = 3.2x, Gross margin: 76%
- So what: We have a 24-month cushion to prove the sales model and hit breakeven. No fundraising emergency, but we must improve CAC efficiency.
- Evidence: Finance sync, March 10; cohort analysis in data warehouse

**Slide 3: Product & Engineering** | Owner: CPO
- Narrative: We shipped ahead of schedule. AI forecasting model launched in Q1 (originally Q2 roadmap), and early user feedback shows 40% accuracy improvement over legacy rules. Engineering velocity is up 15% with new hire onboarding complete.
- Key data: 6 features shipped (vs. 4 planned), 40% improvement in forecast accuracy, 8-week product cycle time (target: 10 weeks), team headcount +2 engineers
- So what: We have a differentiated capability (AI forecasting) that customers are requesting in sales conversations. This is a competitive moat we should highlight in enterprise deals.
- Evidence: Product demo with top-5 customers; engineering retro notes, March 18

**Slide 4: Sales & Revenue** | Owner: CRO
- Narrative: We're winning deals in mid-market but struggling with enterprise sales cycles. Average sales cycle for mid-market (10–50k ACV) is 4 months; enterprise (100k+ ACV) is now 9 months due to regulatory and procurement hold-ups.
- Key data: $580K in new ARR (Q1 closes, on track for 32% annual growth), 6 new customers (vs. 7 Q4), 18 deals in pipeline ($4.2M), 9-month avg. enterprise sales cycle
- So what: Our mid-market playbook is repeatable. We need to hire enterprise AEs who understand buyer coalitions and regulatory budgeting cycles, and we should build case studies from our 8 enterprise customers.
- Evidence: Sales pipeline review, March 12; deal reviews from Q1 closes

**Slide 5: Customer Health** | Owner: VP Customer Success
- Narrative: Retention is solid at 96%, and expansion is accelerating (2.2x NRR). We had one notable churn (ProduceAI, $45K ARR, switched to competitor due to pricing), but we learned a critical lesson: our product is strong, but we need a multi-tier pricing model for value-based deals.
- Key data: 96% gross retention, 2.2x net revenue retention, NPS 48 (+6 from Q4), 1 churn event, $3 avg. expansion ACV per customer
- So what: We have retention and expansion momentum, but we risk losing competitive deals unless we introduce enterprise pricing tiers by Q3. The ProduceAI loss should trigger a pricing review.
- Evidence: CS monthly, March 8; churn RCA with CEO and CRO, March 16

**Slide 6: Team & People** | Owner: CEO
- Narrative: We're building strength in engineering and sales. Hired 1 enterprise AE, 2 engineers, 1 customer success manager. Internally, we've seen early retention wins in customer success and engineering, signaling strong culture. One gap: we lack a Head of Finance for growth-stage FP&A.
- Key data: 12-person team (+4 YTD), 4 open reqs (Head of Finance, 2 enterprise AEs, 1 product manager), team retention: 100%, internal engagement score: 8.2/10
- So what: Team capacity is our limiting factor for enterprise sales and product. We need to move aggressively on Head of Finance and AE hiring. Culture is strong—we can retain talent.
- Evidence: Team sync, March 20; HR data

**Slide 7: Asks & Discussion Topics** | Owner: CEO
- Narrative: Three asks for board feedback: (1) Approve hiring 4 enterprise AEs and Head of Finance by April, adding $340K annual burn to close longer enterprise cycles faster. (2) Authorize $50K for case study and competitive positioning research to sharpen enterprise messaging. (3) Discuss pricing strategy—do we build a multi-tier model now or wait until Series C?
- So what: These investments directly address our bottleneck (enterprise sales cycle) and our biggest competitive risk (pricing model). We're asking for board conviction on sales-led growth.
- Evidence: Hiring plan review, March 18; finance projections under two staffing scenarios

## Edge Cases

**First-ever board deck (no meeting history)**: Start with founder's origin story and current metric snapshot. Pull from Slack threads, email chains, and 1-on-1 conversations if formal meeting notes don't exist. Focus on what has changed since last fundraise.

**Bad quarter with mostly negative news**: Lead with transparency. State the problem (e.g., "We missed revenue target by 18%"), root cause (e.g., "Two anchor customers delayed deals to next quarter due to their budget resets"), plan (e.g., "We've accelerated our Q2 customer success play and adjusted forecasting"), and timeline (e.g., "We expect recovery by mid-Q2"). Bad news + plan + timeline beats silence.

**Board with mixed technical and non-technical members**: Separate technical narrative (for CPO/CTO to present) into a dedicated appendix. Main deck speaks to business impact, not architecture. Avoid jargon; use analogies ("We rebuilt our database indexes like reorganizing a filing system for faster lookup").

**Fundraising board deck (Series A/B/C structure)**: 7-slide core narrative stays the same, but add 3 appendix slides on market opportunity, competitor comparison, and use-of-funds. Investor boards care about unit economics and TAM more than internal operations.

**Emergency board update (crisis communication)**: Skip the full 7-slide structure. Lead with problem statement, transparency on impact (financial, operational, customer), root cause, immediate actions taken, and what you need from the board (capital, strategic advice, customer introductions). Use a 3-slide crisis deck, not a 7-slide routine deck.
