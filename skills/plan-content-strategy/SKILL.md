## Purpose

Ground your content strategy in real customer language. This skill mines Harmony conversation data—sales calls, support tickets, discovery meetings, CS conversations—to surface recurring themes, objections, and questions your audience actually asks. Instead of guessing what topics matter, you extract voice-of-customer insights and build content pillars, topic clusters, and editorial calendars aligned to how your customers actually think and buy. The result: content that resonates because it answers real problems your customers face.

## When to Use

- **Quarterly content planning**: Refresh your strategy with fresh conversation insights each quarter
- **Launching a new blog or content hub**: Start with customer-backed pillars instead of generic categories
- **Content audit**: Identify gaps between what customers ask about and what you've actually published
- **Entering a new market or customer segment**: Mine conversations from that segment to understand unique pain points and language
- **SEO content gaps**: Spot high-intent topics customers mention that rank competitors—but not you
- **Repurposing meeting insights**: Turn sales call language, objections, and success stories into content calendars
- **Competitive differentiation**: Find angles competitors miss by analyzing what your customers uniquely care about

## Instructions

1. **Mine customer conversations for recurring themes**
   - Pull 50–100 recent Harmony transcripts (sales calls, support, discovery, CS check-ins)
   - Highlight recurring questions, objections, pain points, and success moments
   - Extract actual customer phrases and language—these become your messaging anchors
   - Group by topic: product features, use cases, industry pain points, implementation challenges, ROI concerns

2. **Define 3–5 content pillars aligned to buyer journey**
   - Map pillars to stages: Awareness (problem/market education), Consideration (solution comparison), Decision (buying criteria), Adoption (implementation/best practices), Advocacy (case studies, benchmarks)
   - Each pillar should map to a customer conversation theme and internal strength
   - Example pillars: "Solving [Customer Pain]," "Evaluating [Solution Type]," "Scaling [Use Case]," "Avoiding [Common Failure]"

3. **Build topic clusters under each pillar**
   - Generate 10–15 topics per pillar—each a distinct angle on the pillar's core theme
   - Topics should stem from actual customer language extracted in step 1
   - Organize hierarchically: umbrella topic → subtopics → content pieces

4. **Score topics on four dimensions**
   - **Search signal**: Is this topic searched? (use keyword tools, competitor content, Harmony mention volume)
   - **Buyer intent**: Does this topic influence purchase decisions? (high = decision-stage, low = awareness-stage)
   - **Competitive gap**: Do competitors rank for this? Do they own this narrative? (gap = opportunity)
   - **Internal expertise**: Can you credibly own this topic? (based on team knowledge, data, customer success stories)
   - Create a simple 1–5 scoring table; prioritize high scores across all four dimensions

5. **Map topics to content types**
   - **Searchable content** (SEO-driven): In-depth guides, how-to blogs, comparison frameworks, templates
   - **Shareable content** (audience/social-driven): Customer stories, data reports, industry benchmarks, thought pieces, case studies
   - Each topic should have a primary type; some may need both

6. **Build an editorial calendar**
   - Set a cadence: weekly, bi-weekly, or monthly depending on team capacity
   - Distribute topics across pillars evenly—don't over-invest in one pillar
   - Align timing with buyer journey: awareness content early, decision content before sales cycles
   - Include planned republishing, updates, and cross-promotion angles

7. **Create content briefs for top-priority pieces**
   - For your highest-scored topics, draft a brief (400–500 words)
   - Include: core insight, customer language/quotes to weave in, success metrics, primary keyword(s), tone, and content type
   - Make briefs actionable for writers so they don't guess on angle or depth

8. **Flag and prioritize gaps**
   - List topics customers frequently mention in Harmony data that you haven't published on yet
   - Assess: Can you create this content in-house, or do you need external expertise?
   - Prioritize gaps that are high-intent and low-competition

## Edge Cases

**No conversation data yet?**
- Start with industry benchmarks, competitor content gaps, and sales team objections (ask your AE team what they hear in calls)
- Once you collect 20–30 Harmony transcripts, refresh pillars and topics based on real voice-of-customer

**Single-product company with narrow use cases?**
- Define pillars around use cases and buyer personas instead of feature areas
- Example: "Scaling remote hiring" (use case) vs. "Applicant tracking" (feature)
- Add thought leadership pillar (industry trends, best practices) to broaden appeal

**Technical vs. non-technical audience split?**
- Create separate topic clusters for each audience if they're distinct buyer personas
- Use different content types: technical guides for practitioners, business case studies for decision-makers
- Ensure your editorial calendar reflects both audiences proportionally

**Competitor already dominates a key topic?**
- Don't skip it—identify a differentiated angle (unique data, unique customer case, unique framework)
- Look at Harmony transcripts: how do your customers talk about the topic vs. competitor messaging?
- Build content around your differentiation, not their narrative

**Limited internal expertise?**
- Score topics lower on expertise; plan to hire external writers or partner experts
- Use customer case studies and data to compensate—these don't require deep internal expertise
- Build thought leadership through customer interviews, not solo opinion

**Rapid product changes or multiple product lines?**
- Create a pillar per product line or product tier; ensure each has its own topic clusters
- Refresh content strategy quarterly as product roadmap evolves
- Use Harmony data to track which product or feature is most-discussed by customers
