# Run Product Operations

> The invisible infrastructure that lets great product judgment scale across your entire team.

## What It Does

Product operations is how you scale one PM's judgment into a system that shapes how the entire organization makes decisions. Rather than the PM reviewing every decision, you build processes and dashboards that distribute good judgment. This skill helps you standardize how launches happen, route customer feedback automatically to the right owner, create metrics everyone trusts, and identify bottlenecks before they slow you down. Think of it as "PM scaling" — codifying best practices so everyone has access to good decision-making.

## Who It's For

- **VP of Product** — You're scaling from 2 PMs to 5. You need consistent launch playbooks and a way to route feedback so nothing falls through cracks. Pain: chaos, rework, inconsistent quality.

- **Engineering Manager** — Your team ships slowly but you're not sure if it's PM indecision, unclear specs, or process friction. Pain: unclear requirements, hand-off delays, rework cycles.

- **Operations Professional** — You're hired to build the systems that make product faster. You need a playbook for what "good ops" looks like. Pain: unclear mandate, not knowing where to start.

- **Head of GTM** — Your sales team has great customer feedback but it's not reaching product. You want a structured way to funnel customer voice to decision-makers. Pain: feedback getting lost, disconnect between sales and product.

- **Design Lead** — You manage a growing design team and need consistency in how you review work, handle feedback, and prioritize requests. Pain: no standard process, unclear quality bar, feedback getting lost.

- **Principal Engineer** — You see the organization's cycle time getting slower. You want data and a framework to improve shipping velocity. Pain: process isn't explicit, unclear what's slowing us down.

## Use Cases

- **You're launching features too slowly** — Features take 12 weeks from concept to customer. You need to identify bottlenecks and standardize the process. Arrow: 3 months later, most features ship in 6 weeks, with visibility into where time is spent.

- **Customer feedback arrives everywhere and goes nowhere** — Sales has great customer insights, support has bug reports, customers email PMs directly. Nothing is systematized. Arrow: feedback goes into one intake system, gets triaged, automatically routes to the right PM, with SLAs on acknowledgment and decisions.

- **Each team launches differently** — Team A has a strong PM and launches well. Team B's PM is newer and process is ad-hoc. You need one playbook everyone follows. Arrow: all teams follow the same 6-week launch process, enabling you to compare velocity and surface issues.

- **You don't know if you're shipping what matters** — You're building features but have no clear metrics on adoption, retention impact, or customer satisfaction. Arrow: you establish a metrics dashboard showing which features actually drove business results, informing future prioritization.

- **Cross-functional handoffs are slow** — The handoff from product to engineering takes 2 weeks of meetings to clarify scope. The handoff from product to sales takes another week. Arrow: standardized specs and playbooks reduce handoff time from 2 weeks to 2 days.

- **You're running experiments but not learning from them** — You run 1-2 experiments per month but have no structured way to capture learnings or iterate. Arrow: you implement experiment tracking that captures hypothesis, results, and learnings, increasing experiment velocity to 4-6 per month with documented outcomes.

## Connectors

Works standalone, but richer with **GitHub** (deployment frequency and cycle time metrics), **Linear or Jira** (issue tracking and sprint velocity), **Salesforce** (customer feedback and impact routing), and **Slack** (feedback intake and cross-functional communication).

## Setup

This skill walks you through mapping your current product delivery process, defining standardized stages with clear criteria, building metrics infrastructure and feedback routing. You'll create launch checklists, experiment tracking, and decision playbooks that everyone on your team can reference. Expect 4-6 weeks to go from "we need better process" to "new process is live and we're seeing impact." The first week focuses on audit and baseline measurement. Weeks 2-3 involve building systems and playbooks. Weeks 4-6 are about adoption, calibration, and demonstrating impact. You'll likely find that standardizing just one process (launch or feedback routing) cuts cycle time by 20-30% immediately, creating momentum for broader change. Start with the biggest bottleneck your organization faces, prove impact there, then expand to other processes.

## What You Get

- **Standardized launch playbook** that every team follows, reducing variation and cycle time

- **Feedback routing system** that captures customer voice from all sources and automatically routes it to the right decision-maker

- **Metrics dashboard** showing cycle time, deployment frequency, feature adoption, and churn impact

- **Decision playbooks** documenting "when do we ship vs. wait," "how do we prioritize," "when do we fix bugs"

- **Weekly bottleneck reporting** that surfaces where the organization is slowing down and what to fix next

- **Experiment tracking infrastructure** that captures hypothesis, results, and learnings, enabling faster iteration

## Key Insight

The best ops leaders aren't bottlenecks — they're infrastructure builders. The goal isn't for everyone to ask the ops team for permission. The goal is to build systems that let teams move fast without asking permission. When cycle time drops, feedback gets routed in hours instead of weeks, and experiments ship faster, you'll know your ops work is working. Operations is the invisible infrastructure that scales PM judgment without scaling headcount. The teams that win are those that make great decision-making the default, not the exception.

Start with one metric and one process. Don't try to overhaul everything at once. Pick the biggest bottleneck (usually cycle time or feedback routing), standardize it, measure impact, and use that momentum to expand. Product ops is an investment that compounds — the best time to start was 6 months ago. The second best time is today.
