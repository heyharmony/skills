# Define the Problem

> The best products solve problems customers will change behavior to fix. Everything else is a feature in search of a user.

## What It Does

This skill helps you move past symptoms and into root causes using the Five Whys technique and customer evidence. It guides you to distinguish between "hair on fire" problems that drive behavior change and "nice to solve" problems that customers accept as part of the landscape. You'll learn to frame problems from the customer's perspective, validate severity through frequency-and-impact scoring, and document your findings with direct customer quotes so your team stays grounded in reality. You'll discover what workarounds customers currently use (revealing true pain levels), and recognize that the absence of a workaround often signals a problem customers have accepted as unsolvable.

## Who It's For

- **Product managers** evaluating feature requests or new market opportunities
- **Founders** deciding what problem to build a company around
- **Customer success teams** spotting signals of customer churn or expansion
- **Engineers** understanding the why behind a requirement before designing solutions
- **Sales leaders** distinguishing between objections (symptoms) and actual deal blockers (problems)
- **Strategy teams** prioritizing between competing problems in crowded roadmaps
- **Researchers and insights teams** building a common language around customer pain
- **GTM leaders** ensuring product-market fit exists before scaling acquisition

## Use Cases

- **Feature request evaluation**: Customer asks for Feature X. You dig to understand the actual problem they're trying to solve, discover Feature Y would solve it better → better product, happier customer
- **Roadmap prioritization**: Two teams want to build different things. You define the problem each team is tackling, score severity, and the priority becomes obvious → alignment without debate
- **Churn analysis**: A customer is leaving. You identify the unsolved problem driving their decision → you can either solve it or understand your positioning limits
- **New market expansion**: You're entering a new segment. You validate that the problem exists and is severe enough to justify investment → you avoid building products for nonexistent pain
- **Competitive analysis**: A competitor is winning in your market. You reverse-engineer the problem they're solving → you realize the problem is bigger or different than you thought
- **Onboarding or adoption friction**: New customers aren't using the product after signup. You dig into what problem they thought they were solving vs. what your product actually solves → you fix messaging or positioning
- **Distinguishing between "problem" and "request"**: A customer says they need your product to do X. You ask "what's the problem that X would solve?" and discover it's a nice-to-have, not a must-solve

## Connectors

**Works standalone**, but richer with:
- **Zoom**: Extract customer quotes and pain signals from recorded calls; identify where speakers move from symptoms to root causes
- **HubSpot**: Track which customers have the problem most acutely; correlate problem severity with churn or expansion
- **Slack**: Capture customer feedback from sales and support channels; surface recurring pain themes
- **Google Drive**: Collaborate on problem documentation and impact scoring with your team
- **Linear**: Link problem definitions to roadmap items, ensuring the problem you're solving is clear before engineering starts

## Setup

1. Gather 5-10 customer interviews or support tickets mentioning the suspected problem
2. Extract verbatim quotes where customers describe pain, workarounds, or resignation
3. Run Five Whys on 3-5 customer pain points to identify patterns in root causes
4. Score each problem: Frequency (1-5) × Impact (1-5) × Reach (% of customers)
5. Test your framing: Would a customer new to your company immediately recognize their pain in your problem statement?

## What You Get

- A focused problem statement grounded in customer quotes and data (not opinions). Use this in roadmap planning to ensure you're building for real pain.
- A Five Whys breakdown showing the chain from symptom to root cause. This reveals whether you're treating the actual problem or a symptom.
- A severity score combining frequency, impact, and market reach with supporting evidence. This gives you a defensible way to prioritize between competing problems.
- Evidence of what workarounds customers currently accept (revealing true pain levels). No workaround signals acceptance, not lack of pain.
- Clarity on whether this is a "must solve" or "nice to have" opportunity. Use this to decide how much investment is justified.
- A shared definition your team can reference when debating features or priorities. This becomes a conversation anchor.
- A "problem validation" checklist for future feature discussions so you stay grounded in customer reality instead of drifting into assumption

## Key Insight

The best problems are ones customers are already trying to solve—just poorly. If customers have built no workaround, they've either accepted the problem as unsolvable or it doesn't matter enough to act on. Listen for what customers do, not just what they say they wish for.

## Pro Tips

- Watch customer behavior, don't just listen to complaints. The problems they're paying to solve reveal more than the problems they mention.
- Severity isn't just frequency × impact. Include "cost of workaround." If a workaround costs 20 hours/month per customer, that's high-severity pain.
- Revisit your problem definition quarterly. As you solve problems, new ones surface. Your problem thesis should evolve.
