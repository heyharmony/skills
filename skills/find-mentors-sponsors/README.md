# Find Mentors & Sponsors

> Mentors teach you how to think. Sponsors bet their capital on your success. You need both.

## What It Does

This skill helps you build a deliberate network of mentors who accelerate your learning and sponsors who amplify your career velocity. Mentorship doesn't happen passively — it's a relationship you create with specific asks and sustained engagement. You'll identify gaps in your learning, map candidates in your network, learn how to start conversations without making them transactional, and build reciprocal relationships that last.

Sponsors are rarer and require visibility plus trust. Unlike mentors who advise, sponsors bet their professional capital on you — advocating in rooms you're not in. This skill shows you how to develop both types of relationships deliberately.

## Who It's For

- **Early-career professionals** who realize one manager can't teach you everything and need multiple perspectives
- **Career transitioners** moving to a new role, function, or industry who need navigators
- **High performers feeling stuck** who know learning stalls without external input and mentorship
- **Managers moving to director+ roles** who need mentors who've been there before
- **Remote/distributed workers** without natural mentorship from proximity; need deliberate relationship-building
- **People from underrepresented groups** who actively want mentorship to accelerate and reduce underestimation
- **Technical specialists** wanting to develop leadership capability or move toward architecture roles
- **People navigating office politics** who need external advisors to help decode dynamics and find allies

## Use Cases

- **Starting a new role** → Find someone 2–3 years ahead who navigated that role and will level-set expectations
- **Before a career transition** → Mentors help you think through whether it's the right move and how to prepare
- **Stuck on a technical or people problem** → A mentor can hold up a mirror and surface patterns you're too close to see
- **Preparing for promotion or raise conversations** → Sponsors make the difference; they advocate in rooms you're not in
- **Navigating a new company culture** → Internal mentors/sponsors help you understand unwritten rules faster
- **Building leadership credibility** → Mentorship relationships, especially with senior people, signal you're someone worth investing in
- **Recovering from a setback** → A sponsor can be the difference between being passed over and getting a second chance
- **Identifying your blind spots** → Mentors see patterns in how you show up that you can't see yourself

## Connectors

**Works standalone**, but richer with:
- **LinkedIn** — Identify potential mentors by role progression, skills, and timeline (people 2–3 years ahead)
- **Slack** — Surface who you already work well with, who's given you feedback, and who's championed you
- **Google Calendar** — Recognize learning patterns and schedule recurring mentor check-ins
- **Gmail** — Extract feedback threads and recognition to identify advocates and potential sponsors

## Setup

No setup required. You can map your network and build mentor relationships manually. If you connect data sources, the skill can surface relationships you already have and who deserves more attention.

When Harmony conversation data exists — recorded 1:1s, team meetings, skip-levels — the skill identifies people who've already given you meaningful feedback or advocated for your work. These are your latent mentors and potential sponsors. The skill helps you recognize and activate relationships you may not have been deliberately nurturing.

## What You Get

- A mapped mentor network by expertise (3–4 mentors for different strengths: career strategy, technical depth, people leadership, industry fluency)
- Identified sponsors with influence and visibility into your next opportunities (and how to make them aware of you)
- A list of growth gaps and specific mentor candidates to approach
- Templates for initial asks (how to start mentorship without making it awkward, how to be a low-ask mentee)
- A 90-day activation plan to build and strengthen relationships (cadence, topics, reciprocity gestures)
- Conversation frameworks for everything from "first ask" to "follow-up" to "asking for sponsorship"
- Maintenance strategy to keep relationships warm over time (how often, what to share, how to provide value back)

## Key Insight

The number one mistake people make with mentorship is treating it as a formal commitment. The best mentor relationships start with a single, specific question — not "will you be my mentor?" Build a diverse network of 3-4 people with different strengths. Meet each monthly. Always circle back to show how you applied their advice. That follow-through is what transforms a one-time conversation into a lasting relationship. And remember — the best mentors are often only 2-3 years ahead of you, not 20.
