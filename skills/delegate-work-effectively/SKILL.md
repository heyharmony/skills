## Purpose

Great delegation is context without control. You give people the full picture—the why, the constraints, the trade-offs—and then trust them to execute. The trap is either over-controlling (micromanagement) or under-communicating (leaving people hanging). This skill helps you strike the balance: give away work that develops people, retain work that leverages your unique strengths, and build ownership culture.

## When to Use

- Before assigning a new project or responsibility
- When you're overwhelmed and need to move work off your plate
- Transitioning from "doer" to "editor" and struggling with letting go
- When you sense someone isn't clear on what success looks like
- To calibrate what you should own vs. what you should give away

## Instructions

1. **Don't Delegate Your Superpower.** Some work is uniquely high-leverage because of your judgment, relationships, or expertise. Protect that. Delegate everything else. Example: A CEO shouldn't delegate fundraising but should delegate operational execution. A VP Product shouldn't delegate strategy but should delegate roadmap execution.

2. **Give Away Your Legos.** You have skills that compound—writing, decision-making, negotiation, design. These are your "Legos." You don't retain exclusive ownership. You teach others, delegate projects that let them practice, and mentor their growth. The more people who have your skills, the more you multiply impact.

3. **Build Context, Not Control.** In the delegation conversation or brief, cover: Why this matters (customer impact, strategic importance), Constraints (timeline, budget, technical requirements), Decision framework (how to choose between options), Success metrics (what does done look like), and Escalation triggers (when to loop you in). Then shut up and let them execute.

4. **Be Explicit About Ownership and Approval.** Is this fully theirs to decide? Theirs with your review before launch? Fully yours with their input? Clarify the approval chain. Ambiguity creates friction. Example: "You own the design decisions. You'll review with me before engineering starts. I'll review final implementation before shipping."

5. **Specify Escalation Triggers, Not Permission Seeking.** Don't say "check with me if you need to." That kills speed. Instead: "Loop me in if X happens, or if you're hitting this constraint." Make the triggers concrete. "If you need to cut scope, tell me. If you're adjusting timeline beyond two weeks, let's sync."

6. **Use the Doer-to-Editor Transition.** As a company grows, leaders shift from doing to editing others' work. This is hard. You have taste and opinions but must let other people's competence lead. Start by giving them the work, staying hands-on for the first iteration, then stepping back. Feedback (editing), not control.

7. **Practice Selective Micromanagement When Needed.** Being in the details isn't inherently micromanagement—it's contextual. New hires or high-stakes projects warrant more detail-level visibility. Mature, high-performing people need less. Be explicit about why you're asking for more detail, and dial it back as their confidence increases.

8. **Run the Six-Month Calibration Test.** After six months of ownership, check: Has this person grown into the role? Are they exercising good judgment? Making the calls you'd make? If yes, lean back. If no, increase context and coaching, not oversight. The goal is to build judgment, not to prove your intelligence.

9. **Create Feedback Loops, Not Surprise Reviews.** Don't wait six months to tell someone they're struggling. Check in early and often. "How's the project feeling? What are you learning? What's blocking you?" Build rhythm into the relationship so feedback comes naturally, not as a performance shock.

10. **Document the Delegation.** Write down: ownership (who's leading), success metrics (how we measure done), timeline, constraints, decision authority, and escalation triggers. Store in Notion or your shared system. This creates clarity and a record if context is needed later.

## Output Format

The skill produces a delegation brief and accountability structure:

**Delegation Brief (shared with the person taking the work):**
- Project or responsibility name and owner
- Why this matters (customer, strategic, business impact)
- Success metrics or definition of done (specific, measurable)
- Timeline and key milestones
- Constraints (budget, technical, organizational)
- Decision framework (how to evaluate trade-offs)
- Scope boundaries (what's included, what's not)
- Approval chain (who reviews what, when)
- Escalation triggers (specific moments to loop you in)
- Your commitments (what you'll provide: context, resources, unblocking)

**Ownership & Accountability Document:**
- Owner name and role
- Project/responsibility
- Start and review dates
- Success metrics and how you'll measure
- Regular check-in cadence (weekly, bi-weekly, etc.)
- Feedback mechanism (how you'll give input)
- Growth opportunities in this work (skills they'll develop)

## Example

**Delegation Brief: Launch New Pricing Page at Elevate (SaaS platform)**

Owner: Priya, Senior Product Manager

Why this matters: Current pricing page is low-conversion. New design tests show 23% higher click-through to trial signup. Shipping this in Q2 unblocks revenue growth targets.

Success metrics:
- Page ships by May 31
- A/B test shows 20%+ lift vs. old page (or we iterate)
- Support volume for pricing questions drops 15%
- Zero security/compliance issues

Timeline:
- Week 1: Design finalized (Priya + Design)
- Week 2-3: Build and QA (Priya + Engineering)
- Week 4: Testing and iteration
- May 31: Ship to 100%

Constraints:
- Marketing budget for promotion: $50K
- Must work on mobile (we're 65% mobile traffic)
- Must comply with new regulation on tax display (Javier in Legal will advise)
- Can't require new infrastructure

Decision framework:
- When evaluating design options: optimize for conversion, then mobile UX, then brand consistency
- When cutting scope: maintain A/B test rigor, then feature completeness, then polish
- When timeline pressure hits: add people before cutting scope

Approval chain:
- Priya owns design and copy decisions
- VP Design reviews final design before handoff to engineering
- You (Chief Product Officer) review pricing positioning and messaging before launch
- Legal reviews tax/compliance language (Javier signs off)

Escalation triggers:
- If engineering capacity changes, tell me immediately
- If the A/B test shows opposite results, we sync before iterating
- If timeline slips more than one week, loop me in for resource planning

Your commitments:
- Unblock any legal/compliance questions within 48 hours
- Provide marketing context (audience, positioning, promotion plan) in week 1
- Review messaging mockup by [date] so design doesn't iterate
- Run weekly 15-min sync with Priya (every Monday, 2pm)

---

**Ownership & Accountability Document:**

Owner: Priya
Responsibility: Lead Launch of New Pricing Page
Start: March 24
Review Cycle: Monthly checkpoint (April 24, May 24) + post-launch review (June 14)

Success metrics:
- Launch on time (May 31)
- A/B test hit 20% lift target
- Support ticket deflation (-15% pricing questions)

Calibration questions (check-in each month):
- How confident are you in the current design direction?
- What's your biggest risk or unknown?
- What decision could only you make, vs. what needs my input?

Feedback mechanism:
- Weekly async Slack updates (Friday end-of-day)
- Monthly sync meetings (design review, results, blockers)
- Real-time escalation if [trigger] happens

Growth opportunity: This launches Priya into more autonomous product ownership. You'll use this as reference for larger initiatives later.

## Edge Cases

**Delegation to Someone Struggling or New.** More structure, more check-ins, more context upfront. Break the work into smaller increments with feedback loops. "You've got this, and I'm here to help." This builds confidence and competence faster than hands-off delegation.

**You're Delegating Away Your Favorite Work.** Hard but necessary. If you're in the details of something you love, you're blocking two things: the person's growth and your own scaling. Delegate it. Coach them. Step back. This is the progression of leadership.

**Delegated Work Goes Wrong.** Don't retroactively take it back and redo it yourself. That kills ownership culture. Instead: understand what happened, coach them through fixing it, and adjust context or oversight for next time. Failure is data.

**Person Over-Escalates (Asks Permission Too Often).** Redirect: "What would you do if I weren't here? Try that first, then tell me what happened." Build judgment, not dependency. If they keep escalating, your context wasn't clear or they're not ready. Address it directly.

**You're Maintaining Too Much Approval Authority.** This kills speed. For non-critical decisions, push approval down. "You're the owner. Decide and tell me what you decided." You review decisions, not every step. This is the editor mindset.

**Delegation as Punishment.** If you delegate something hard/unpopular as a consequence, people feel it. Delegation should develop people and distribute work. If you're using it as an excuse to unload, people check out. Separate urgent unloading from intentional development.
