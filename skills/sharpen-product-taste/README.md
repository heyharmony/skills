# Sharpen Product Taste

> Product taste is the ability to make thousands of micro-decisions that add up to a coherent, delightful experience. It's learnable.

## What It Does

This skill helps you develop product taste—the ability to articulate why something works and make intentional design choices that compound into a coherent experience. You'll build a personal library of great and terrible products, document the principles that make them work, and use those principles to guide design decisions. Instead of "I like this" or "I don't like that," you'll learn to say "This violates our principle of simplicity" or "This follows the pattern we admire in Stripe." Taste becomes a decision-making tool: when your team debates a design, you reference principles instead of personal preference. Over time, thousands of small intentional choices create a product that feels deliberately designed, not like features bolted together.

## Who It's For

- **Product managers** making daily decisions about features, UX, and product coherence
- **Design leaders** teaching the team to think about principles, not just pixels
- **Founders** establishing a product culture and philosophy from day one
- **PMs new to a company** learning the existing taste and product philosophy
- **Teams with taste disagreements** who need a principled framework for decisions
- **Design reviewers** who want to evaluate work against coherent standards
- **Hiring managers** assessing product taste in candidates during interviews
- **Cross-functional teams** who need a common language for evaluating design tradeoffs

## Use Cases

- **Design review process**: Before approving a feature, evaluate whether it fits your product's coherence and taste principles → you ship more intentional products that feel like one unified thing, not feature salad
- **Teaching product culture**: Onboard a new PM or designer by building a taste library together → they understand not just "what we do" but "why we choose that over this"
- **Resolving design disagreement**: Two designers propose different approaches to the same problem. Reference your taste principles → decisions are principled, not about who argues better
- **Competitive teardown**: A competitor ships something clever. Analyze what principle they're following, then decide whether to adopt it → you make intentional choices about what fits your product, not reactive copies
- **Feature prioritization**: You're considering 10 features. Some fit your taste, some don't. Use taste principles as a filter → you focus on things that compound toward coherence
- **Hiring or onboarding**: You need to assess or teach product sense. Use taste analysis as a training tool → you can have conversations about principles, not just gut feelings
- **Product refinement cycles**: Every few quarters, audit your product against your taste principles. What's accumulated complexity? What no longer fits? → you proactively de-clutter before debt becomes overwhelming

## Setup

1. Start a "taste library" in Notion with products you love and hate
2. For each product, take screenshots of moments that feel elegant or wrong
3. Document the principle: What design choice underlies that moment?
4. Identify 3-5 taste principles that define your product
5. Use those principles in design reviews and feature decisions
6. Add 1-2 new products to your library each week
7. Discuss principles with your team regularly; build shared taste

## What You Get

- A taste library of 20+ products with analyzed principles and specific examples
- 3-5 documented principles that define your product and guide daily decisions (e.g., "simplicity", "unambiguous", "delightful")
- A framework for evaluating design decisions without resorting to personal preference or the loudest voice
- Skills to teach taste to new PMs and designers, turning intuition into teachable patterns
- A product that feels intentional, not like features bolted together or accumulated over time
- Faster design reviews: debate principles instead of debating personal taste
- A taste "rubric" for design reviews that your whole team can reference and use consistently

## Key Insight

Taste is accumulated judgment. Every design choice either reinforces your product's principles or erodes them. The teams that win build products where thousands of small intentional choices compound into something coherent. Start building your taste library now—the longer you study great products, the more obvious these principles become.

## Pro Tips

- Analyze products you hate as carefully as ones you love. What principle is violated? What could fix it? This sharpens your taste.
- When you disagree with a design, force yourself to articulate the principle being violated instead of just saying "I don't like it."
- Your taste library compounds over time. In 6 months, you'll have 50+ products analyzed. In a year, 100+. This becomes your design reference library.
