# Define Brand Voice

## Purpose

Most brand voice guides are aspirational fiction written by agencies that don't know your business. This skill does the opposite: it mines authentic voice from evidence — actual conversations your team has with customers in sales calls, support interactions, and internal meetings captured in Harmony. Instead of guessing how you *should* sound, you codify how you actually do sound. The result is a tone guide that feels natural to your team, resonates with your customers, and solves the consistency problem across content creators, channels, and time.

## When to Use

- **Launching your brand for the first time** — You need a voice foundation, but starting from conversations is faster and more authentic than blank-page worksheets
- **Rebranding or refreshing your voice** — Your old guidelines feel stale; conversation data shows how your voice has naturally evolved
- **Onboarding new writers, marketers, or customer-facing teams** — Give them a voice playbook grounded in real examples, not vague adjectives
- **Your brand voice feels inconsistent across channels** — Email sounds different from social; sales messaging doesn't match support tone. Find the root cause in transcripts
- **Preparing a brand book or style guide** — Use this as the foundation; add visual elements and brand history later
- **Post-acquisition or merger** — Two companies need to blend voices. Conversation data reveals what's worth keeping from each culture
- **Launching into a new market or customer segment** — Does your voice need to shift? Analyze calls with the new audience to find the right tone

## Instructions

**Step 1: Gather and Mine Conversation Transcripts**
Pull 10–20 recent call transcripts or conversation exports from Harmony covering sales, support, CS, and internal team discussions. Look for communication patterns: Are your people formal or casual? Technical or plain English? Direct or diplomatic? Empathetic or solution-focused? Note the moments where reps or CSMs sound most authentic and persuasive.

**Step 2: Identify Your Natural Vocabulary**
Scan transcripts for words and phrases your best performers use repeatedly. Not buzzwords—actual language. Examples: "Let's dig into this" vs. "We will conduct a deeper analysis"; "Help you avoid surprises" vs. "Risk mitigation strategies." Create a list of signature vocabulary, frequently used metaphors, and customer-friendly alternatives to industry jargon.

**Step 3: Build a Voice Attributes Matrix**
Distill your voice into 3–4 core attributes. Each attribute should be a spectrum, not a binary. Example: "Direct but warm" (not "direct OR warm"), "Expert but accessible" (not "expert OR accessible"). For each attribute, write 2–3 do examples and 2–3 don't examples drawn directly from your transcripts.

**Step 4: Create a Tone Spectrum**
Show how your core voice shifts by context. Sales pitch might lean into confidence and urgency; support ticket might emphasize empathy and clarity; blog might be educational and conversational; internal Slack might be irreverent. Map your three core attributes across five contexts and show how they flex without breaking character.

**Step 5: Document Vocabulary Rules**
List your preferred terms, banned jargon, and customer-friendly alternatives. Examples: "onboard" vs. "get you set up"; "leverage" (avoid) vs. "use" or "tap into"; "synergy" (never). Include technical terms that are non-negotiable (API, SDK) and which audience needs them explained.

**Step 6: Write Sample Copy Across Channels**
Draft short, realistic examples showing your voice in action. Include: sales email pitch, social media post, help doc, landing page headline, internal team message. Each should feel consistent in personality but appropriate to the medium. Reference real product features or customer wins from your calls.

**Step 7: Build a Voice Card**
Distill everything into one-page, printable reference. Include: three voice attributes with do/don't bullets, tone spectrum one-liner, three banned words, three signature phrases, and a quickstart ("When in doubt, sound like your best sales rep on a good call"). This is what writers will keep on their desk or pin in Slack.

## Output Format

Your Define Brand Voice deliverable includes:

1. **Voice Attributes Matrix** (3–4 attributes, each with 3 do/don't pairs)
2. **Tone Spectrum** (your core voice across 5 contexts: sales, support, marketing, product, internal)
3. **Vocabulary Guide** (preferred terms, banned jargon, customer-friendly rewrites)
4. **Sample Copy** (5 channels, 50–100 words each, showing voice in context)
5. **Voice Card** (one-page printable quick reference)
6. **Conversation Audit Trail** (list of transcripts mined, dates, call types, key patterns found)

All sections emphasize specificity: exact phrases, real examples, conversation-backed patterns. No vague adjectives.

## Example: Pollen (Sustainability Analytics SaaS)

### Transcript Excerpts Revealing Natural Tone

From sales call with enterprise prospect:
> "Look, sustainability reporting is a nightmare right now. You're either using spreadsheets from 2005 or paying six figures for software that's harder to configure than your tax return. We built Pollen because our founders got tired of that too. You plug in your data, we handle the complexity, you get a report you can actually show your board in an hour. No PHD required."

From support chat:
> "Hey! Saw you hit a snag with the API. Totally normal—that endpoint has a quirk. Let me walk you through it real quick. Also just pushed a fix that'll make this smoother for everyone. You should see it live in about an hour."

From internal Slack:
> "This customer is trying to report on something our data model doesn't quite support yet. Instead of telling them 'not possible' (boring), can we figure out what they're *actually* trying to measure and find a creative workaround? They're a perfect fit for us otherwise."

### Voice Attributes Derived

**Attribute 1: Practical, Not Preachy**
- DO: "Your current process probably takes weeks and feels error-prone"
- DO: "Here's the shortest path to getting this live"
- DON'T: "Enterprise sustainability challenges require innovative solutions"
- DON'T: "Unlock the power of real-time environmental intelligence"

**Attribute 2: Expert but Irreverent**
- DO: "The compliance landscape is a mess, so we obsess over getting the details right"
- DO: "We handle the boring math so you don't have to think about it"
- DON'T: "Pollen leverages advanced algorithmic frameworks"
- DON'T: Overly formal or corporate-speak

**Attribute 3: Helpful Problem-Solver**
- DO: "What are you actually trying to measure? Let's figure this out together"
- DO: "This is broken; we're fixing it today"
- DON'T: "Per your inquiry..." or "As per our policy..."
- DON'T: Deflecting or passing the buck

### Sample Copy Across Channels

**Sales Email:**
"Sustainability reporting used to mean choosing between incomplete data and spreadsheet hell. We built Pollen because that choice shouldn't exist. In an hour, you'll have a board-ready report that actually reflects your operations. Let's see if it's a fit—here's a quick walkthrough (no sales jargon, we promise)."

**Social Media:**
"Your sustainability data is probably scattered across five systems and a spreadsheet named 'FINAL_v3_ACTUALFINAL.xlsx.' Ours too, once. Pollen fixes that. Clean data. Clear reports. No spreadsheet drama."

**Help Doc:**
"If your export is taking forever, it's probably because you're pulling multiple years of data across all facilities at once. Try filtering by year first, then we'll merge them. (We're working on making this automatic—should land next month.)"

**Landing Page Headline:**
"Sustainability reporting that doesn't suck. Finally."

**Internal Slack Message:**
"Got a feature request from a customer today that we technically don't support yet. But I think we can hack a workaround using the reporting API. Might actually be better than our roadmap. Want to pair on it tomorrow?"

### Voice Card (One Page)

**POLLEN VOICE CARD**

**Three Core Attributes**
1. **Practical, Not Preachy** — We solve real problems, not sell buzzwords
2. **Expert but Irreverent** — We know our stuff and refuse to sound boring about it
3. **Helpful Problem-Solver** — We work with you, not at you

**DO Say**
- "Here's the shortest path to..."
- "This is broken; we're fixing it today"
- "What are you trying to measure?"
- "Our founders were frustrated too"

**DON'T Say**
- "Leverage," "synergy," "unlock potential"
- Formal corporate-speak
- "Per your inquiry"
- Overly technical jargon (explain or skip it)

**Tone by Context**
- Sales: confident, problem-focused, brief
- Support: empathetic, solution-oriented, conversational
- Marketing: witty, concrete, relatable
- Product (in-app): helpful, clear, humble
- Internal: collaborative, honest, direct

**When in Doubt** — Sound like your best sales rep on a good call: honest about the problem, confident about your solution, and actually giving a damn about the customer's outcome.

## Edge Cases

**Inconsistent voice across your team** — If your reps sound totally different, pick the strongest voice patterns and create a brief training. Make consistency a team practice, not a police action. Reference specific calls: "Remember when Sarah said X on that enterprise demo? That's our voice."

**Highly technical product needing dual voice** — B2D2C plays need both. Create a "Technical Voice" (for engineers: precise, jargon-rich, reference-filled) and a "Business Voice" (for buyers: problem-focused, benefit-driven, jargon-lite). Show when each applies and how they coexist.

**Startup with zero established voice** — You probably have one anyway; you just haven't noticed it. Pull your last 10 customer calls. Whichever rep had the best close or happiest customer—that's your voice baseline. Build from there.

**International or multilingual teams** — Voice attributes translate, but vocabulary and tone don't. Build separate voice guides for each language/market. Core attributes can be shared; execution must be local. Example: "Helpful problem-solver" stays true, but the jokes and phrasing shift.

**Merger or acquisition** — Interview reps and leaders from both teams about their voice. Often one company's voice is stronger or better aligned with your future. Sometimes you need a blend. Use conversation data to make this decision, not ego.

**Post-launch voice drift** — Run this skill quarterly on fresh transcripts. You'll notice if your voice is drifting. This is actually healthy—voices should evolve with your product and market. Just make sure it's intentional, not accidental.
