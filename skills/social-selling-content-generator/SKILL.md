## Purpose

Turn real customer conversations — sales calls, discovery meetings, objection handling, success stories — into authentic, high-engagement social selling content. When Harmony conversation data exists, extracts direct quotes, buying signals, and competitive insights to create posts that prove your value, not just claim it. When it's not available, works from provided customer stories and competitive context to generate social content angles. Every piece of content grounds the claim in something real, making it more credible and shareable.

## When to Use

- Sales reps building their personal brand on LinkedIn and needing authentic talking points
- Sales teams running social selling campaigns and needing 10–20 posts to cover core objections or use cases
- Sales leaders wanting to share wins, insights, and methodology without naming customers
- Product marketing wanting social proof from real customer conversations to support campaign messaging
- Organizations building a thought leadership program and needing content that resonates with the target buyer

## Instructions

### 1. Define the social selling goal

Ask the user:

- **What's the goal?** Build rep personal brand, drive inbound leads, establish thought leadership, support a campaign?
- **What's the audience?** Prospect personas, vertical, company size, title?
- **What themes?** Objections you hear repeatedly, win patterns, market insights, methodology, product features?
- **Where does it run?** LinkedIn posts, LinkedIn articles, Twitter/X, company blog?

### 2. Gather customer conversation data

If Harmony data exists, pull:
- Sales calls mentioning pain points, objections, or buying signals
- Customer meetings where the customer articulated their problem in their own words
- Demo or objection-handling calls where the rep overcame a common resistance
- Win calls or success stories where the customer described the impact
- Competitive discussions where prospects mentioned alternatives and why they chose you

For each moment:
- **The quote:** Exact language from the customer (anonymized or attributed)
- **The insight:** Why this quote matters (addresses common objection, proves a value prop, shows market trend)
- **The use case:** Which prospect or situation would find this relevant

### 3. Identify high-value content angles

Map customer insights to social selling themes:

**Objection responses:**
- Common objection you hear? Pull the response from a winning rep's call
- How did the rep reframe it? What data did they use? Turn that into a "here's how we address concern X" post

**Use case stories:**
- Which customer problem appears across multiple deals? That's a repeatable use case worth highlighting

**Competitive context:**
- What competitors do prospects mention? How do you differentiate? Turn that into a "why we're different from [alt]" post without naming them

**Methodology or process:**
- Your MEDDIC sales process? How to qualify faster? What's a red flag in a customer conversation? Turn those into thought leadership

**Market insights:**
- Patterns you see across calls (budget concerns, timing, buying committee size)? That's insight-driven content

### 4. Generate content variants

For each angle, create multiple variants:

**Hook (attention-grabbing line for the feed):**
- Question that resonates (e.g., "Why do 70% of the deals we see stall in evaluation?")
- Counterintuitive statement (e.g., "The best sales reps skip the demo")
- Stat or insight (e.g., "It takes 5 stakeholders to approve in mid-market")

**Body (the insight or story, 100–300 words):**
- What did customers say (quote or paraphrased)
- Why it matters (proof that this is a real problem, not a made-up one)
- How to think about it (share your methodology, not just your product)

**CTA (what you want the reader to do):**
- Comment with their experience
- DM you to discuss
- Share a related insight
- Visit a resource

### 5. Add context and proof

For each post:

- **Mention the source** (anonymously): "In a recent call with a VP of Sales at a Series B company..." or "Our data from 50+ deals shows..."
- **Add a data point if possible:** From Harmony Insights or CRM (e.g., "average deal cycle is 120 days" or "3.5 stakeholders involved")
- **Reference the methodology or framework** if it's yours (e.g., "In our MEDDIC process, we call this the Economic Buyer step")

### 6. Format for publishing

Create the final post with platform-specific guidance:

- **LinkedIn post:** Under 1,300 chars (auto-cut), line breaks for readability, 1–2 hashtags, emoji if tone is lighter
- **LinkedIn article:** 800–2,000 words, headline + subheading, 3–5 sections, pull quote or data callout
- **Twitter/X:** Under 280 chars, hook + link, relevant hashtag
- **Blog post:** 1,500–3,000 words, SEO headline, intro, sections, closing, CTA

## Performance Expectations

**Typical LinkedIn post engagement (for sales rep with 500–2K connections):**
- Impressions: 1,500–3,000
- Engagement rate: 2–5% (25–150 interactions)
- Share/comments signal: Stronger if you ask a question or invite disagreement

**Tips to boost:**
- Posts with quotes outperform generic advice
- Posts that acknowledge the hard truth (not just the solution) get more comments
- Specificity beats generality ("3 objections we hear in SaaS sales" beats "how to handle objections")

## Now What?

- **Schedule in advance:** Use LinkedIn's native scheduler to post 2–3x per week consistently
- **Track engagement:** Note which themes, formats, and hooks get the most traction; double down on winners
- **Measure pipeline impact:** Tag posts with UTM or note which deals mentioned "I saw your post on this"
- **Iterate:** After 4 weeks, review top-performing posts and create more in that vein

### Post #3: The Integration Checklist
**Format:** LinkedIn Post
**Theme:** Market insight / Objection response
**Source:** Customer calls across 5 deals mentioning integration as buying criteria

---

Evaluating new software?

Here's the integration checklist your engineering team should use (and most buying committees skip):

✓ **Can I set it up without a vendor engineer?** (Most can't. If they require 40 hours of pro services, you're locked in.)

✓ **Does it use webhooks or API-first?** (Webhooks = you maintaining a connection. API = it maintains the connection.)

✓ **Is the integration in *their* product or *yours*?** (If it's only in theirs, they own the timeline for updates.)

✓ **What's the implementation fee and timeline?** (If they won't quote it upfront, walk away.)

I've seen teams pick tools that *technically* integrate but create 3+ hours of weekly maintenance. That kills ROI.

Ask the hard questions. Most vendors won't have answers.

### Post #7: The Competitive Trap
**Format:** LinkedIn Article (this one is longer — 1,000 words, pub on LinkedIn)
**Theme:** Thought leadership
**Source:** Patterns from sales calls and competitive win data

**Headline:** Why Feature Parity Kills Product Strategy (And How to Avoid It)

---

Every SaaS vendor does it: they raise funding, see a competitor's feature roadmap, and immediately panic.

"We need that feature."

Within 6 months, they've added 40 features that:
- Distract from core value
- Confuse the buying team
- Slow down the product
- Fragment the customer base

I see it constantly in calls with prospects.

They're evaluating us against Competitor Corp. Both of us have 90% feature parity. But one is 10x easier to implement.

Here's what happens: They choose on implementation speed, not features.

But guess what? The sales team doesn't know that. They're still pitching features.

**The Real Differentiator: Operational Excellence**

Features are table-stakes. What wins is *how* you deliver them.

Three things I hear from customers who chose us over alternatives with "better" features:
1. Setup is 2 weeks, not 8
2. Our API is readable and documented
3. They can switch integrations without calling us

That's not exciting. But it's valuable.

**What This Means for Your Buying Decision**

If you're evaluating SaaS, ignore feature lists. They're almost always the same.

Focus on:
- **Time to value:** How fast can your team get real work done?
- **Maintainability:** How much of this is your problem vs. theirs?
- **Flexibility:** Can you customize or are you locked into their workflow?

The vendor who can answer those questions clearly wins. Most can't.

**For Product Teams Reading This**

Don't chase feature parity. It's a losing game.

Double down on operability instead:
- Make setup 10x faster than competitors
- Document everything like your business depends on it (it does)
- Build customer self-service first, vendor support second

The feature checklists will always be similar. The ones that win are the ones that *work*.

## Edge Cases

- **Customer requests anonymity:** Anonymize the company name but keep the specificity ("VP of Operations at a Series B SaaS company" vs. "a customer"). Quote still lands if it's authentic.
- **Competitor asks their legal team to take down a post calling them out:** You can post about competitive advantages without naming them. Instead of "Competitor Corp takes 8 weeks," say "Most vendors take 8 weeks, we do it in 2 weeks." Same point, legal-proof.
- **Your favorite customer story is too specific:** Break it into generic lessons. Instead of "Acme Corp saved 200 hours," say "Our average customer saves 40 hours per FTE in year one." Truth + privacy.
- **Sales VP asks you not to post about a competitive win:** Respect it. There may be renewal risk or strategic reasons. Generate content from wins you can publicly celebrate.
- **Post lands but nobody engages:** Check the timing (maybe try a different day), the hook (maybe it's too vague), or the length (maybe it's too long). Iterate, don't kill the theme.
- **Someone argues with your post (respectfully):** Engage. Best social selling happens in the comments. "Great point — here's how we think about it..." builds credibility.
