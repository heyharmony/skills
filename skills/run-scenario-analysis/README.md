# Run Scenario Analysis

## What It Does

This skill stress-tests strategic decisions by modeling best, worst, and likely scenarios. It captures the assumptions and risks discussed in leadership meetings, builds structured scenario models with financial and operational implications, and identifies trigger points that should activate contingency plans.

Instead of hoping your plan works, you model what happens if it doesn't—and what you'll do when those scenarios unfold.

## Why You Need It

Big decisions get debated across multiple meetings but rarely get stress-tested:

- "What if we lose that enterprise deal?"
- "What if the competitor launches first?"
- "What if we can't hire fast enough?"

These "what-ifs" surface in conversation but get lost. Leadership makes the bet without knowing what could break it or what to do when it does.

## Who Should Use It

- **VPs and C-suite** making strategic bets (market expansion, pricing changes, headcount plans, product pivots)
- **Finance teams** preparing for board presentations and need to anticipate "what if" questions
- **Product leaders** stress-testing feature bets and adoption assumptions
- **Sales leaders** modeling scenarios around pipeline, hiring, and sales cycle length

## Quick Start

1. Gather recent leadership meeting notes (board memos, Slack, standup recordings) where assumptions were discussed
2. Pick one major decision to stress-test (e.g., "Should we expand into Europe?")
3. Extract the 3–5 core assumptions driving that decision (sales cycle, hiring velocity, regulatory timeline, etc.)
4. Run through the 8-step process to build best/base/worst scenarios
5. Identify trigger points (observable signals that tell you which scenario is unfolding)
6. Design contingency actions for each scenario
7. Present as a one-page decision brief + scenario models + trigger dashboard

## Example Output

For a Series B company deciding to enter a new market, you'll get:
- **Decision Brief**: "We recommend European expansion because [reason]. Best case: $800k Year 2 ARR. Base case: $200k. Worst case: $50k. We monitor [triggers] and activate [contingency]."
- **Scenario Models**: One for each of best/base/worst with narrative, financials, and operational impact
- **Trigger Dashboard**: "If pipeline < 3 conversations by May 31, we're in worst case. Activate partner-led entry instead."
- **Contingency Playbook**: "Week 1: assess partner viability. Week 2: contact 2–3 partners. Week 3: pause hiring beyond committed people. Month 2: launch partner-led entry."

## When to Use It

- Before major strategic investment (market expansion, new product line, acquisition offer)
- Annual or quarterly planning cycles
- When a key assumption changes (lost customer, competitor move, hiring market shifts)
- Before board presentations (get ahead of "what if" questions)
- Hiring plan stress-testing (model cash impact if you need to cut)
- Pricing or packaging change evaluation

## What You'll Need

- Recent leadership meeting notes, emails, or Slack discussions (source material for assumptions)
- Access to: financial models, sales pipeline data, hiring plans, customer retention data
- 90 minutes to model three scenarios
- Optional: Google Drive or Notion access to store and share scenario briefs

## Key Concepts

- **Assumptions**: The claims underlying your decision (e.g., "Enterprise sales cycle will be 9 months")
- **Scenarios**: Three plausible futures: best case (optimistic but realistic), base case (most likely), worst case (pessimistic but survivable)
- **Trigger Points**: Observable signals that tell you which scenario is unfolding (e.g., pipeline velocity, hiring velocity, regulatory approval timeline)
- **Contingencies**: Actions you'll take if a scenario unfolds, who decides, timeline, and cost

## Tips

- Focus on the 3 variables that move the needle most, not 15 variables with marginal impact
- Anchor base case to data (analyst research, past performance, historical benchmarks), not hope
- Make triggers measurable and trackable—not "if things are slow" but "if pipeline < $2M by April 15"
- Update scenarios quarterly as new data comes in
- Use this skill early in decision-making, not after you've already committed
