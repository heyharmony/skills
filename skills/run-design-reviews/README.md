# Run Design Reviews

> Quality is built in design reviews, not added afterwards. Structure them right and they transform how your team ships.

## What It Does

Design reviews are where work gets critiqued and improved before it ships. But without structure, they become either rubber stamps or flame wars. This skill teaches you how to run reviews that improve work without demoralizing designers. You'll learn to critique at the right fidelity for the design stage (don't pixel-perfect wireframes), use a framework that keeps feedback constructive ("I like / I wish / What if"), include engineers early so design doesn't conflict with feasibility, and document decisions so the next person understands your thinking. The output is a team that ships higher-quality work, maintains consistency through a design system, and builds shared standards for what "good" looks like.

## Who It's For

- **Design Lead managing a growing design team** — You have 3-5 designers and need them to ship consistently good work. Weekly reviews calibrate quality standards. Pain: inconsistent quality, designers operating in silos, rework because of conflicts.

- **VP Product who reviews design** — You see design before it ships, but reviews are happening ad-hoc in Slack and 1-on-1s. Structured reviews centralize decisions. Pain: design decisions made piecemeal, inconsistency, no documented rationale.

- **Engineering Manager** — Your designers ship work that's beautiful but can't be built in the timeline. Engineering in design reviews prevents surprises. Pain: design-engineering friction, rework, missed handoffs.

- **Junior Designer** — You're new and learning how good design gets made. Design reviews show you what the bar is and how senior designers think. Pain: unclear what "good" looks like, feedback feels random.

- **Design System Owner** — You're building or maintaining a component library. Design reviews are where you enforce and improve it. Pain: designers not using components, inconsistent patterns, system erosion.

- **Product Manager** — You're shipping features and want to ensure design is solving the problem, not just looking good. Design reviews are where you validate that approach. Pain: beautiful designs that don't solve customer problems.

## Use Cases

- **You're onboarding a new designer** — New designer has different aesthetic preferences than the team's direction. Run design reviews to show them what "good" looks like, calibrate taste. Arrow: designer learns team standards in 3-4 weeks instead of 6 months of feedback.

- **Design quality has drifted over last quarter** — You have inconsistent button styles, components are being created instead of reused, UX patterns are fragmented. Weekly design reviews catch drift immediately. Arrow: in 4 weeks, new work follows standards consistently, design system adoption improves to 85%+.

- **Engineering says "this can't be built"** — Design ships to engineering and they say it requires 2x the time or won't work on mobile. Include engineers in design reviews. Arrow: design and engineering collaborate from the start, no surprises at handoff.

- **You're shipping a complex feature with lots of interactions** — Multi-step flows, conditional states, edge cases. Review helps identify issues before development starts. Arrow: feature ships with fewer UX edge case bugs, users have clearer mental model.

- **Multiple designers are shipping inconsistent work** — Button styles vary, error messaging differs, interaction patterns are fragmented. Design reviews enforce consistency. Arrow: users have consistent experience across product, component library is the source of truth.

- **You want to build a stronger design culture** — Design decisions are made in hallways instead of documented. Design reviews create a shared language and build team muscle. Arrow: team ships faster and more confidently because decisions are collaborative and documented.

## Connectors

Works standalone, but richer with **Figma** (design files and version history), **Slack** (async prep and feedback capture), **GitHub** (engineering feasibility perspective), and **Zoom** (recorded reviews for asynchronous feedback and future calibration).

## Setup

Establish a weekly 45-minute design review at a consistent time. Invite product, design, engineering, and design system owner. Create a Slack channel where designs get posted 24 hours before review (async prep). Set team agreement: critique the work, not the person. Use "I like / I wish / What if" framework for feedback. Expect the first month to feel slow as the team learns the structure. By month two, reviews will accelerate quality and reduce rework significantly. Document all decision rationale in Figma files or a shared wiki. The facilitator role matters — they keep discussion focused, prevent nitpicking, ensure engineers are heard, and document decisions. Pick someone trusted by the team for this role.

## What You Get

- **Consistent design quality across the team** — All designers understand what "good" looks like and ship to the same bar

- **Early feasibility feedback** — Engineers spot impossible requirements during review, not after design ships

- **Documented design rationale** — You know why each design choice was made, saving time when revisiting decisions later

- **Stronger design system adoption** — Reviewing against components encourages reuse instead of creating variants

- **Cross-functional collaboration** — Product, design, and engineering are aligned on approach before development starts

- **Faster onboarding for new designers** — New team members see how decisions are made and learn standards quickly

- **Improved UX consistency** — Users experience consistent interaction patterns, reducing cognitive load

- **Reduced rework cycles** — Issues are caught in review instead of after customer feedback

## Key Insight

The best design teams don't hire more designers — they improve their review process. Good reviews take 45 minutes and prevent 2 weeks of rework. Poor reviews take no time and create 2 weeks of pain. The structure matters: reviewing at the right fidelity, including engineers early, using constructive feedback frameworks, and documenting decisions. When your team understands that design reviews are about making work stronger, not about personal critique, they embrace them. Quality compounds over time as designers see their work get better and the team's standards rise together. The companies with the most polished products aren't those with the most talented designers — they're the ones with the best review processes.
