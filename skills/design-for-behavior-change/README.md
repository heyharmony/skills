# Design for Behavior Change

> Products don't succeed by being feature-rich. They succeed by changing what users do. Design for behavior first, features second.

## What It Does

This skill teaches you to apply behavioral science to product design. Using the Fogg Behavior Model, you identify what behavior you want to change, ensure motivation is strong enough to matter, reduce friction to nearly zero, and design prompts that turn actions into habits. You learn to leverage social proof, strategic defaults, and variable rewards to amplify adoption. Most products invest in motivation (marketing the benefit) but ignore ability (friction) and prompts (habit loops). This skill fixes that imbalance. You'll learn to audit the critical path from first use to habit formation, eliminate unnecessary steps, and design the environment so the desired behavior becomes automatic instead of requiring willpower.

## Who It's For

- **Product managers designing features**: You can't measure success by feature completeness. You measure it by behavior change. Design backwards from behavior.
- **Growth and engagement teams**: Onboarding, retention, and habit formation are behavioral challenges, not marketing challenges.
- **Designers and interaction designers**: Friction lives in every step. This skill teaches you where to cut and where to clarify.
- **Founders launching new products**: Your first weeks matter enormously for habit formation. Get the critical path right, or users never return.
- **Customer success and support teams**: You see where users drop off. This skill gives you language to explain why and how to fix it.
- **Conversion rate optimization teams** focused on moving users through funnels
- **Anyone building social or community products** relying on user behavior and habit loops

## Use Cases

- **Feature adoption is stalled** → Map motivation, ability, and prompts. Usually ability (friction) is the culprit, not motivation
- **Onboarding completion is low** → Audit the critical path. Count steps. Eliminate everything that's not absolutely necessary
- **Users churn because they never develop a habit** → Design a weekly prompt and social proof layer so returning becomes automatic
- **You want to change a low-leverage user behavior into a habit** → Add variable rewards (surprise elements) so the behavior becomes compelling
- **Conversion rate is stuck at 5%** → Every step of the funnel has friction. Cut form fields, reduce decisions, make next step obvious
- **You need to increase an existing behavior without adding features** → Reduce friction on the critical path and redesign your prompts, maybe without changing the product at all
- **Users say they want a feature but adoption stays low** → Map the behavior more carefully: the feature exists, but friction or prompts prevent habit formation

## Setup

1. Define the desired behavior clearly (not the feature, the action users will take). Be specific: "submit a report" not "use the analytics dashboard".
2. Audit motivation: do users believe the benefit? Test with 10 target users. If they don't see the benefit, no friction reduction will help.
3. Audit friction: count the steps, test with a new user, time them. What's the critical path? Can you eliminate 50% of the steps?
4. Design ability: eliminate Category B friction (optional steps before the core behavior). Don't require email verification before core action.
5. Design the prompt: what reminds users to do this? When do they return? Build habit loops so the behavior becomes automatic.
6. Add social proof: show that others are doing this behavior. Social signals amplify adoption when friction is low.

## What You Get

- **Higher adoption rates**: Users adopt faster when friction is eliminated before motivation is marketed. Your features work out of the box.
- **Stronger habit formation**: Users who develop a habit become stable, predictable, and drive word-of-mouth. Habit users are your best marketing.
- **Better retention**: Churn drops when users develop a behavior routine, not when you add more features. Retention becomes sustainable.
- **More reliable product roadmap**: You know what drives behavior. You can predict which features will matter and which won't. Roadmap bets improve.
- **Clearer design decisions**: Stop debating features. Debate whether a design change will increase behavior adoption. Decisions become objective.
- **Sustainable engagement**: You've built a product that works because of how it's designed, not how hard you market it. Marketing amplifies what's already working.
- **A behavior audit framework**: A repeatable process for analyzing any feature through the lens of motivation, ability, and prompts. Use this for every launch

## Key Insight

Friction is the difference between "I want to do this" and "I actually do this." Most products fail not because people don't want the benefit, but because getting there requires too many steps, too many decisions, or too much effort. Eliminate 80% of the friction, and adoption becomes automatic. The goal is to design a product where the path of least resistance IS the behavior you want.

## Pro Tips

- Count steps like a user would experience them, not like an engineer would count them. Every field, every decision, every page is a step.
- Test friction with a new user, not with someone who's used the product before. Experienced users don't feel friction that onboards haven't learned to ignore.
- Variable rewards (surprise, delight) are powerful habit-forming tools. But they only work if friction is low. Don't add variables rewards to high-friction workflows.
