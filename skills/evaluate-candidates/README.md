# Evaluate Candidates

> Reference checks beat interviews. Strengths beat absence of weaknesses. Structure beats gut.

## What It Does

Synthesize interview data, reference checks, and work samples into a candidate ranking that separates signal from noise. Weight different information sources correctly (reference checks carry more signal than interviews), identify world-class strengths vs. generic competency, and avoid hiring mistakes driven by urgency, pedigree, or overweighting one interview perspective. Creates structured comparison framework so hiring committees decide on data, not opinions.

## Who It's For

- **Hiring managers** — consolidating feedback from multiple interviewers into a decision before groupthink sets in
- **Hiring committees** — need to compare candidates fairly when stakeholders have different opinions
- **Startup founders** — making early-team hires where mistakes compound; need discipline to override urgency bias
- **Technical leaders** — evaluating whether candidates' strength areas match your specific gap
- **People & HR partners** — coordinating multi-candidate evaluations and building hiring rubrics that stick
- **Growth-stage leaders** — scaling team and need to identify unicorns vs. specialists vs. developing talent

## Use Cases

**After interview rounds, before references** → Map which 2-3 candidates are worth deep reference investment; avoid wasting references on maybes → top candidates enter reference phase with confidence

**Reference check synthesis** → Call references with behavioral questions, compare against interview scores, look for pattern consistency → discover what interviews hid or over-emphasized

**Tie-breaker decisions between finalists** → Candidate A is technically stronger, Candidate B is a better cultural fit; use your team gap analysis to decide which matters more → hiring becomes clear instead of coin-flip

**Building candidate comparison matrix** → Side-by-side strengths, reference validation, work trial performance, and team gap impact → stakeholders see data instead of arguing opinions

**Pre-offer Bar Raiser review** → Bring in outside perspective to sanity-check before committing → prevents hiring mistakes that feel obvious in hindsight

**Post-hire recalibration** → Compare actual performance to hiring assessment; learn which signals truly predicted success → improve future hiring decisions

## Setup

1. **Create your evaluation rubric** — 4-5 core competencies specific to this role (not generic)
2. **Separate interview scores by interviewer** — see patterns and outliers
3. **Schedule reference calls early** — don't wait until after offer
4. **Prepare work trial or case study** — if feasible for your role; real work reveals what interviews hide
5. **Build comparison matrix** — columns = candidates, rows = competencies; populate with scores + evidence
6. **Identify Bar Raiser** — outside reviewer with veto power for final sanity check
7. **Weight your signals** — Decide before evaluating: how much do interviews count vs. references vs. work samples?
8. **Prepare stakeholder debrief** — Schedule hiring committee meeting before making final offer

## Key Insights

**Reference checks trump interviews.** Years of working together beat 45 minutes of performing. Invest time in reference calls; they carry signal interviews cannot.

**Unicorns don't exist; strengths do.** Stop looking for people good at everything. Identify world-class strength in what you actually need and accept gaps elsewhere.

**Urgency kills judgment.** Most bad hires happen when you need someone yesterday. Structured evaluation forces you past urgency and into clear thinking.

**Paid work trials reveal reality.** How candidates perform under real stakes (real project, real feedback, real constraints) is more predictive than interview brilliance. If possible, use them.

**Disagreement is valuable.** When co-interviewers disagree, that's data. Don't average the scores; investigate the difference. One interviewer may have seen something important.
