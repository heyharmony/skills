# Plan Change Rollout

## Overview

A structured change management skill for Harmony that helps teams design people-centered rollouts for organizational change. Rather than treating change as a technical project, this skill mines meeting sentiment to uncover resistance, maps stakeholders by influence and support, and builds phased rollout plans grounded in how people actually react.

## What This Skill Does

- **Mines sentiment signals** from Harmony meeting transcripts around announced changes
- **Maps stakeholders** on a 2×2 matrix (influence × support) to identify champions and resistance pockets
- **Identifies root causes** of resistance — loss of control, uncertainty, workload, values conflicts
- **Designs targeted communication** for different stakeholder groups
- **Builds training plans** tailored to who needs what and when
- **Sets adoption milestones** with clear leading and lagging indicators
- **Creates feedback loops** so people feel heard throughout the rollout
- **Plans review checkpoints** at 30/60/90 days with go/no-go decision points

## When to Use

Use this skill when rolling out:
- New tools or platforms (CRM, communication, project management)
- Organizational restructures or leadership transitions
- Process changes (workflows, approval chains, documentation)
- Policy changes (remote work, compensation, benefits)
- Post-acquisition team integration

## Key Outputs

- Change Brief (what, why, who, timeline, success criteria)
- Stakeholder Map and Resistance Analysis
- Tailored Communication Plan by audience
- Training Plan with timelines and delivery methods
- Adoption Milestones Dashboard (30/60/90 targets)
- Feedback Mechanism and Review Schedule

## Data Sources

Connects to Slack, Notion, Asana, Google Calendar, and Gmail to gather context on the change, monitor sentiment, track adoption, and coordinate rollout communication.

## Why It Works

Change fails because organizations skip the people side. This skill brings the human element front and center — listening to what people really think in meetings, building champions, addressing specific resistance concerns, and measuring what matters (adoption, not just completion).
