# Build Team Culture

> Culture isn't a poster on the wall—it's what happens when the boss isn't around. Surface your team's actual culture, reflect it back, and make it visible to everyone.

## What It Does

Stop inventing aspirational values that your team doesn't practice. This skill excavates the *actual* culture your team lives—through meeting patterns, communication norms, decision-making rituals, and peer feedback—then structures it into a living document. Rather than imposing culture from the top, you articulate what's already there, make it explicit for distributed and new hires, and identify which norms you want to protect or challenge as you scale.

## Who It's For

- **VPs and Engineering Leaders** scaling teams and onboarding new members who need to absorb culture quickly, not through osmosis.
- **Operations Leaders and People Ops** trying to make culture explicit and consistent across growing organizations.
- **Founders and Leadership Teams** who sense a culture drift or gap between stated values and actual behavior.
- **First-time Managers** leading distributed teams who need to make culture visible for remote people.
- **Organizational Development / Change Leaders** navigating transitions (new exec, acquisition, funding round) who need to understand existing culture before shifting it.
- **HR and Recruiting** who want new hires to know "what good looks like here" from day one, not month three.

### Pain Points They Face

- New hires can't absorb culture informally; existing culture stays implicit and inconsistent.
- Leadership talks about values that the org doesn't actually practice.
- Distributed teams experience culture differently; remote people feel excluded from norms.
- As you scale, informal culture breaks down; new people don't know "how we do things here."
- Lack of shared culture language makes conflicts harder to resolve—people don't understand trade-offs.
- Culture becomes a bottleneck: "We've always done it this way" blocks necessary evolution.

## Use Cases

- **After you scale 2–3x** → Document what you've been doing implicitly before new hires dilute it.
- **New exec or manager arrives** → Surface decision-making norms and what "good" looks like on this team so they can lead with alignment, not against tradition.
- **Distributed team onboarding struggles** → Remote hires can't learn culture in hallways; make it an onboarding artifact.
- **You sense culture mismatch** → Leadership says "we move fast," but decision-making is slow; document reality to address it.
- **Preparing for funding or acquisition** → Investors and new stakeholders want to understand (and potentially shift) your culture; show what you actually have.
- **Trust has broken** → After conflict or misalignment, rebuild by documenting psychological safety norms and decision processes explicitly.

## Connectors

Works standalone with qualitative interviews and observation, but richer with:

- **Slack**: Reveals tone, what topics get amplified, norms around dissent and feedback.
- **Google Calendar**: Maps sacred meetings, who convenes decisions, actual vs. stated meeting culture.
- **GitHub**: Shows code review culture, merge cadence, and collaboration norms.
- **Notion**: Surfaces documented practices, informal handbooks, and emerging team standards.
- **Google Drive**: Reveals shared templates, decision frameworks, and what practices are maintained.

## Setup

1. Schedule a 60–90 minute culture listening session with leadership and 3–5 senior ICs.
2. Interview 3–5 people individually (15 min each) about observed norms and rewarded behaviors.
3. Review communication and collaboration patterns from available tools (Slack, Calendar, GitHub).
4. Synthesize findings into a 500–800 word culture document (4 key dimensions + narrative).
5. Circulate for feedback; hold a reflection meeting to resolve and decide on ownership.
6. Hardwire into onboarding; make managers reference it in week-one conversations.
7. Revisit quarterly as culture evolves.

## What You Get

- **A documented culture** grounded in observed behavior, not aspirational slogans—honest about who you are, not who you think you should be.
- **Onboarding clarity** for new hires, distributed teams, and leaders joining the org.
- **A language for culture** (decision-making, communication style, shipping rhythm, psychological safety) so conflicts and evolution can be discussed explicitly.
- **Sacred cow inventory** naming practices worth protecting and norms that may no longer serve you.
- **Living artifact** that scales with your team, revisited quarterly to stay aligned with how you actually operate.
