## Purpose

Most people negotiate salary and forget the conditions that actually determine success. Compensation is often a symptom: "I'm underpaid" usually means "I don't feel respected" or "I'm not learning." This skill flips the order. You negotiate for success factors first (clear role scope, budget, team size, learning opportunities, autonomy), then money becomes the easy part of the conversation.

The goal is to move from adversarial ("I want X") to collaborative ("Here's what I need to succeed; are you open to discussing it?").

## When to Use This Skill

- **You have a job offer** — Before you accept, understand what's negotiable and what isn't
- **You're discussing a raise or promotion** — Come with data, not feelings; frame it as solving their problem
- **You're moving to contract or freelance work** — Terms matter more than you think; flexibility is a negotiation point
- **Joining a startup where equity is offered** — Know what equity is actually worth (likely nothing) and negotiate accordingly
- **Switching companies** — Your leverage is highest before you accept; use it strategically
- **Renegotiating after a year** — Market conditions change; your value has increased; frame the ask
- **Joining in a recession or slow period** — Other levers exist when salary is frozen; flexibility, title, scope, growth opportunity

## Instructions

**1. Before any negotiation, clarify what success actually means to you**
   - Money is rarely the real issue. What are you actually optimizing for?
     - Respect? Autonomy? Learning? Growth velocity? Flexibility? Health? Sanity?
   - Be honest. "I want $200K" might be code for "I don't feel valued." That's a different conversation.
   - The best negotiation handles the real thing, not the symptom.

**2. Map "success factors" before discussing salary**
   - Identify 5–7 non-monetary things that determine whether you'll thrive:
     - Role clarity: Is the job clear or am I figuring it out?
     - Team quality: Who am I working with? Manager competence?
     - Scope and autonomy: Can I make decisions or do I need approval for everything?
     - Learning: What will I master in this role? What access do I have?
     - Growth path: What comes after this role? Is it clear?
     - Resources: Do I have headcount, budget, tools to succeed?
     - Work-life fit: Pace, hours, flexibility, location?
   - For each: What does "good" look like? What would be dealbreaker-level bad?

**3. Research actual market value (not what you wish, what you're worth)**
   - Use: Levels.fyi, Blind, Glassdoor, Radford, Salary.com (multiple sources)
   - Talk to: Recruiters, people in similar roles, mentors who've negotiated recently
   - Know: Industry standard, company-size standard (Series B pays different than Series D), geography, role level
   - Be specific: "Senior PM at Series B enterprise software in SF" is more useful than "PM salary"
   - If you're underpaid by market, that's your leverage. Use it.

**4. Understand what you're actually negotiating**
   - Base salary: Fixed, immediate, no risk
   - Bonus: Often fantasy; assume 50% of stated to be safe
   - Equity: Likely worth $0 (startup), sometimes meaningful (public company, late-stage private)
   - Sign-on bonus: Covers losses from old job; counts as cash
   - Options: Understand vesting, strike price, probability of liquidity
   - Remote/flexibility: Huge factor for quality of life; often traded for salary
   - Title: Doesn't matter for day job; matters for future jobs; negotiate clearly
   - Benefits: Health insurance, 401k match, parental leave, unlimited PTO (check if it's real)
   - Vacation/sabbatical: In writing, not policy

**5. Identify your actual leverage before negotiating**
   - Do you have competing offers? (Gold standard leverage)
   - Is there a tight labor market for your skill? (Market leverage)
   - Are you replacing someone who left? (Timing leverage)
   - Are you solving an urgent problem for them? (Problem leverage)
   - Can they replace you easily or would it take 3 months? (Replacement cost)
   - What's the cost if you say no? (Their calculation)
   - No leverage is OK. You can still negotiate. But be strategic.

**6. Create your negotiation frame (not demand, question)**
   - Frame as collaboration: "Before I accept, I want to make sure we're set up for success."
   - Ask questions instead of making demands:
     - "Are you open to discussing [X]?" (Gives them an out that isn't "no")
     - "What flexibility do we have on [Y]?"
     - "How do we handle [Z]?" (Assumes it's negotiable)
   - Avoid: "I want," "I need," "I demand," "That's not enough"
   - Avoid email: Live conversation is 87% more likely to succeed. Phone, video, or in-person.

**7. Separate the negotiation into rounds (don't ask for everything at once)**
   - Round 1: Success factors (role, scope, team, learning)
   - Round 2: Compensation structure (base, bonus, equity, sign-on)
   - Round 3: Details (title, start date, flexibility, benefits)
   - Don't present as a list. Have a conversation. Listen to their constraints.

**8. Negotiate for things that are hard to change later**
   - Easy to change: Salary (can go up in 6 months if you're crushing it)
   - Hard to change: Team, scope, learning path, manager, location policy
   - Almost impossible to change: Equity (vesting is locked in)
   - Prioritize: Get the right team and scope. Salary will follow good work.

**9. Build an alternative if they say no**
   - "I understand. Here's what would work for me: [lower ask that's still meaningful]"
   - Or: "Let's revisit in 6 months. I'll prove the value. Then we adjust."
   - Or: "What if we did X instead of Y?" (Trade salary for flexibility, for example)
   - Never walk away empty. There's always a deal if both sides want it.

**10. When they counter, don't immediately accept or reject**
   - "Let me think about it and get back to you" (gives you space)
   - Sleep on it. Talk to mentors. Check if it actually solves the problem you identified.
   - Don't negotiate just to negotiate. If the counter addresses your success factors, take the win.
   - If it doesn't, come back with: "I appreciate the offer. I'm still concerned about [original issue]. How do we handle that?"

• What "good" looks like: VP Product is thoughtful, gives you space, gives you feedback, and advocates for you internally. They're someone you'd learn from.

• Negotiation approach: "I'd love a chance to talk with [VP] about management approach before I start. What does good collaboration look like? What do you expect in first 30 days?"

Success Factor 3: Team Quality
• What "good" looks like: You have 1–2 other PMs you'll collaborate with (not siloed). Engineering counterpart is strong and trusted. You have design and data support. Team isn't burned out.
• Current offer status: You know there's a senior engineer on the team; you don't know the other PMs or if anyone's burned out from rapid growth.

• What "good" looks like: There's a clear path to either (a) Director of Product in 18–24 months, or (b) Principal PM track. You'll learn go-to-market, org design, and how to work at scale.
• Current offer status: Not discussed. Job posting says "growth opportunity" but that's vague.
• Your ask: "What does success look like in year 1? What would growth to [next level] require?"
• Negotiation approach: Raise in 1:1 with VP: "I'm thinking about [Director] track long-term. What does that look like here? What should I focus on in the first year to set myself up?"

Success Factor 5: Decision-Making Speed & Autonomy
• What "good" looks like: You can make decisions without 5 layers of approval. You have budget to spend on experiments. You move fast.
• Current offer status: Unknown. Series B often has faster decision-making than enterprise, but if the company is growing and adding layers, it could slow down.
• Your ask: "How fast do decisions happen? Can I greenlight experiments without approval, or does it go to exec team?"
• Negotiation approach: Raise in VP conversation: "I'm used to moving fast. How much autonomy do I have to experiment and iterate?"

Success Factor 6: Comp is Last
• What "good" looks like: Competitive with market; equity is real enough to care (company runway, traction); no shocking gap between your value and what they offered.
• Current offer status: See below.

**Round 1: Success Factors (Role, Team, Scope, Manager) — Do These First**

Your approach: "What does collaboration look like? How often do we sync? What are your expectations for me in first 30 days?"
What success looks like: VP describes thoughtful, hands-off-but-supportive approach. You feel confident in her.
If they're vague ("We'll figure it out"): Red flag. This is hard to fix. Ask more directly: "How do you mentor your PMs? What's an example of how you've helped someone grow?"

Success Factor to address: Team
Your approach: "I'd love to meet [engineer name], and I'd like to grab coffee with [PM from adjacent line] to understand how the teams work together. Can we set that up?"
What success looks like: You meet them, they seem solid, and you don't hear "everyone's burned out."

Your approach: "I'm used to moving fast—testing ideas, shipping incrementally, learning from users. How does that work here? Can I green-light experiments, or does everything go to the exec team?"
What success looks like: VP says "You have autonomy to experiment. If it's big [>$50K spend], you loop me in. Otherwise, go."
If they say "Every decision is collaborative/discussed with execs": This company moves slower. Is that OK for you?

Outcome of Round 1: You now understand if the role, manager, and team are good enough to join. If yes, proceed to Round 2. If no major red flags, continue.

You're in an email or call with recruiter or VP. You have competing offer intel (but don't lead with it).

Market data: $200K – $230K for this profile
Your ask: $210K base (10–15% bump)

→ That's $200K + $30K = $230K Year 1 (more realistic if bonus hits)
→ This is acceptable. Take it, unless you want to push on equity.

Equity negotiation:
• Current: 0.4%
• Market for senior PM at Series B: 0.6–1.0%
• Your ask: "Equity feels light at 0.4%. I'd like to see 0.75%."
• Their constraint: "Our cap table is tight. We can do 0.6% but not more." [Very typical response]
• Accept: 0.6% is fair. That's a win.

Alternatively, if they won't move on base or equity: "What about a sign-on bonus to cover my unvested equity at [previous company]? $25K would help bridge the transition."

Final comp agreement: $200K base + 15% bonus + 0.6% equity + $25K sign-on = Realistic Year 1 of ~$225K + upside on equity

• If base doesn't move above $200K AND equity doesn't move above 0.5% AND no sign-on = too low relative to market. Walk.
• If manager seems disorganized or unsupportive = hard to overcome. Walk.
• If product is truly peripheral = scope isn't there. Walk.

## Edge Cases

- **You don't have competing offers** — You still have leverage (market conditions, skill scarcity, their timeline). Use it. Say "I'm comparing this with market data" not "I have offers." Usually true enough.
- **They say "take it or leave it"** — This usually means they're negotiating with multiple people and afraid of losing you if they move. Call their bluff gently: "I want to work here. Let's figure this out." Usually they'll move.
- **You're joining a startup with massive equity** — Equity is worth $0 unless they get funding and exit. Never optimize for "potential" equity. Optimize for fair cash salary now. Equity is bonus.
- **Role sounds vague or not well-defined** — This is your red flag. Don't negotiate comp for a job you don't understand. Negotiate for clarity first. A bad role with great comp is still a bad role.
- **Company is in crisis or fundraising** — Comp might be frozen. Negotiate for other things: title, role, timeline, growth, learning, flexibility. Salary can come after stability returns.
- **You're moving from a much lower-paying job** — Don't anchor on your old salary. Use market data. "I was paid $120K, so $180K is a huge jump for me!" is not leverage. Market pays $200K—that's the anchor.
- **They ask you for your salary history** — In many states, illegal. Avoid it. "I'd rather focus on market value for this role than my history." If they press: "My last comp was X, but I'm basing my ask on market data for this position, which is Y."
