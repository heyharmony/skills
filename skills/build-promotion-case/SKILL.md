## Purpose

A promotion case is not a plea—it's evidence. This skill helps you build a compelling narrative around what you've accomplished, the problems you've solved, and how you've already demonstrated competence at the next level. Rather than asking for promotion, you're simply documenting the work that speaks for itself.

The goal is to reframe the conversation from "I deserve this" to "I'm already doing this—let's just update the title."

## When to Use This Skill

- **Performance review cycles** — Before your manager evaluates you, have your case ready
- **After a major win** — Momentum matters; capture it immediately after shipping something significant
- **When visibility is lacking** — You've done great work but leadership hasn't seen it; this becomes your case
- **Preparing for sponsor conversations** — Internal advocates need ammunition; give it to them
- **Role misalignment** — You've outgrown your title; document what your actual job has become
- **Transition planning** — Moving teams, functions, or seniority levels; make the bridge clear
- **Compensation negotiation** — Impact directly supports equity, bonus, or salary discussions

## Instructions

**1. Inventory your impact across 3 dimensions**
   - Business outcomes (revenue, retention, growth metrics tied to your work)
   - Team leverage (scaled processes, mentoring others, created tools that multiplied team capacity)
   - Technical/strategic excellence (solved hard problems, mastered new domains, shaped direction)

**2. Extract concrete metrics and stories from available data**
   - Pull from transcripts if you have them: decisions you made, problems you solved, trade-offs you navigated
   - Check Slack, email, or documentation for: features shipped, bugs prevented, cycles saved, revenue influenced
   - Identify moments where you acted at higher scope: cross-functional leadership, budget decisions, hiring/mentoring

**3. Build a "next level" evidence map**
   - List 4–6 situations where you already performed the role you're seeking
   - For each: what was the problem → what did you do → what was the outcome
   - Be specific: "Reduced onboarding time" becomes "Designed new IC onboarding doc; improved time-to-first-contribution from 4 weeks to 2 weeks"

**4. Diagnose why you haven't been promoted yet**
   - Lack of advocacy: Do influencers know your work? Do decision-makers see it?
   - Role doesn't exist: Are you doing the job but the level doesn't exist? Advocate for the level itself
   - Impatience: Have you actually been performing at the next level for 6+ months?
   - Development gap: Is there a concrete skill or pattern you need to demonstrate first?

**5. Craft the visibility strategy**
   - Weekly updates: Make progress visible to manager and skip-level
   - Project debuts: Socialize wins beyond your immediate team
   - Speaking/writing: Document what you've learned; signal authority
   - Mentoring visibility: Demonstrate you've scaled impact beyond yourself

**6. Write the narrative (not the ask)**
   - Start with business context: "Here's what the team needed"
   - Middle with your actions: "Here's how I stepped up"
   - End with sustained results: "Here's what we've done since then"
   - Omit: "I think I deserve"; include: "This is what I've actually been doing"

**7. Prepare talking points for conversation**
   - Have 2–3 stories where you demonstrated judgment, scope, and impact
   - Know how your work connects to company strategy
   - Have a candidate response if someone says "I don't see you at that level yet"—ask what blockers exist and how to remove them

## Example

```
PROMOTION CASE NARRATIVE

[CONTEXT]
Department: Product @ TechVenture (Series B SaaS)
Role Timeline: July 2023 → March 2026 (~2.5 years)
Manager: Sarah Chen
Current Level: Senior Product Manager
Seeking: Principal Product Manager

---

[IMPACT AT CURRENT LEVEL]

Business Outcomes:
• $2.8M ARR: Led the Analytics Suite product line from zero to 18% of company revenue
  - Indicator: Measured through monthly recurring revenue reports and customer cohort analysis
• 42% net revenue retention: Built scalability playbook adopted across product org
  - Indicator: Tracked via retention dashboards; playbook now used for 3 other product lines

Team Leverage:
• Mentored 5 APMs: Two promoted to Senior PM, three are promotion-track with active development
  - Adoption: Weekly 1:1 learning framework, documented decision-making template used across team
• Created "Go-to-Market Ops Playbook": Documented launch sequence now used by 6 teams
  - Adoption: 80+ launches in past 18 months; reduced time-to-market by 23%

Technical/Strategic Contribution:
• Architected product strategy for enterprise motion: Designed land-and-expand model
  - Downstream impact: Enterprise ARR grew 156% YoY; shaped how sales and success teams operate

---

[NEXT LEVEL EVIDENCE]

Performance at Next Level - Situations Where I've Already Done The Work:

1. Navigating the "Pricing Inflection" Dilemma (Q4 2024)
   - Problem: Analytics Suite was underpriced relative to value; raising prices risked churn; sales wanted lower prices to close faster
   - My approach: Built economic model showing price elasticity; led cross-functional alignment (finance, sales, customer success) with data; tested price tiers with 5 strategic accounts first; created customer communication strategy for existing base
   - Outcome: Implemented tiered pricing; only 2% churn (vs. feared 8%); ASP increased 31%; revenue impact $320K additional ARR in 6 months
   - Why this was "next level": Required navigating competing org incentives, quantifying soft variables (customer satisfaction vs. revenue), making the call when data was ambiguous, taking on accountability across functions

2. Building a Cross-Product Roadmap (Q2 2024)
   - Problem: Product org had 6 lines running independently; customer feedback overlapped; engineering was confused about priorities
   - My approach: Convened the PM team (my first time in explicit leadership capacity); designed discovery sessions; synthesized overlaps into shared platform needs; presented integrated roadmap to exec team with both individual line and platform view
   - Outcome: Platform work became visible; 3 engineering teams realigned; Q3 shipped 2 platform features that unlocked features across all lines; engineering velocity improved 18%
   - Why this was "next level": Required systems thinking beyond my own product line, influencing without authority, making others' work better, taking on implicit org design responsibilities

3. Closing the "Gaps in Coverage" Crisis (Q1 2025)
   - Problem: Two major customers (combined $400K ARR) complained they were "falling through the cracks" between sales, CS, and product; competitive risk
   - My approach: Instead of finger-pointing, I designed a "Lifecycle Ownership Model" for each customer segment; mapped accountabilities; created playbook; led training with sales and CS; built feedback loops
   - Outcome: Both customers renewed with expansion (net +$150K ARR); framework adopted for all enterprise accounts; customer satisfaction improved 34 points
   - Why this was "next level": Took ownership of a cross-org systemic problem rather than staying in "my lane"; solved for organizational misalignment, not just product features

4. Advocating for "Unpopular But Right" Decision (Q3 2024)
   - Problem: Sales wanted us to bolt on feature requests that didn't fit product strategy; we had said "no" multiple times; political pressure mounting
   - My approach: Built alignment with CEO on product principles; made the case economically (feature bloat costs more than it earned); offered alternative (domain-specific integration path); communicated reasoning to sales in their language (pipeline impact, customer lifetime value, competitive moat)
   - Outcome: Sales accepted the framework; we built a more scalable solution; customer acquisition cost improved; the alternative path unlocked 3 logos sales couldn't have closed otherwise
   - Why this was "next level": Required strategic conviction, cross-org influence without authority, making the hard call, taking pressure and staying grounded

5. Stepping In as Interim Director (Q4 2024 – 6 weeks)
   - Problem: Product organization director went on leave; three PMs needed coordination; roadmap was mid-flight
   - My approach: Stepped in without title change; held sync meetings, cleared blockers, maintained team morale, shipped on time; took on hiring interviews for two new APM roles
   - Outcome: All committed deliverables shipped; team felt supported (morale survey +12 points); two high-quality APM hires (both still strong performers)
   - Why this was "next level": Demonstrated I can hold a larger org together, make trade-off decisions at higher altitudes, handle ambiguity without title

---

[BLOCKERS & REMOVAL PLAN]

Potential Concern: "Principal PMs often come from outside. Why promote internally?"
Response: I've proven I can operate across silos and influence the whole org. That's actually harder than owning a single domain. The playbooks and PM culture I've built are part of what makes this team function. Institutional knowledge + capability is rare.

Potential Concern: "You haven't led a team formally yet."
Response: Fair. I've acted as informal leader (mentorship, roadmap alignment, process design). I'm actively seeking formal team lead opportunities. But many Principal tracks don't require line management—they reward strategic impact and thought leadership. I'm building that now.

Potential Concern: "You've been here 2.5 years. Usually we wait until year 3."
Response: I've operated at this level for 6+ months across multiple dimensions. Happy to continue proving it, but the work is already happening. Let's discuss what would accelerate this if timing is the blocker.

---

[VISIBILITY & MOMENTUM]

Over Next 60 Days, I'll:
1. Ship "Analytics Integration API": Unlock customer self-service; reduces sales cycles by 2 weeks
2. Publish "How We Built a $2.8M Product Line" internally: Capture playbook in shareable format; position as thought leader
3. Lead cross-product "Pricing & Unit Economics Workshop": Teach framework to all PMs; demonstrate leadership on strategic topics

---

[TALKING POINTS - Stories]

If asked "How do I know you're ready?":
→ "When pricing felt ambiguous, I built the model to remove the ambiguity. When roadmap alignment broke, I fixed the system rather than complaining about it. When customers were slipping through cracks, I took ownership. Each time, the outcome was bigger than the problem I was handed. That's what 'next level' looks like."

If asked "What's one area to develop?":
→ "I want to develop deeper financial acumen. I've done pricing work, but I want to understand unit economics and capital allocation more systematically. I'm reading [book], sitting in on finance meetings with Sarah, and I'd love to own a P&L on a new product line to deepen that muscle."

If told "You're not at that level yet":
→ "I appreciate that. I'd like to understand what 'at that level' looks like from your perspective. Is it scope? Impact? A specific skill? Let's make a plan. I know what I need to prove."
```

## Edge Cases

- **You work in a role without clear progression** — Map to adjacent roles at next level (IC → PM, PM → Director). Build a case for what the role should be, then step into it.
- **Your team/company is contracting** — Expand your scope horizontally. Document impact beyond your literal responsibilities. Show you're doing the work even if the title won't change soon.
- **You're blocked by a manager who doesn't advocate** — Build relationships with skip-level and other influential leaders. Make your work visible through skip-level syncs, cross-org projects, and public wins. Your manager isn't the only voice.
- **You're new to the level and didn't come up through the ranks** — Document everything early. Get explicit feedback on what "success at this level" looks like. Over-communicate impact in first 6 months.
- **You're in a rapidly growing company where titles move slowly** — Focus on impact and compensation instead of title. The title will follow. Advocate for "Senior" equivalent roles or new levels if needed.
- **You keep getting "not yet" but don't understand why** — Ask for a specific rubric. Don't leave promotion conversations without clarity on what moves the needle next.
