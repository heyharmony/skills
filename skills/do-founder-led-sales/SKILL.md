## Purpose

Founders have an unfair advantage: you can change product, adjust pricing, commit to roadmap items, and make decisions on the spot. Your job isn't to sell—it's to do customer discovery that happens to close deals. The founder should close the first 10-20 customers personally because:

- **You learn what the market actually wants** — faster than any sales hire could discover it
- **You validate willingness to pay** — pricing becomes clear through conversations, not surveys
- **You understand objections** — you hear firsthand why people hesitate, not filtered through a sales team
- **You build the repeatable process** — once you've documented patterns across 10-20 customers, you have a playbook to hand to a future sales hire
- **You make relationships** — early customers become advocates, references, and advisors if the founder is involved

The mistake most founders make is hiring a sales person before they can describe the repeatable sales motion. Then the new hire has to invent it from scratch.

## When to Use This Skill

- **You haven't closed your first 5 customers yet** — you need to learn the market, not optimize the process
- **You can't articulate your sales process** — "We just close people" means it's not repeatable yet
- **Pricing feels uncertain** — you don't know if you should charge $100/month or $10K/month
- **Objections feel unpredictable** — if different customers raise different concerns, you haven't mapped the objection landscape
- **Your network has warm prospects** — your first 10 customers should come from warm intros, not cold outreach
- **You're weighing a sales hire** — before hiring, close 15 customers personally so you know what you're hiring for
- **Early-stage revenue is lumpy** — some months you close 2 deals, others zero; pattern isn't clear yet

## Instructions

1. **Build a prospect list from your network first**
   - Start with people who know you (or know people who know you)
   - Avoid cold outreach in Year 1; warm intros convert 5-10x better than cold
   - List potential customers who have the pain you're solving (even if they haven't asked for a solution yet)
   - Prioritize based on: (a) relationship strength, (b) pain relevance, (c) ability to afford your product
   - You need 15-20 qualified leads to close your first 5-10 customers (typical conversion is 30-50% from warm intros)

2. **Prepare your discovery framework**
   - Your job is to listen 70% and talk 30%
   - Open with: "I want to understand the problem you're facing" — not "Let me show you my product"
   - Ask about current state: "How do you solve this problem today? What's broken about that approach?"
   - Quantify the pain: "How much time does this waste? How much does it cost?"
   - Understand their process: "Who else is involved in this decision? How would they evaluate a solution?"
   - Never pitch before you've asked at least 8 discovery questions
   - Document what you learn in your CRM so you see patterns across calls

3. **Run the conversation like a discovery meeting, not a demo**
   - Start by saying: "I want to understand your world before we go into our product"
   - Ask about their goals, constraints, and decision process
   - Take notes visibly (they should know you're listening)
   - If they ask about features, answer briefly, then go back to questions
   - At the end, only show the product if it's clearly relevant; otherwise just schedule a follow-up
   - Your goal is clarity on fit, not amazement at features

4. **Price higher than you think and be willing to adjust**
   - Your first customer will tell you if your price is way off
   - Start with a number that feels uncomfortable; you can always lower it
   - Don't offer multiple tiers if you have no data; pick one price and learn
   - If 3 people ask "Can you do it for less?" you might be priced high
   - If nobody asks about price, you're priced too low
   - Pricing leverage comes from: exclusivity (limited spots), speed of implementation, or unique value
   - As you close more customers at the same price, your confidence in that price increases

5. **Handle objections as data collection, not rejection**
   - Every objection you hear is a pattern (usually)
   - "Your product doesn't integrate with our Salesforce" heard from 3 prospects? That's a top-10 objection
   - "It's too expensive" from a company doing $100M revenue is different from "It's too expensive" from a $5M startup
   - Create an objection log: objection, who raised it, your response, what you learned
   - Share your objection log with your team; this becomes the sales training playbook
   - Don't argue with objections; instead, ask: "What would need to be true for this to be a fit?"

6. **Position the product as founder-backed, not as finished**
   - Customers buying from a founder expect flexibility
   - Say: "You're one of our first 10 customers, which means we're going to build some custom stuff for you"
   - Use this as a closing tool: "Since you're an early customer, we can commit to X feature you need"
   - Document custom requests; they become product priorities or clear nos for the roadmap
   - Your founder advantage is making commitments to roadmap items; use it

7. **Close the deal by understanding the real objection**
   - If they hesitate, ask: "What would need to change for this to be a yes?"
   - Often the objection isn't price or features; it's "I don't want to be your beta customer" or "I need board approval"
   - Address the real blocker, not the stated one
   - For early customers, offer: shorter contract (3 months instead of 12), success guarantee (if you don't hit these metrics, you don't pay), or risk reversal
   - Don't discount; instead, offer different terms or scope

8. **Document every conversation and build your playbook**
   - After each call, spend 15 minutes documenting: prospect profile, objections raised, their pain quantified, how you responded, what worked/didn't
   - After 10 calls, you'll see patterns (common questions, objections, pain points)
   - After 20 calls, you can articulate your repeatable motion: "Here's how we typically approach discovery with [customer type]"
   - This document becomes the training guide for your first sales hire
   - Share it with your team so everyone learns from each conversation

9. **Manage founder fatigue—founder-led sales is temporary**
   - Founder-led sales is not scalable forever
   - You should be closing 2-3 deals per month
   - Once you hit 10-20 customers OR you're closing 5+ deals per month, it's time to hire
   - Don't optimize for speed yet; optimize for learning
   - If you're miserable on calls, hire faster; if you're learning rapidly, keep doing it

10. **Know when to stop selling and hand off to a sales team**
    - You've documented the repeatable process (playbook exists)
    - You can describe the ideal customer and their pain points clearly
    - You've closed 15+ customers and see consistent patterns
    - You're closing 5+ deals per month (scaling requires a team)
    - You should still be involved in enterprise deals, even after hiring a sales team
    - Your exit from daily sales should be gradual; mentor the sales hire by sitting in on their calls for their first 20 conversations

## Edge Cases

1. **Prospect asks for custom feature as buying condition**
   - How it breaks: "We'll buy if you add [custom feature]"; you commit in the moment; feature takes 6 months to build
   - Prevention: Ask "How critical is this to your decision?" before promising; offer workaround or integration instead
   - Recovery: Push the custom request to "let's validate value with the core product first; then we'll prioritize that feature"

2. **You price wrong (too high or too low) and close 5 customers**
   - How it breaks: You charge $1K/month but should've charged $5K; now you're locked in at wrong price
   - Prevention: Price higher than you think; adjust down later if needed, but adjusting up is painful
   - Recovery: If too low, grandfather existing customers at old price, but announce new pricing for future customers immediately

3. **First customer wants concessions you can't deliver**
   - How it breaks: "We'll buy if you add SSO, custom reporting, and a dedicated account manager"
   - Prevention: Qualify before discovery—if their demands seem outsized, have a filter conversation early
   - Recovery: Offer partial concessions (e.g., "We'll build SSO; the other two you can access through our standard tooling")

4. **You're splitting your time and sales motion stalls**
   - How it breaks: You're raising funding, building product, and doing sales; none of them move
   - Prevention: Protect 10 hours/week for founder-led sales even when busy; non-negotiable
   - Recovery: Stop other activities; sales motion is the fastest way to get traction (traction helps fundraising too)

5. **Network runs out of warm prospects**
   - How it breaks: You've talked to everyone in your network; now what?
   - Prevention: After 10 customers, you should have references who can make warm intros to similar companies
   - Recovery: Shift to "warm-ish" outreach (LinkedIn message from mutual connection, cold email with specific personalization)

6. **Objections keep changing; no pattern emerges**
   - How it breaks: Every customer raises different concerns; you can't build a playbook
   - Prevention: Document ruthlessly; objections are data. You need 20 conversations to see patterns
   - Recovery: Keep selling; once you hit 15-20 customers, objections will cluster into top 3-5 that dominate
