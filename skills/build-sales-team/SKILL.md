## Purpose

Most teams fail at building sales because they hire a VP Sales and expect that person to close deals AND build process AND train a team. That's three jobs. This skill teaches you to:

- **Hire a player-coach, not a VP** — someone who can sell AND build process in years 1-2
- **Hire in pairs** — one AE + one SDR early, so you can evaluate performance against a baseline
- **Build comp plans before hiring** — compensation drives behavior; get it right or your team chases the wrong deals
- **Document everything** — playbooks, talk tracks, battle cards, and call recordings become your training machine
- **Set ramp expectations** — new hires should generate pipeline in month 1, close small deals by month 3, hit quota by month 6-9
- **Weekly pipeline reviews** — catch stalls early; don't discover problems at month 12 when you're firing people
- **Culture first** — hire for competitive but collaborative mindset; sales teams can turn toxic fast

The mistake most founders make is hiring a VP too early and expecting that person to replace them. VP Sales should amplify founder involvement in enterprise deals, not eliminate it.

## When to Use This Skill

- **You've closed 15-20 customers yourself** — you have enough data to know what you're hiring for
- **You can articulate your sales motion** — "Discovery calls → 2-week trial → close" (or whatever your process is)
- **You're ready to scale past founder bandwidth** — you're turning away prospects or deals are stalling
- **Your repeatable motion is documented** — playbook exists; you can hand it to someone else
- **Monthly revenue is growing but uneven** — some months 3 deals, others zero; you need consistent pipeline
- **You're fundraising or planning Series A** — VCs want to see a sales leader and repeatable revenue process
- **Multiple deals are in flight** — founder-led sales doesn't scale to managing 10+ simultaneous deals

## Instructions

1. **Decide: SDR/AE split vs. full-cycle AE**
   - **Full-cycle AE** (best for SMB, <$5K deals) — one person does discovery to close; ramp is faster (3-4 months)
   - **SDR/AE split** (best for mid-market, $10K+ deals) — SDRs generate pipeline, AEs close; ramp is longer (6-12 months total)
   - **Hybrid** (best for mix of SMB + mid-market) — 2 full-cycle AEs for SMB + 1 SDR feeding them + 1 AE working mid-market
   - Your decision depends on: deal size, sales cycle length, and your ability to invest in pipeline development
   - Early-stage: start with 2 full-cycle AEs; add SDRs once deal velocity requires it

2. **Hire a player-coach first, not a VP**
   - A player-coach is someone who can sell 60% of their time and build process 40%
   - Job title: "Senior Sales Manager" or "Sales Manager" (not VP yet)
   - Background: 5+ years closing deals similar to your customer size, NOT a VP at a massive company
   - Avoid: People who've never had direct sales experience, people who want to manage instead of sell, people from very large companies (process will be overkill)
   - First hire should be the person you'd want in the field, not the person who wants to hire 20 people
   - Player-coach should report to you (founder) not to a COO or CFO

3. **Hire in pairs for credibility**
   - Never hire one salesperson and expect to evaluate if they're good or bad
   - Hire 2 AEs or 1 AE + 1 SDR at the same time with similar background
   - This lets you compare: both followed the same playbook, same comp plan, same training
   - If one closes 5 deals and one closes 1, you know the difference is execution, not process
   - Pairs also create internal accountability and friendly competition

4. **Build the comp plan before you hire**
   - Comp plan drives behavior more than anything you say
   - **For AEs:** 50% base / 50% commission is standard at early stage
   - **For SDRs:** 60% base / 40% commission (or bonus on pipeline)
   - **OTE (On-Target Earnings):** should be market rate for your geography (not below market; you'll lose reps)
   - **Quota:** should be 4-5x OTE for AEs (if rep earns $100K, quota should be $400-500K)
   - **Accelerators:** 1.5-2x commission rate above quota (rewards outperformers)
   - **Clawback:** if a customer churns in first 90 days, you claw back 50% of commission (aligns sales + CS)
   - **Plan simplicity:** if a rep can't calculate their commission in their head, the plan is too complex
   - Get legal review; don't change mid-year

5. **Document your sales playbook before ramp starts**
   - Your playbook should cover: ICP (ideal customer profile), discovery talk track, common objections + answers, demo flow, closing techniques, proof points / case studies
   - Record calls with early customers and annotate them (here's where I ask about pain; here's where I lost them)
   - Battle cards: one-pager for each competitor comparison
   - FAQ: common questions that come up in discovery (pricing, integration, timeline)
   - Case studies: 3-5 customer stories that reference can be pulled from
   - Assign one person (usually the first hire) to own playbook updates as new learnings emerge

6. **Hire the first rep from your network, not a recruiter**
   - Best first sales hire comes from your network (founder referral or reference from early customer)
   - Second-best: hire from your customer base (they know your product and believe in it)
   - Avoid: hiring from a large enterprise (they'll bring overcomplication and slow processes)
   - Interview for: curiosity (will they learn your market?), coachability (will they follow your playbook?), hunger (do they want to build something?)
   - Ignore: "10 years sales experience" if they only closed deals in comfortable situations

7. **Set clear ramp expectations (not promises)**
   - **Month 1:** Ramp phase; focus on learning the playbook, shadowing founder calls, understanding customers
     - Output: 10-15 discovery calls conducted, 5-10 meetings scheduled for next month, zero deals expected
   - **Month 2-3:** Early pipeline generation; start conducting independent discovery, begin closing first small deals
     - Output: 20+ discovery calls, 5-10 qualified opportunities in pipeline, 1-2 small deals ($1-5K) closed
   - **Month 4-6:** Building momentum; closing deals with growing frequency, pipeline expanding
     - Output: 30+ discovery calls/month, 10-15 qualified opportunities, 3-5 deals closed/month
   - **Month 6-9:** Hitting 50-70% of quota; becoming autonomous, contributing to team culture
     - Output: 50% of assigned quota ($200K quota = $100K revenue)
   - **Month 12+:** Hitting 90-100% of quota; mentoring new hires, improving playbook
   - If rep isn't at 50% of quota by month 6, you have a rep problem or a market/product problem

8. **Build sales enablement (playbooks, recordings, case studies)**
   - **Sales playbook:** living document covering ideal customer, discovery approach, objection handling, pricing, close techniques
   - **Call recordings:** annotated examples of good discovery calls, strong closes, handling tough objections
   - **Battle cards:** one-pagers for top 3-5 competitors (their strengths, where we're better, how to position against them)
   - **Case studies:** 3-5 detailed customer stories (company, problem, solution, results); include quantified outcomes
   - **FAQ document:** answers to the 20 questions that come up every week (pricing, integrations, timelines, etc.)
   - **Objection responses:** list of common objections (mapped to customer type) + tested responses
   - Update playbook quarterly based on new learnings from team

9. **Implement weekly pipeline reviews**
   - Every Monday: 30-minute standup with each salesperson
   - Agenda: (1) Deals that closed last week, (2) Pipeline changes (new, stalled, lost), (3) Blockers or support needed
   - Watch for: deals stuck in early stage (should be moving), declining pipeline (indicates activity issues), deals with no clear next steps (indicates no champion)
   - Your job: unblock, coach, escalate if needed; not to take over the deal
   - Track: deals won, deals lost (and why), average deal size, sales cycle length, conversion rate by stage
   - Share: weekly metrics with the team and founder; celebrate wins, address patterns

10. **Culture: competitive but collaborative**
    - Sales teams can turn toxic fast if you're not careful
    - Competitive elements: individual quotas, commission, opportunity to lead bigger deals for top performers
    - Collaborative elements: shared comp bonus if team hits target, knowledge sharing on tough deals, celebrating team wins
    - No: internal competitions where reps poach each other's prospects, shaming underperformers, winners take all
    - Yes: mentorship where top performer helps struggling rep, shared case studies and playbook improvements, public recognition
    - Manager role: coach and culture keeper, not just quota enforcer

## Edge Cases

1. **First rep doesn't work out (low activity, poor close rate, misses quota)**
   - How it breaks: By month 4-5, you realize they're not going to work; now you've lost 5 months of ramp time
   - Prevention: Weekly pipeline reviews catch underperformance early; clear expectations set in month 1
   - Recovery: Make the change by month 3 (if obvious); don't wait until month 6; hire a replacement immediately

2. **You hire a VP Sales who insists on "their way" vs. your playbook**
   - How it breaks: VP comes in and throws out your playbook; wants to start from scratch; reps are confused
   - Prevention: Hire a player-coach first, not a VP; don't give them authority to change process without founder buy-in
   - Recovery: If you've hired a VP, make it clear: "Validate the playbook for 90 days, then we'll iterate together"

3. **Both AEs are stuck in ramp; neither is progressing**
   - How it breaks: Month 4 arrives; both reps have closed 0 deals; you question if the market exists
   - Prevention: Weekly pipeline reviews surface issues by week 3 (deals aren't moving); debug whether it's rep or market
   - Recovery: Are deals stalling because of rep execution or product fit? If market issue, adjust ICP or product pitch; if rep issue, increase coaching or replace

4. **Comp plan creates internal conflict (one AE closes bigger deals, the other frustrated)**
   - How it breaks: Territory-based quota seems unfair if one territory has bigger deals; team morale suffers
   - Prevention: Design comp plan with founder + future manager before hiring; adjust territories based on market knowledge
   - Recovery: Pivot from territory-based to skill-based (AE owns deal size, not region); or adjust quotas to account for territory differences

5. **Sales team scales faster than operations can support**
   - How it breaks: 2 AEs are closing $3M ARR but customer success, product, and legal are overwhelmed
   - Prevention: Hire sales + CS in parallel; don't scale sales ahead of support functions
   - Recovery: Slow down new sales hiring; focus on customer retention and success; fix ops before hiring more reps

6. **SDR hire turns out to be "pipeline generator" but reps don't close deals**
   - How it breaks: SDR sets 50 meetings/month but reps are closing 2 deals; pipeline is fluffy
   - Prevention: Define what "qualified" means upfront (not just meeting; needs to be qualified opportunity)
   - Recovery: Have SDR shadow rep calls; understand why meetings aren't converting; adjust SDR filtering or rep coaching
