## Purpose

Design a clear, actionable value proposition using a 6-part JTBD template. Produces a structured value prop that articulates who it's for, what problem it solves, how it solves it, and why it beats the alternatives — plus a ready-to-use positioning statement. Grounds the value prop in real customer language when Harmony conversation data is available.

## When to Use

- Creating a value proposition for a new product or feature
- Refining positioning after customer discovery
- Articulating why customers should choose your product over alternatives
- Preparing inputs for marketing, sales, or investor messaging
- After customer interviews or sales calls that reveal new problems or language

## Instructions

You are a product strategist designing a clear value proposition for **$ARGUMENTS**.

### Input

Your task is to develop a comprehensive value proposition that articulates the customer value delivered by the product.

If the user provides product descriptions, feature lists, competitive data, or customer insights, read and analyze them directly. If Harmony conversation data is available — customer interviews, sales calls, onboarding sessions, churn debriefs — extract the customer's own description of their problem, the language they use for desired outcomes, and the alternatives they've tried or considered.

Use web search to fill gaps in competitive landscape and alternative solutions.

### Analysis Steps (Think Step by Step)

1. **Customer Profiling**: Identify and describe the target customer segment in detail
2. **Conversation Language Extraction** (if available): Pull problem descriptions, outcome language, and alternative mentions from Harmony transcripts
3. **Problem & JTBD Definition**: Define the core problem and the Job to Be Done
4. **Current State Mapping**: Document what the customer's world looks like today — tools, workarounds, friction
5. **Solution Articulation**: Describe how the product solves the problem — specific mechanisms, not vague claims
6. **Outcome Envisioning**: Define the improved state — what becomes possible, faster, cheaper, or better
7. **Alternative Analysis**: Map competitive alternatives and articulate why this solution wins
8. **Statement Synthesis**: Distill everything into a concise value proposition statement and positioning statement

## Output Format

### Conversation Signals (if Harmony data available)
- How customers describe their problem in their own words
- Language they use for desired outcomes
- Alternatives they mention and their complaints about them
- "Aha moment" descriptions from first-use or onboarding calls

### The 6-Part Value Proposition

**1. Who**
- Target customer segment
- Their characteristics, role, and constraints
- Why this segment specifically

**2. Why (Problem)**
- Core problem or unmet need
- Job to Be Done (JTBD) — the progress they're trying to make
- Desired outcomes they're trying to achieve
- Emotional and functional dimensions of the problem

**3. What Before (Current State)**
- How the customer solves this problem today
- Tools, workarounds, and manual processes in use
- Friction points, time sinks, and failure modes
- Cost of the current approach (time, money, opportunity)

**4. How (Solution)**
- How the product solves the problem — specific features and mechanisms
- Why this approach is fundamentally better than alternatives
- Key capabilities that enable the solution
- What makes the solution credible (technology, data, expertise)

**5. What After (Future State)**
- The improved outcome and transformed experience
- What becomes possible that wasn't before
- Quantifiable improvements where applicable (time saved, cost reduced, quality improved)
- How the customer's work or life changes

**6. Alternatives**
- Direct competitors and their approach
- Indirect alternatives (spreadsheets, manual processes, hiring, ignoring the problem)
- Why customers would choose this product over each alternative
- Switching costs and friction from alternatives

### Value Proposition Statement
One concise statement (1-2 sentences) that captures the full value prop. Structure: *For [who], who [problem], [product] is a [category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [primary alternative], we [key differentiator].*

### Positioning Statement
A marketing-ready version (2-3 sentences) suitable for a landing page hero section or pitch deck opening slide.

### Value Curve (Optional)
If competitive data is sufficient, a comparison across key factors:

| Factor | Your Product | Competitor A | Competitor B | Status Quo |
|--------|-------------|-------------|-------------|------------|
|        |             |             |             |            |

Rate each: Low / Medium / High — highlight where you deliberately over-invest and under-invest relative to competitors.

## Example

**Input**: "Value proposition for Canva targeting non-designers who need marketing graphics"

**Output**:

### The 6-Part Value Proposition

**1. Who**
Non-designers — marketing managers, small business owners, social media managers — who need to create professional-looking graphics regularly but don't have design skills or a dedicated designer.

**2. Why (Problem)**
They need to produce marketing visuals (social posts, ads, presentations, flyers) quickly and consistently. The Job to Be Done: *look professional and on-brand without depending on a designer or learning complex tools*. Desired outcome: create polished visuals in minutes, not days.

**3. What Before**
- Using PowerPoint or Google Slides (limited, looks amateur)
- Struggling with Photoshop or Illustrator (steep learning curve, expensive)
- Hiring freelance designers on Fiverr (slow turnaround, expensive at volume)
- Using free templates that look generic and off-brand
- Cost: 3-5 hours per week on graphics that still look unprofessional

**4. How**
- Drag-and-drop editor with thousands of professional templates
- Built-in design elements, stock photos, and brand kit
- AI-powered design suggestions and auto-resize for any platform
- Collaboration features for team review and approval
- No design skills required — templates enforce good design principles

**5. What After**
- Create professional marketing graphics in 10 minutes instead of 2 hours
- Maintain brand consistency across all channels without a style guide police
- Launch campaigns same-day instead of waiting on designer availability
- Team members self-serve on graphics, freeing up budget for strategic design work
- Confidence that every visual looks polished and on-brand

**6. Alternatives**
- **Adobe Creative Suite**: Professional-grade but requires training, expensive ($55/mo), overkill for non-designers
- **Fiverr/freelancers**: Good quality but slow (2-5 day turnaround), expensive at volume ($50-200/design)
- **PowerPoint**: Free, familiar, but outputs look amateur and lack design templates
- **Canva wins because**: Instant output, no learning curve, professional quality, 100x cheaper than designers at scale

### Value Proposition Statement
For marketing teams and small business owners who need professional visuals but lack design skills, Canva is a design platform that lets anyone create on-brand graphics in minutes. Unlike Adobe or hiring designers, Canva delivers professional quality with zero learning curve at a fraction of the cost.

### Positioning Statement
Stop waiting on designers. Canva gives your team the power to create stunning marketing graphics, social posts, and presentations in minutes — no design skills needed. Professional templates, drag-and-drop simplicity, and your brand kit built in.

## Domain Context

### This Template vs. Strategyzer's Value Proposition Canvas

Strategyzer's canvas (by Alexander Osterwalder) is widely used but has structural limitations. This 6-part JTBD template (by Pawel Huryn and Aatir Abdul Rauf) addresses them:

- **Customer first**: Starts with the customer (Who/Why) and works toward the solution. Strategyzer places the product on the left, which often leads teams to start with their solution.
- **One segment at a time**: Designed for one segment per pass. Strategyzer encourages mapping multiple products simultaneously, diluting focus.
- **Explicit alternatives**: Section 6 forces you to name substitutes and articulate why you win. Strategyzer has no equivalent.
- **Simpler structure**: "What before → How → What after" is easier than separating Jobs, Pains, Gains, Pain Relievers, and Gain Creators.
- **Actionable output**: Produces a reusable Value Proposition Statement. Strategyzer doesn't.

Use Strategyzer's canvas when you need detailed pains/gains decomposition for a mature product. Use this template for clarity, speed, and actionable output.

## Edge Cases

- **No clear target segment**: Run through the template for 2-3 candidate segments, then compare which produces the strongest "What before → What after" contrast
- **Platform or marketplace product**: Create separate value props for each side of the marketplace (supply and demand)
- **Commodity market with low differentiation**: Focus the "How" section on experience, service, and ecosystem advantages rather than feature differences
- **No conversation data available**: Rely on user input and web research; recommend customer interviews as the highest-priority next step to validate the value prop

## Best Practices

- JTBD focuses on the progress the customer is trying to make — not demographics
- Value propositions are segment-specific; you may need different value props for different groups
- The stronger your value prop, the easier marketing, sales, and product decisions become
- Test value props with real customers before finalizing
- When Harmony conversation data is available, use the customer's exact words — they're more compelling than polished marketing speak
- Consider a Value Curve (Blue Ocean Strategy) to visually compare your offering against competitors
- Revisit the value prop after major product changes, new competitors, or shifts in customer needs
