# Drive Partnerships & BD

> Scale revenue beyond direct sales with partnerships that have clear mutual value, dedicated resources, and measured impact — start with integrations, avoid the partnership graveyard.

This skill teaches you to build scalable revenue through partnerships without getting stuck in analysis paralysis or vaporware agreements that never launch. You'll identify partners with real mutual value (not just "nice fits"), structure revenue models that motivate both sides (not one-sided extraction), start with integrations (the easiest to execute and prove), assign dedicated resources so partnerships actually happen (not just a title with no budget), and measure results monthly so you know what's working and what's dead weight. Most importantly, you'll avoid the partnership graveyard—that space where 50 partnerships are "in progress" but nothing ships because nobody's accountable. Includes partnership templates, revenue model examples with pros and cons, monthly measurement checklists, and how to exit partnerships gracefully when they're not delivering.

## Use Cases

- **Scaling beyond direct sales** → At $1M+ ARR, partnerships become critical lever; reach new customers through their platforms; diversify revenue channels
- **Launching integration with complementary tool** → Build integration first (no permission needed), prove value with users, then formalize with revenue-share
- **Entering new market or segment** → Partner with local player or category expert; they bring relationships; you bring product; faster than hiring local sales
- **Expanding product ecosystem** → Position your product as hub; build integrations with supporting tools; partners benefit from richer ecosystem; increases stickiness
- **Building channel sales** → System integrators, resellers, or consultants sell your product; you take commission or flat fee per deal; scale without hiring
- **Driving co-marketing campaigns** → Joint webinar, case study, event sponsorship with complementary vendor; share audience and credibility; split costs
- **Identifying and vetting partners** → Find companies that reach your ICP; assess mutual value before signing; start with pilot instead of 3-year agreement
- **Revitalizing stalled partnerships** → Partnerships need quarterly check-ins, exec sponsorship refreshes, and measurement; under-resourcing kills them

## Setup

Start by identifying 3-5 potential partners based on audience overlap and complementary products (not just "nice to have"). Assess mutual value for each explicitly—what do they gain from the partnership? What do you gain? Avoid imbalanced partnerships. Start with integration partnerships if possible (easiest to execute, lowest risk). Build the integration first internally (ask forgiveness later, not permission). Once users are adopting it, approach the partner with adoption metrics. Assign one dedicated person from each side (not "this is their 20th partnership"). Have CEO/VP-level executive sponsors align on expectations. Lock in with 6-month pilot agreement (fixed term, clear success metrics, clean exit clause). Measure pipeline and revenue sourced monthly. Expand only if delivering mutual value.

## First Steps

1. List 3-5 companies that reach your ideal customer profile and offer complementary products (not competitive).
2. For each, assess mutual value: What do they gain? What do you gain? If it's one-sided, skip them.
3. Start with integration partnerships if possible; they're the lowest-risk way to test partnership dynamics.
4. Build your integration first internally (ask for forgiveness later, not permission); prove adoption before approaching the partner.
5. Assign one dedicated person from your side (not "this is their 20th partnership"); have CEO-level sponsors on both sides.
6. Lock in a 6-month pilot agreement with clear success metrics and an exit clause; measure monthly (pipeline, revenue, adoption).

## Key Insight

Most partnerships fail because of benign neglect, not misalignment. You sign a nice agreement with aligned incentives, then nobody gets assigned to manage it. The partner gets busy and forgets you exist. Your integration has a bug and sits unfixed for months. A partnership with no dedicated DRI is a partnership that will fail.

The second insight: start with a 6-month pilot, not a 3-year contract. Six months gives you enough time to test the relationship, measure results, and decide if you want to expand. Three-year agreements lock you into something that might not be working. Pilots have an exit clause by default; long-term partnerships require renewal. This gives both sides room to course-correct or exit gracefully.

Template: assign one dedicated person from each side (not "this is their 21st partnership"). Have CEO/VP-level exec sponsors on both sides to unblock issues. Measure partnership health monthly (pipeline, revenue, adoption, NPS). Track it like a P&L. Be willing to end partnerships that aren't delivering after the first 6 months. Partnerships scale revenue exponentially, but only if both sides have real incentives and dedicated resources. Anything else is just a handshake.
