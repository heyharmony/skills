# Prepare for Fundraising

> Fundraising is a process, not an event. Get your house in order, build momentum with warm intros, and negotiate from strength.

## What It Does

Fundraising is a full-time job for 3-6 months. This skill walks you through the complete lifecycle: getting your cap table, financials, and narrative in order (months 1-2); building an investor target list and executing warm introductions (months 2-4); managing multiple conversations and demonstrating traction (months 3-5); preparing for due diligence proactively; and negotiating from strength when you have multiple term sheets. Most founders underestimate the intensity. The goal is not one term sheet; it's multiple term sheets so you have leverage to negotiate valuation, terms, and investor quality. Your story and investor relationships matter more than your deck.

## Who It's For

- **First-Time Founders** raising for the first time; need clarity on the process and common pitfalls
- **Seed-Stage Founders** ready to raise after hitting product-market fit milestones
- **Series A Founders** transitioning from angel/seed investors to institutional VCs
- **CFOs & Operators** managing the financial and legal side of fundraising for founders
- **Advisors & Mentors** coaching founders through the process; want a framework to reference
- **Angel Investors** evaluating founder readiness; looking for founders with clean cap tables and clear narratives

## Use Cases

- **First Seed Round** → Clean your cap table, audit finances, identify 50 investors → Get warm intros to 15 → Land 2 term sheets → Close $500K at good valuation and terms

- **Series A Fundraising** → Share metrics showing product-market fit (growth, retention, unit economics) → Target Series A VCs with construction/SMB expertise → Land $2M from lead investor with strategic value-add → New capital to expand sales

- **Raising While Bootstrapped** → No previous investors, clean cap table → Build narrative around traction (maybe 10 MRR, profitability) → Attract impact investors or non-traditional funders → Maintain founder control while getting growth capital

- **Managing Due Diligence Proactively** → Organize all cap table docs, financials, customer agreements in a shared drive → Investors ask for diligence items; you deliver in 24 hours → Investors appreciate professionalism; close faster → Avoid extended due diligence delays

- **Negotiating Better Terms** → Get 3 term sheets in same timeframe → Review terms with lawyer → Negotiate valuation, board seats, liquidation preference → Close with most founder-friendly terms → Preserve control for future rounds

- **Building Long-Term Investor Relationship** → Identify investors whose portfolio and thesis align with your long-term vision → Build relationship 6 months before raising (CEO dinners, updates) → When you raise, they move fast because they know you → Less friction, better terms

## Setup

Start 6-12 months before you need money. Get your cap table documented and auditable in a tool like Carta. Pull 24 months of clean financials. Write your business narrative: problem, why you're the right team, why now. Create a 20-30 slide pitch deck. Identify 50+ investors in your target market, tiered by fit. Start building warm intro paths. Practice your pitch. When you're ready to raise actively, execute 2-3 investor conversations per week. Aim for 5+ investor meetings monthly. Track conversations and watch for "very interested" investors; these become your focus for term sheets.

## What You Get

- **Multiple Term Sheets** — Warm-intro strategy and systematic investor outreach land 2+ term sheets, giving you negotiating leverage
- **Better Valuation** — Competing term sheets drive valuation up; founders typically get 15-30% higher valuation when they have options
- **Founder-Friendly Terms** — Negotiate board seats, liquidation preference, anti-dilution from strength, not desperation
- **Faster Closes** — Prepared founders with clean cap tables and clear narratives close in 2-4 weeks; unprepared founders take 8-12 weeks
- **Better Investor Partners** — Multiple term sheets let you pick investors for value-add, not just capital; better partners accelerate your growth
- **Reduced Stress** — Knowing your numbers cold, having a clear narrative, and managing multiple conversations takes anxiety out of fundraising

## Fundraising Psychology

Understanding investor incentives shapes your approach:

- **Investors hate FOMO** — Multiple term sheets create fear of missing out. Suddenly the investor who was "thinking about it" moves fast because others are interested.

- **Relationships Compound** — The investor who rejects you today may fund your next round if you keep them updated. Stay on good terms with everyone.

- **Story Beats Numbers** — A clear narrative about why this market and this team matter moves investors more than perfect unit economics.

- **Momentum is Contagious** — Investors herd. When one investor commits, others move faster. Use this. Banking 1-2 early commitments accelerates the rest.

- **Timing Matters** — Some investors fund in Q1, not Q4. Some have limited budgets and will fund first great opportunity, then radio silence. Research investor timing.

## Key Insight

Fundraising is 30% process and 70% relationships. The process part (cap table, financials, pitch deck) is necessary table stakes; but the outcome is determined by your relationships. Investors fund people, not spreadsheets. They want to invest in founders they trust, have conviction about, and believe can execute. Start building these relationships 6-12 months before you need money. Get intros from people they respect. Share updates. Demonstrate progress. When you finally ask for an intro, you're not a cold ask — you're someone they already believe in. This is why warm intros convert 10x better than cold outreach. The second insight: optimize for multiple term sheets, not one big check. One term sheet means you have no leverage; the investor knows you have no alternatives. Multiple term sheets (even from smaller investors) let you negotiate and choose based on fit, not desperation. This mindset shift — from "please give me money" to "I'm shopping my round" — changes everything.

Build relationships before you need them. This is the entire game.
