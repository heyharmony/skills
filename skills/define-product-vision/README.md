# Define Product Vision

> A vision is not a tagline. It's the specific world your company will create in 5-10 years.

## What It Does

This skill helps you articulate a clear, actionable vision by extracting the core problems your company solves and translating them into a concrete description of your customer's future world. It guides you to move beyond vague aspirations ("be the market leader") to specific behavior changes ("engineering teams ship daily with zero production downtime"). You'll learn to ground your vision in the emotional and practical shifts your customers will experience, not in features or technology. The skill validates your vision against four criteria—lofty, realistic, tech-agnostic, and problem-grounded—then checks whether it actually guides team decision-making and helps resolve conflicting priorities. A great vision should make trade-offs obvious: some bets support it, others don't.

## Who It's For

- **Product leaders** struggling to articulate why their company matters beyond the next quarterly release
- **Founders** who have a feeling about where they're going but can't explain it clearly to investors or teams
- **Engineering managers** who need to align technical strategy with business purpose
- **Design leaders** defining the future user experience and emotional tone of their product
- **Go-to-market leaders** positioning the company to new markets or segments
- **Board members and investors** asking "What are you *actually* building toward?"
- **Team members and ICs** who need clarity on why their work matters so they can make decisions without constant approval
- **Hiring managers** looking to attract candidates who share your long-term mission

## Use Cases

- **Strategic pivot or expansion**: You've outgrown your original market, and your vision no longer fits. Redefine what world you're creating → align team priorities before building the wrong product
- **Team misalignment on priorities**: Engineers want to optimize for speed, design wants to optimize for beauty, sales wants to optimize for conversion. A clear vision becomes the tiebreaker → teams make decentralized decisions in harmony
- **Hiring or culture friction**: You keep hiring misaligned people, or your best people leave after 18 months. A crisp vision filters for mission-aligned talent early → culture strengthens around a shared "why"
- **Fundraising or partnership conversations**: Investors or partners want to understand your long-term moat. A memorable vision that describes customer impact (not product features) → differentiation becomes obvious
- **New market or product line**: You're entering a new vertical. Do you extend the existing vision or pivot? Clarity here prevents strategy drift → execution becomes coherent across teams
- **Annual planning or OKR setting**: You're about to commit to quarterly goals. Do they actually serve your 5-10 year vision, or are you just chasing revenue? Vision-grounded OKRs → team effort compounds toward something meaningful
- **Deciding whether to build or acquire**: A potential acquisition target appears. Does acquiring them accelerate or distract from your vision? Clarity makes the decision obvious

## Connectors

**Works standalone**, but richer with:
- **Zoom**: Capture founder and leadership reflections on long-term ambitions and strategic direction
- **Notion**: Store vision documents, iteration history, and alignment references for team access
- **Slack**: Share vision drafts and gather async feedback from stakeholders
- **Google Drive**: Collaborate on mockups, competitive landscape research, and future state prototypes
- **Linear**: Link your vision to quarterly OKRs and product roadmap, ensuring every project traces back to long-term direction

## Setup

1. Gather leadership reflections on long-term ambitions (recorded calls, strategy docs, or fresh interviews)
2. Identify 2-3 foundational customer problems from your transcript or case studies
3. For each problem, map the emotional and behavioral shifts you want to enable
4. Draft the vision in customer-centric language (their world, not your product)
5. Test against the four criteria and common mistakes
6. Share with teams and listen for whether it actually guides their work

## What You Get

- A 1-paragraph vision statement that's specific enough to guide decisions and tell a compelling story to investors and customers. This becomes your north star for hiring, fundraising, and roadmapping.
- A visual artifact (prototype sketch, "day in the life" narrative, or mood board) that makes the vision tangible and memorable. People remember images and stories better than words.
- A one-page expansion documenting the problem, emotional shift, and strategic implications for your market. This is your internal reference when priorities conflict.
- A "vision check" framework for resolving conflicting priorities and trade-offs when your team debates direction. Use this in quarterly planning sessions.
- Clarity on one-way door decisions your vision rules out or requires (what you'll never build). This prevents drift and wasted effort on misaligned bets.
- A test: can your team articulate the vision without reading a document? If yes, it's real and embedded in culture.
- A quarterly or annual "vision refresh" checklist to ensure your roadmap still serves the long-term goal and to surface when the vision needs updating

## Key Insight

Vision isn't aspirational fluff—it's a decision-making tool. The best visions make a thousand downstream decisions obvious. When your team debates a feature, they should be able to ask "Does this move us toward [vision]?" and have a clear answer. If your vision doesn't guide decisions, it's not a vision; it's a tagline.

## Pro Tips

- Share your vision in multiple formats: elevator pitch, one-page, detailed narrative, and visual. Different people absorb it differently.
- Test your vision against acquisition decisions: would buying Company X accelerate or distract from your vision? If unclear, your vision isn't crisp enough.
- Revisit your vision yearly. If it hasn't changed in 3+ years, either you're very aligned or you've stopped thinking about the future.
