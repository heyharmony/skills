## Purpose

Brand stories are the most underutilized asset in go-to-market. Features change; problems stay constant. Customers remember how your product made them feel and what became possible, not your feature list. This skill teaches you to identify and shape the transformation your customer experiences, anchor it in real evidence, and repeat it everywhere—website, sales calls, support interactions, internal meetings.

The best brand stories make the customer the hero, not your company. Your job is to illuminate the turning point—the moment they discovered your product and realized their world changed. That's the story that sticks, that drives word-of-mouth, and that aligns your team around the same narrative.

Most companies make three mistakes: they make themselves the hero (the company built this, the company achieved this), they skip customer evidence (generic claims beat specific numbers nowhere), and they tell different stories in different channels (website says one thing, sales says another, support says a third). This skill fixes all three.

## When to Use This Skill

- You need to differentiate from competitors and your feature set isn't unique anymore
- Your sales team is struggling to articulate why customers should care beyond price
- Your website reads like a feature list and visitors bounce without engaging
- You're hiring and new employees can't tell the company story in a clear, credible way
- You're creating a case study or customer testimonial but it feels generic and unmemorable
- Your messaging is inconsistent across channels (website, emails, sales decks, support)
- You're preparing for a media pitch and need to articulate the underlying narrative trend, not the product announcement
- Customers say "your product changed how we work" but you haven't captured or weaponized that transformation
- You're building a deck for investors and need to show product-market fit through customer outcomes, not just metrics

## Instructions

1. **Identify the transformation: before state, turning point, after state**
   - Interview 5-10 recent customers (mix of active and advocates) with this structure: "Walk me through how you were working before our product. What was the hardest part? What changed when you started using us? What's different now?"
   - Don't ask "what do you like about us?" (they'll list features). Ask "what became possible?" or "what do you no longer worry about?"
   - Document the exact language they use to describe the problem, the moment they realized your solution worked, and the outcome they achieved
   - Look for the emotional note: was there frustration in the before? Relief in the after? That's where the story hooks

2. **Extract before-and-after specifics: metrics, quotes, operational changes**
   - For each customer transformation, write it as: "Before [specific pain], they tried [failed approach], then discovered [your solution], now [concrete outcome]"
   - Quantify the outcome if possible: time saved, cost reduced, quality improved, speed increased (e.g., "reduced report time from 4 hours to 20 minutes")
   - Capture 2-3 direct quotes from the customer describing the transformation (their voice, not marketing-speak)
   - Identify the single biggest shift: was it time? Money? Confidence? Ability to scale? That's your anchor
   - Avoid feature language ("we use the dashboard") in favor of outcome language ("we finally have visibility into what's actually happening")

3. **Document your founder/company origin story: why did you build this?**
   - Founders often skip this, assuming it's self-indulgent. It's not. Your origin story is credibility proof that you understand the problem deeply
   - Answer: What problem did you personally experience? What gap did you see in the market? Why did you care enough to leave your previous job and build this?
   - The best origin stories have a specific moment: you were frustrated trying to solve X for Y reason, realized nobody else was solving it well, and decided to build it yourself
   - Avoid the "we saw a market opportunity" cliché. Instead: "I spent 6 months onboarding customers at my previous startup and watched our success team waste 3 days per customer on manual data entry. I realized nobody had built software that automated this, so I left and built it."
   - Your origin story should answer: why is your team uniquely positioned to solve this? What do you know that outsiders don't?

4. **Identify the narrative threads that connect your origin story to customer transformations**
   - Ask: do your customer outcomes align with the problem you originally experienced? If yes, that's proof your origin story is tied to real, recurring pain
   - Look for common themes across multiple customer stories: are they all trying to save time? Gain visibility? Scale without hiring? Whatever the pattern is, that's your narrative anchor
   - Connect the dots: "I experienced X problem at [previous company]. Now 50+ customers are experiencing the same problem. They solve it by using our software to [outcome]. This is the story."
   - Your narrative thread should be believable by your team—every engineer, salesperson, and support person should understand the core problem you're solving, not just the features you're selling

5. **Write your brand story in three versions: 30-second elevator pitch, 2-minute explanation, 5-minute detailed story**
   - 30-second version (for introductions, website header, social): "We help [customer] transform [before state] into [after state]. [One proof point with number or quote]."
   - 2-minute version (for sales calls, email copy, customer demos): Opening hook ("Most [customer type] spend 4 hours on [manual task]"), the transformation ("We built software that automates this"), proof ("Our customers save 3 hours per week"), and the invitation ("Here's how we do it for you")
   - 5-minute version (for case studies, webinars, press): Full story arc with specific company example, their before state, the moment they discovered your solution, detailed outcome, and their own words (long quote)
   - Each version should feel like the same story, told with different levels of detail—not three different narratives

6. **Build a customer story playbook with 3-5 detailed case studies across different segments**
   - Select customers representing your best use cases: small business + enterprise, different industries, different outcomes
   - For each, follow the template: Company name, role of primary user, the before-state pain (specific and credible), the turning point, the after-state outcome, and 1-2 direct quotes
   - Vary the stories: one about speed, one about cost, one about scale, one about quality, one about team alignment
   - Include a "why they chose us over competitors" insight (e.g., "They evaluated [competitors] but chose us because...")
   - Publish 2-3 of these as full case studies on your website; keep others as sales materials or interview fodder for media

7. **Ensure consistency across all channels: website, sales, support, internal**
   - Audit current messaging across these channels: website homepage, sales deck, customer email onboarding, support documentation, team handbook
   - Do they tell the same story? Or does each channel have its own narrative?
   - Rewrite for consistency: every channel should reference the same customer problem, the same transformation, and the same proof points
   - Website should tell the customer story (what's possible now), not list features
   - Sales deck should walk through the before-and-after transformation with a real customer example
   - Support documentation should explain not just "how to use X feature" but "why this feature solves the problem you came to us with"
   - Internal handbook should describe the company's origin story and core customer transformations so every hire understands what they're building
   - Do a quarterly audit: pick one channel per month and refresh its story alignment

8. **Test your brand story in real conversations and iterate based on responses**
   - Use your next 5 customer conversations to test your story: "Here's how I've been describing what we do—does this feel right to you?"
   - Watch for the places where prospects lean in (emotional resonance) vs. glaze over (feature language)
   - Ask prospects: "What story would make you choose us over [competitor]?" (their answer reveals what narrative moves them)
   - In sales calls, when prospects say "your product changed how we work," pause and ask "tell me the story of that transformation"—capture it, add it to your playbook
   - Iterate monthly: your stories should get tighter, more specific, and more emotionally resonant as you collect more customer language

## Edge Cases

- **Different outcomes for different customers**: You might have one customer who saves time, another who makes better decisions, another who can finally scale without hiring. Instead of one generic story, build 3-5 specific stories (one per outcome type) and let sales match the right story to the prospect's pain point.

- **No quantifiable outcome yet**: If you're early and customers haven't measured impact yet, ask them for qualitative proof: "What became possible that wasn't before?" Emotional transformation (confidence, peace of mind, ability to focus) is as compelling as metrics, especially early.

- **Founder doesn't want to be the hero**: Some founders are uncomfortable with personal origin stories. That's fine—shift the story to the team or the market insight instead of the founder. But don't skip the origin story entirely; it provides credibility that you understand the problem deeply.

- **Multiple product use cases**: If your product solves three different problems for three different customer types, build separate stories for each. One story for each use case—don't try to jam them into one narrative.

- **Competitive pressure to tell feature stories**: Sales will push back: "Competitors talk about features. We need to too." Agree, but make the case that your story should come first (on the website, in the opening of the sales call, in the pitch deck). Features are the supporting evidence, not the main narrative.

- **Story fatigue**: If you've been telling the same three customer stories for 6 months, refresh them. Interview new customers, find new transformations, and rotate your case studies. Stale stories feel corporate.
