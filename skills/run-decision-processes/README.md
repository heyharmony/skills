# Run Decision Processes

> Make high-quality decisions fast by surfacing assumptions, distinguishing reversible from irreversible choices, and committing to outcomes—then moving on.

## What It Does

Indecision creates organizational anxiety. This skill helps you make decisions fast without being reckless by making implicit assumptions explicit (so you're debating reality, not gut feelings), distinguishing between reversible and irreversible decisions (so you don't over-engineer simple choices), and assigning clear ownership so decisions stick. It also helps you gather input rigorously, resolve genuine conflict through structured debate, and document decisions so your org remembers *why* you chose what you chose.

## Who It's For

- **Engineering Leaders and CTOs** making technical decisions (architecture, platform choices, tool selections) that affect team velocity and scalability.
- **Product Leaders and PMs** navigating roadmap trade-offs and feature prioritization in the face of competing demands.
- **Founders and CEOs** deciding on strategy, funding, hiring, market pivots, and other high-stakes bets.
- **Operations and People Leaders** making resource allocation, hiring, and restructuring decisions.
- **Cross-functional Leadership Teams** navigating conflict and aligning on shared direction.
- **First-time Managers** learning how to make decisions with confidence and stakeholder buy-in.

### Pain Points They Face

- **Analysis paralysis**: Team keeps researching, debating, or waiting for more data. Months pass with no decision.
- **Decision by committee**: Everyone gets a vote; no one owns the outcome. Decisions are watered down or slow to converge.
- **Implicit assumptions**: People feel strongly about a choice but can't articulate *why*. Leads to circular debate.
- **Treating all decisions equally**: Spending weeks on a reversible decision (marketing experiment) when it should move fast.
- **Relitigating settled decisions**: After a decision, someone re-opens it. Team loses confidence and momentum.
- **Decisions lack ownership**: No one drives execution; the decision fades into the background.
- **No institutional memory**: Three months later, no one remembers *why* you chose that technology or strategy. New hires can't understand the decision's context.

## Use Cases

- **Major roadmap pivots** → Should we enter a new market? Rebuild our platform? Pivot the product? Use this skill to debate synchronously, surface assumptions, and commit.
- **Resource allocation conflicts** → Sales wants 3 engineers; product wants 5. Use structured decision-making to weigh trade-offs and move on.
- **Technical architecture decisions** → Microservices vs. monolith? GraphQL vs. REST? Use decision records so future teams understand the rationale.
- **Hiring or reorganization** → Should we hire a head of sales? Restructure teams? Use curiosity loops to test assumptions before committing.
- **Strategic uncertainty** → You're making a bet in unfamiliar territory (new market, new technology, new hire level). Make assumptions explicit so you know what you're risking.
- **Team is stuck** → Conversation loops endlessly. Use this skill to set a deadline, gather input, make a call, and move forward.

## Connectors

Works standalone with structured input and documentation, but richer with:

- **Slack**: Captures decision debate, assumptions, and stakeholder input; creates searchable record.
- **Notion**: Stores decision documents with assumptions, options, rationale, and revisit dates.
- **Google Drive**: Houses decision briefs and trade-off analyses.
- **Linear / GitHub**: Tracks decisions that result in roadmap changes or technical implementations.

## Setup

1. **Identify if it's Type 1 (irreversible) or Type 2 (reversible)**—affects process rigor.
2. **Assign decision-maker** (DACI: Driver, Approver, Contributor, Informed).
3. **For Type 1 decisions**: Create decision brief with options, assumptions, and trade-offs. Set deadline for input.
4. **Surface assumptions** through curiosity loops and targeted questions, not debate.
5. **Use structured debate** if there's genuine dissent: each side gets time to make the strongest case.
6. **Make the call** (one person decides, not consensus). Document the rationale and *why* you rejected other options.
7. **Disagree and commit**: Team commits to executing the decision even if they disagreed.
8. **Document decision record**: Create decision artifact for institutional memory (especially for technical decisions).
9. **Plan revisit date**: When will you re-evaluate if assumptions held? (Especially for Type 1 decisions.)

## What You Get

- **Fast decisions** on Type 2 (reversible) choices; rigorous-but-not-slow decisions on Type 1 (irreversible) choices.
- **Visible assumptions**: Everyone understands *why* you chose X over Y, based on assumptions and trade-offs.
- **Stakeholder alignment**: Input is gathered rigorously; no one feels blindsided or unheard.
- **Clear ownership**: Decision-maker is named; accountability is assigned.
- **Institutional memory**: Decision record documents context, rationale, and when to revisit.
- **No relitigating**: Decision is settled; team commits and moves forward; no circular debate.
- **Learning loop**: You revisit decisions and learn whether assumptions held, feeding future decision-making.
