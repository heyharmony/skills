# Plan Under Uncertainty

> Plans are useless, but planning is indispensable. The goal isn't to predict the future—it's to stay flexible while committed to a direction.

## What It Does

This skill builds plans that survive when assumptions break. Instead of a single rigid roadmap, it identifies your core assumptions (what must be true for your plan to work), creates decision points (if X happens by date Y, we do Z), and keeps doors open through optionality investments. When the market shifts, customer needs change, or your first hypothesis proves wrong, your plan adapts instead of shattering. This is agile planning: structured uncertainty management, not chaos.

## Who It's For

- **Product Strategists**: Building a new market or product line with high uncertainty. You need a planning framework that embraces unknown unknowns instead of pretending to predict the future.

- **Startup Founders**: Your assumptions are being tested constantly. You need a way to document what you think is true, test it cheaply, and pivot when data says so.

- **Engineering Leaders**: Technical strategy has unknowns (scalability, architecture choices, new tech adoption). This skill helps you test unknowns before committing a full team.

- **Business Leaders**: Making strategic bets with limited information. You need scenarios and contingency plans, not false confidence in a single future.

- **Long-Cycle Project Managers**: 12-month project. Too much can change. You need decision points and learning phases, not a fixed waterfall.

- **Teams in Shifting Markets**: Your competitive landscape is moving, or customer needs are evolving. This skill helps you plan adaptively while staying decisive.

## Use Cases

**Case 1: New Product Launch with Unclear Market**
You're entering a space you don't fully understand. Use this skill → Document core assumptions (who's the buyer, what's the price, what's the differentiator) → Create scenarios (best, base, worst) → Build decision points (if adoption is slow by month 2, we pivot) → Ship MVP → Learn → Decide → Scale. You're committed but not rigid.

**Case 2: Strategic Pivot Needed**
Market data shows your planned direction is less attractive than you thought. Use this skill to document alternative scenarios upfront → Identify decision triggers → When those triggers fire, you're ready to execute the pivot without chaos.

**Case 3: Multiple Technical Approaches**
You could build with Tech Stack A, B, or C. Different tradeoffs. Use scenario planning to explore each → Spike the risky one → Make the commitment based on data.

**Case 4: Long Roadmap with High Dependency on Customer Feedback**
You planned features 1-10 for the year. Use this skill → Design phases that learn from customers → Each phase answers a question → Based on that learning, the next phase adapts → You're not locked into the original plan, but you're not chaotic either.

**Case 5: Risk Management for a Big Bet**
You're allocating significant resources to an unproven idea. Use this skill to identify what could go wrong, create tripwires that force decisions if things are going sideways, and plan a pivot option before you need it.

**Case 6: Stakeholder Alignment on Uncertainty**
Executive team wants a detailed 12-month plan, but you know the future is uncertain. Use scenarios and decision frameworks to show you're serious about planning while being honest about uncertainty.

## Connectors

Works standalone with brainstorming and scenario writing. But richer with:

- **Notion**: Stores your assumption list, scenarios, decision logs, and plan iterations. Creates a record of what you thought was true versus what you learned.

- **Slack**: Shows where decision discussions happened, what context triggered pivots, and when team sentiment shifted about assumptions.

- **Google Docs**: Accesses strategy documents and business cases. Shows what you originally planned and lets you compare against what actually happened.

- **Linear**: Tracks which planned features got built, which were de-prioritized, and which assumptions they were based on. Shows which bets paid off.

## Setup

Start with a strategic vision and timeframe. Brainstorm: What assumptions are you making? Which are risky? Write scenarios (best, base, worst). Identify decision points: If X happens, we do Y. Create a learning plan: What will each phase teach you? No special tools needed—the skill is about structured thinking, not software. If you connect Notion or Google Docs, you can document assumptions and version them over time as you learn.

## What You Get

- **Core assumptions made explicit**: Not "we'll hit this timeline" but "this timeline assumes tech stack X works and customer adoption is Y." When reality differs, you're not surprised.
- **Scenario planning**: Three futures mapped out: best case, base case (what you plan for), worst case (your floor). Helps you see implications of different market conditions.
- **Decision frameworks**: Instead of "we'll decide later," you have "if X by date Y, we do Z." Removes decision paralysis when the moment comes.
- **Optionality mapping**: Identifies what to keep flexible (two-way doors, cheap to change) versus what to lock down (one-way doors, expensive to change).
- **Learning plan**: Each phase is designed to test an assumption and inform the next phase. You're not just building; you're learning systematically.
- **Tripwires**: Automatic triggers that force action ("if churn >15%, we redesign"). Prevents rationalization and delays.
- **Assumption tracker**: Monthly updates showing which assumptions held and which were proven wrong. This data informs future planning.

## Key Insight

The best plans aren't the ones that predicted the future perfectly—they're the ones that stayed flexible and adapted as the future unfolded. Scenario planning isn't an excuse to avoid commitment; it's a way to commit smartly: "We're all-in on this direction, but if data says we should pivot, here's how." Your first plan will be wrong in some ways—that's not failure, that's learning. The skill is building plans that expect to be adjusted, rather than plans that pretend certainty exists. By documenting assumptions upfront, you catch surprises early. By creating decision points, you respond fast. By investing in optionality, you stay agile. Agile planning isn't chaos—it's disciplined uncertainty management.
