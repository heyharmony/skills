## Purpose

Community is not a marketing channel. It's a product. Great communities give members identity (you're part of this tribe), belonging (people here understand me), and status (I can grow and be recognized here).

The 1-9-90 rule: 1% create content, 9% contribute/engage, 90% lurk. Design for all three. Lurkers get value from observing (don't force them to participate). Contributors get recognition (replies, reactions, etc.). Creators get status and community leadership.

Communities don't scale with community size. They compound with engagement depth. A 500-person community with 3 posts per week and no moderation is dead. A 50-person community with daily conversations, weekly programming, and clear culture is alive.

Your job is to start small (50 passionate early members), establish culture fast, give members escalating roles, create programming cadence, and moderate relentlessly. The community that compounds is the one where members feel invested.

## When to Use This Skill

- You're launching a community and want to avoid the "empty room" problem
- You have an existing community with low engagement and don't know how to revive it
- You want to measure whether your community is actually creating value (vs. just existing)
- You're considering whether your product even needs a community (not every product does)
- Members have different levels of activity and you want to nurture different roles
- You're seeing member churn in community and don't know why
- You want to predict whether your community will compound or decay

## Instructions

1. **Assess community-market fit before building**
   - Does your product naturally create peer-to-peer value? (Slack: yes. Calculator app: no.)
   - Will members want to share wins and ask questions? (E-commerce: yes. Spreadsheet tool: maybe not.)
   - Can members help each other solve problems? (CMS: yes. Accounting software: maybe.)
   - Community-market fit check: Would you have started this community if it didn't have product benefits?
   - Sub-bullets: Survey 5-10 customers; ask if they'd want to connect with peers; assess product fit for peer learning

2. **Identify and recruit 50 early members (your founding community)**
   - Your first 50 set the culture. Choose them intentionally, not at random.
   - Who to recruit: power users, curious learners, teachers/mentors, influencers in their own networks
   - Avoid: only customers; include non-customers who care about the category
   - Recruitment: personal outreach; "We're starting a community and want you as a founding member"
   - Sub-bullets: Make a list of 50-100 potential founding members; reach out personally to 50 strongest; expect 50% to accept; start with those 25-50

3. **Define community culture and norms early**
   - Culture is established in the first 10 conversations. Decide what you want and exemplify it.
   - Norms include: How helpful vs. critical? How formal vs. casual? Who can post vs. lurk? Moderation approach?
   - Example: "We celebrate wins, help people unblock, and assume good intent. Spam and self-promotion get one warning then removal."
   - Write 3-5 community guidelines and pin them visibly
   - Sub-bullets: Draft guidelines; share with founding members; refine based on feedback; enforce consistently from day 1

4. **Design escalating member roles to encourage growth**
   - Member (default): Can post, comment, lurk. Recognition: listed in member directory.
   - Contributor: Posted helpful content >3 times. Recognition: "Contributor" badge, listed first in directory.
   - Moderator: Trusted community member who handles day-to-day moderation. Recognition: special role, input on community direction.
   - Ambassador: Top contributors who recruit new members or represent community externally. Recognition: highest status; may get perks.
   - Define how people graduate between levels (transparent criteria: "Get 3 upvotes on helpful posts" or "Contribute for 3 months")
   - Sub-bullets: Define roles; communicate criteria; recognize people publicly when they level up; avoid gatekeeping

5. **Create programming cadence (weekly/bi-weekly events)**
   - Empty communities are dead. Regular programming creates rhythm and expectation.
   - Programming types: AMAs (Ask Me Anything with founder/expert), Showcases (members share projects), Challenges (solve problem together), Office Hours (1-on-1 help)
   - Cadence: At least 1 event per week (minimum). 2-3 per week = healthy. Daily = mature community.
   - Who runs it: Mix of founder/team + top community members (so members feel ownership)
   - Sub-bullets: Plan 8 weeks of programming; announce schedule; rotate hosts; measure attendance and engagement

6. **Establish moderation as your immune system**
   - Bad behavior (spam, harassment, tone-deaf pitches) kills culture if not addressed
   - Moderation types: preventative (clear norms), active (watch for rule breaks), responsive (warn and enforce)
   - Response: First offense = warning + education. Second = removal. Consistent enforcement is critical.
   - Give moderators clear tools and authority; don't make them guess
   - Sub-bullets: Define moderation policy; train moderators; establish escalation path; measure moderation load (should be <5% of posts)

7. **Measure engagement depth, not just size**
   - Vanity metric: Total members
   - Real metric: Active members (posted in past 30 days), engaged members (replied or reacted in past week), deep engagement (multiple conversations)
   - Calculation: If 500 members but only 30 post in any month, your community is 94% lurkers
   - Track: Total members, monthly active, daily active, topics/threads per week, replies per thread, member growth rate
   - Sub-bullets: Build dashboard tracking engagement metrics; set targets (e.g., 30% MAU); flag when engagement declines

8. **Design community experiences that compound**
   - Compounding experience: One member's contribution helps 10 others → 10 others feel motivated → 3 contribute back
   - Mechanics: Upvotes/reactions show value, threading keeps conversations alive, search makes old wisdom discoverable, tagging makes expertise visible
   - Non-compounding: Flat chronological feed, no way to upvote, no search, help questions get lost
   - Sub-bullets: Audit your platform for compounding mechanics; add threading/upvotes/tags if missing; test if members help each other

9. **Measure community impact on business metrics**
   - Impact metrics: % of active community members who remain customers (vs. non-members), revenue per community member, NPS of community members
   - Recruitment impact: How many new signups referred by community? % of signup source attribution?
   - Retention impact: Do community members churn less than similar non-members?
   - Cost: How much are you spending on community (time, hosting, events)? ROI = business impact / cost
   - Sub-bullets: Track cohort retention for members vs. non-members; measure referral impact; calculate community ROI

10. **Respond to declining engagement with interventions**
    - Warning signs: Fewer posts per week, fewer new members joining, longer time to first reply, members asking to leave
    - Interventions (in order): Revive programming (missed cadence?), recruit new voices (get fresh members), re-moderate (culture drifted?), rebrand/relaunch
    - Avoid: Blasting members with "please be more active" (makes problem worse); adding too much structure (kills organic growth)
    - Sub-bullets: Monitor engagement weekly; identify decline early; run 1-2 intervention tests; measure impact before escalating

## Edge Cases

- **Community-market fit is weak**: If your product doesn't naturally create peer value, a community will feel forced and fail. Better to skip it than build a dead one.

- **Community becomes too big too fast**: Slack communities >5k members need heavy moderation and multiple moderators. Growth pace matters more than size.

- **Members only lurk**: 90% lurking is normal. But if <10% engage, something's wrong with culture or programming. Fix that before growing.

- **Moderators burn out**: If you have 1 moderator managing 1k members, they'll quit. Distribute load across 4-5 people. Each person should handle <250 members max.

- **Community becomes sales channel**: If founder/team spends all time pitching product, members leave. Community is separate from sales; maintain culture.

- **Platform limits growth**: Slack is great for 1-2k members. Discord for 5k+. Forum for 10k+. Choose platform that matches growth trajectory.
