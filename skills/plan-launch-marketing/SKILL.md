## Purpose

Launches aren't campaigns—they're moments. Most teams focus on launch day (the fireworks) but ignore the 4 weeks before (building momentum) and the 4 weeks after (when the real work begins). This skill walks you through the entire launch arc: pre-launch (anticipation, insider access, waitlists), launch day (coordinating all channels simultaneously), and post-launch (measuring what actually worked, iterating based on feedback).

Founders often treat all launches the same—a new product gets the same energy as a minor feature. That's wasteful. Tier your launches. A new product should be a company-wide effort with media, community outreach, and weeks of pre-launch seeding. A major feature gets an email sequence and blog post. A minor update gets a changelog entry. Clarity on tier saves you from burning out your team on something that doesn't deserve it.

The hardest part isn't the launch day itself—it's internal alignment. If your sales team doesn't know what's launching before customers do, they'll undersell it. If support doesn't know about it, they'll get blindsided by questions. If your customer success team isn't ready with onboarding docs, customers will churn. This skill forces alignment first, execution second.

## When to Use This Skill

- You're 4-6 weeks out from launching a new product and need to coordinate across marketing, product, sales, and support
- You've launched before but it felt chaotic and you want a repeatable playbook for the next one
- Your team is debating whether to launch on Monday, Thursday, or next week (you need a tiering framework and decision criteria)
- You're launching on Product Hunt or Hacker News and need to maximize visibility (seeding strategy)
- You're planning a launch with paid media (ads, sponsorships) and need to coordinate with organic (blog, email, PR)
- You need to write one launch brief that everyone (sales, support, product, marketing) actually reads and follows
- You're wondering what to measure on launch day (spoiler: not signups, but activation at day 7 and day 30)
- You're planning a launch that spans multiple time zones and need to coordinate global communications

## Instructions

1. **Tier your launch and determine what investment level is appropriate**
   - Tier 1 (Major investment): New product, brand-defining feature, major positioning shift. Deserves 6-8 weeks of pre-launch, media outreach, community seeding, possibly paid media.
   - Tier 2 (Moderate investment): Major feature within existing product, significant improvement to core workflow, competitive response. Deserves 2-4 weeks of pre-launch, email sequence, blog post, community posts.
   - Tier 3 (Light investment): Bug fix, minor feature, incremental improvement, internal tool launch. Deserves changelog entry, brief email note, maybe a Slack post.
   - Ask: What does this launch enable for customers that wasn't possible before? If it's transformative, it's Tier 1. If it's useful, Tier 2. If it's good hygiene, Tier 3.
   - Decision: Based on tier, decide team investment, timeline, and channels. Tier 1 might need a dedicated launch lead. Tier 3 doesn't.
   - Document your tier and timeline in a shared doc so everyone aligns on what this launch is about

2. **Map your pre-launch phase (4-8 weeks before, depending on tier) and build anticipation**
   - Week 1-2: Teaser content (blog post, video, social) that hints at the problem you're solving without revealing the solution. Example: "Most teams struggle with X. Here's why it's hard." Don't name your feature yet.
   - Week 2-3: Insider access (beta access for power users, closed beta groups, VIP customers). Let them experience it first and become advocates. Ask them: "What surprised you? What's missing? What would you tell a friend about this?"
   - Week 3-4: Waitlist (for public launches). Offer something in exchange: early access, exclusive guide, community access. Track waitlist growth daily and optimize signup messaging if growth stalls.
   - Week 4+: Exclusive previews (media, analysts, influential voices in your space). Give one publication the exclusive story before you go public; that exclusive drives bigger coverage than blasting everyone at once.
   - Daily content (if Tier 1): Social posts, community engagement, education content that builds context for why this matters.
   - Measurement: Waitlist growth rate, beta user engagement, insider advocate quotes captured for launch day messaging.

3. **Get internal alignment before external announcement—this is the hardest part**
   - Sales alignment: Sales team should know what's launching, why it matters to customers, and how to position it in conversations. Do a 30-minute call one week before launch where the product team explains the feature, the sales leader role-plays objections, and the team nails the pitch.
   - Support/success alignment: Customer success and support should have documentation ready (not just technical docs, but guides on how this solves specific customer problems). They should be ready for an influx of questions and feature requests. Run a knowledge-base workshop 3 days before launch.
   - Marketing alignment: Make sure your blog post, email sequence, social copy, and paid media are all live and reviewed. No surprises on launch day.
   - Executive alignment: Your CEO should know exactly what's launching, why it matters, and be ready to amplify on personal channels if needed.
   - Create a single "launch brief" document (one page) that everyone reads: what's launching, why it matters, what the main talking points are, when it goes live, what channels we're using, what success looks like. This is the source of truth.

4. **Decide on your launch day strategy: simultaneous vs. phased, Day 1 vs. Week 1 focus**
   - Simultaneous launch: All channels go live at the same time (best for Tier 1). Blog, email, Product Hunt, Hacker News, Twitter, community posts, paid media (if applicable), press outreach. This requires tight coordination and a clear launch time.
   - Phased launch: Blog goes live Day 1, email goes out Day 2, paid media starts Day 3 (if you want to control pacing or need time to see organic momentum first). Less coordination required but spreads out initial impact.
   - Focus on Day 1: Don't measure success by signups on Day 1. Measure by engagement (clicks, comments, shares). Day 1 is awareness, not conversion.
   - Focus on Day 7 and Day 30: Your real metric is activation at day 7 (did people actually use the feature/product?) and day 30 (are they still using it?). That's product-market fit, not Day 1 noise.
   - Decide: simultaneous or phased? Will you launch Day 1 at 9 AM or spread it out over a week? Document and communicate one week in advance.

5. **Seed early adopters who will amplify your launch (critical for visibility)**
   - Identify 10-20 influential voices in your space: angel investors, other founders, journalists, community leaders, power users in your space
   - Send them exclusive early access 2-3 days before launch with a personal note: "We're launching [feature] Thursday. We'd love your feedback and would be grateful if you could share this with your audience."
   - Don't ask for amplification directly ("please tweet about this"). Let them choose to share if they love it. The shares that come from genuine excitement are 100x more credible than paid placements.
   - Measure: How many of your seeded early adopters shared? What tone did they use? Their framing becomes social proof for your launch.
   - Product Hunt + Hacker News: If launching a product (Tier 1), designate a founder or early team member as the "maker" for Product Hunt and the "author" for Hacker News. These communities value authenticity and direct engagement. Someone should be actively in comments answering questions during the launch day.

6. **Prepare your messaging for three different audiences: investors, customers, press**
   - Investor narrative: Focus on market size, competitive advantage, and growth potential. "This feature addresses a $500M market and we're the only product solving it this way."
   - Customer narrative: Focus on transformation and outcomes. "This feature saves teams 5+ hours per week and gives them visibility they never had before."
   - Press narrative: Focus on trend and insight. "We're seeing more teams need X. Here's why, here's what we built to address it, and here's what this means for the industry."
   - These are three different stories. Your CEO might lead the investor narrative, your head of product the customer narrative, and your communications lead the press narrative. But they should all point to the same underlying truth about what you've built and why it matters.

7. **Coordinate all channels simultaneously on launch day and measure real-time momentum**
   - 24 hours before: Final check—all copy is written, all emails are scheduled, all social posts are queued, all team members know their role
   - 2 hours before: Send Slack message: "Launch in 2 hours. All systems ready?"
   - Launch time (all at once): Blog post goes live, email sends to all lists, social posts go out, Product Hunt/Hacker News go live, press outreach begins
   - During launch day (next 4-6 hours): Monitor comments on all channels. Product team and marketing should be active answering questions, addressing objections, and capturing feedback for post-launch iteration
   - Measurement: First 6 hours—engagement rate (clicks, comments, shares), reach (unique visitors to blog, email opens, social impressions). NOT conversions yet. That comes later.
   - Evening: Team retrospective—what worked? What broke? What will we do differently next time?

8. **Plan post-launch phases: Week 1-4 optimization and decision point (Tier 1)**
   - Week 1 (momentum): Email sequences to people who signed up but didn't activate. Create educational content, guides, and use cases. Product team should be monitoring for bugs and quick wins (simple tweaks that unlock adoption).
   - Week 2 (activation): Measure activation rate at day 7. Did 30%+ of people who signed up actually use the feature? If not, where are they dropping off? Quick customer interviews to understand barriers.
   - Week 3 (retention): Measure week 2 retention (are day 7 users still active on day 14?). If retention is weak, product has a problem. If activation is strong but retention is weak, messaging/expectations were wrong.
   - Week 4 (decision): At day 30, you have real data: activation rate, retention rate, user feedback. Now you decide: is this working? Does it need iteration? Should we double down or pivot?
   - For Tier 2 and 3 launches, condense this: Week 1 measurement and quick iteration, then move on.
   - Success metric by tier:
     - Tier 1: 25%+ activation at day 7, 70%+ day 7 to day 30 retention
     - Tier 2: 15%+ activation at day 7, 50%+ retention
     - Tier 3: No specific target; monitor if it drives any adoption and iterate if needed

## Edge Cases

- **Launching on Friday is almost always a mistake**: Things break on Friday and your team can't fix them until Monday. Launch on Tuesday or Wednesday instead.

- **Tier creep**: Sales will want every launch to be Tier 1. Push back. Be honest: "Is this transformative or useful?" If useful, it's Tier 2. Apply Tier 1 to 2-3 launches per year maximum.

- **No Product Hunt for B2B**: If your customer is a CFO or VP People, Product Hunt won't move the needle. Skip it and invest in industry-specific communities (HR tech forums, LinkedIn groups, Slack communities) instead.

- **Simultaneous global launches are hard**: If you have customers in APAC, EU, and US, pick one time zone and go with it. Some regions will get the announcement while sleeping. That's okay—stagger your follow-up emails instead.

- **Post-launch measurement confusion**: Don't measure Day 1 signups as launch success. That's noise. Day 7 activation and Day 30 retention are signal. Set expectations with leadership before launch day or they'll panic on Day 1 when signups look mediocre.

- **Over-seeding kills buzz**: If you give exclusive access to 50 "influencers," none of them feel special and the launch day surprise is gone. Be selective: 10-20 genuine early adopters, not everyone with a Twitter account.
